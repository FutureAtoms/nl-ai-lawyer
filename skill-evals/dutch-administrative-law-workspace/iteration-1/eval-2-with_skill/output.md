# With-Skill Response: Dutch Administrative Law - Test 2

**Prompt:** "A government agency took 4 months to decide on our subsidy application when the legal deadline was 8 weeks. Can we do anything about this delay?"

---

## Administrative Law Analysis

**Decision**: Delayed decision on a subsidy application (aanvraag subsidieverlening)
**Administrative Body**: Government agency (bestuursorgaan) -- specific agency not identified
**Legal Basis**: Algemene wet bestuursrecht (Awb), Chapter 4 (Subsidies); specific subsidy regulation
**Issue**: The administrative body has exceeded the statutory decision period (beslistermijn) by approximately 8 weeks

## Standing Analysis

As the applicant for the subsidy, you are an **belanghebbende** (interested party) under Art. 1:2 Awb. You have a direct interest in receiving a timely decision on your application.

## Applicable Legal Framework

- **Art. 4:13 Awb** - Decision period: the administrative body must decide within the statutory period, or if no period is specified, within a reasonable period (not exceeding 8 weeks)
- **Art. 4:14 Awb** - If the body cannot decide within the period, it must notify the applicant before the deadline expires
- **Art. 4:15 Awb** - Suspension (opschorting) of the decision period (e.g., if additional information is requested from the applicant)
- **Art. 4:17 Awb** - Dwangsom bij niet-tijdig beslissen (penalty for failure to decide in time)
- **Art. 6:2 sub b Awb** - Failure to decide in time is equated with a decision (fictieve weigering) for purposes of legal protection
- **Art. 6:12 Awb** - Right to file beroep (appeal) against failure to decide in time

## Analysis

### Was the Decision Period Actually Exceeded?

Before taking action, verify the following:

1. **What is the statutory decision period?** You mention 8 weeks. Check the specific subsidy regulation (subsidieverordening or subsidieregeling) to confirm the applicable deadline. If the specific regulation does not set a period, the default under Art. 4:13 Awb is 8 weeks.

2. **Was the period suspended (opgeschort)?** Under Art. 4:15 Awb, the decision period may be suspended if:
   - The body requested additional information from you (and the period is suspended until you provide it)
   - You agreed to an extension
   - The delay is caused by force majeure
   - Check whether you received any notification of suspension or extension

3. **Was the period extended?** The specific regulation may allow the body to extend the period (verdaging). Check whether you received a written notice of extension.

If none of these exceptions apply and the body has indeed taken 4 months (approximately 16 weeks) to decide on an 8-week deadline, the decision period has been exceeded by approximately 8 weeks.

### Your Options

#### Option 1: Ingebrekestelling and Dwangsom (Art. 4:17 Awb) -- RECOMMENDED

This is the most direct and effective remedy:

**Step 1: Send an Ingebrekestelling (Formal Notice)**
- Send a written **ingebrekestelling** to the administrative body
- State that the statutory decision period has expired and demand that a decision be made
- The body then has **2 weeks** (14 days) to make a decision (Art. 4:17 lid 3 Awb)

**Step 2: Automatic Dwangsom (Penalty)**
If the body still has not decided within 2 weeks after receiving the ingebrekestelling:
- An automatic **dwangsom** (penalty payment) starts running:
  - **EUR 23 per day** for the first 14 days
  - **EUR 35 per day** for the next 14 days
  - **EUR 45 per day** for the remaining period
  - **Maximum total**: EUR 1,442 (Art. 4:17 lid 2 Awb)
- The dwangsom is owed to you (the applicant) and must be paid by the administrative body
- The body must determine the dwangsom by a separate decision (beschikking) within 2 weeks after the last day on which the dwangsom accrues

**Step 3: Collect the Dwangsom**
- If the body does not voluntarily pay the dwangsom or issue a dwangsom decision, you can file bezwaar and subsequently beroep to enforce payment

#### Option 2: Beroep Niet-tijdig Beslissen (Appeal for Failure to Decide - Art. 6:2 sub b / 6:12 Awb)

You can file an appeal directly with the administrative court (bestuursrechter at the Rechtbank):

- Under Art. 6:2 sub b Awb, the failure to make a timely decision is equated with a decision, which you can appeal against
- **Prerequisite**: You must first have sent an ingebrekestelling and the 2-week period must have expired without a decision (Art. 6:12 lid 2 Awb)
- The court can:
  - Order the body to decide within a specified period
  - Impose a judicial dwangsom (which can be higher than the statutory dwangsom)
  - Potentially decide the matter itself in certain circumstances

**No bezwaar required**: For failure to decide in time, you can go **directly to beroep** at the Rechtbank -- the bezwaar phase is skipped (Art. 7:1 lid 1 sub f Awb).

**No court fee**: As of recent amendments, no griffierecht (court fee) is required for beroep against failure to decide in time.

#### Option 3: Klacht bij de Nationale Ombudsman or Complaint

In addition to the legal remedies above, you can:
- File a **klacht** (complaint) with the administrative body about the delay
- If the complaint is not satisfactorily resolved, file a complaint with the **Nationale Ombudsman**
- The Ombudsman can investigate whether the delay constitutes "behoorlijk bestuur" (proper governance)
- This is not a legal remedy but can exert pressure and result in recommendations

### What If the Decision Is Still Pending?

If the decision has still not been made (which appears to be the case at 4 months):

1. **Send the ingebrekestelling immediately** -- this starts the clock on the 2-week period and triggers the dwangsom
2. **Wait 2 weeks** for the body to decide
3. If no decision: the dwangsom accrues automatically AND you can file beroep

### What If the Decision Has Now Been Made (Late)?

If the body has already issued a decision (even though it was late):
- The dwangsom still applies for the period between the expiry of the decision deadline + 2 weeks after ingebrekestelling and the date the decision was actually made
- However, you can only claim the dwangsom if you sent an ingebrekestelling **before** the decision was made
- If you did not send an ingebrekestelling before the decision was made, you cannot retroactively claim the dwangsom

## Practical Steps

1. **Send an ingebrekestelling TODAY** -- by registered mail (aangetekende brief) to ensure proof of delivery. Include:
   - Reference to your subsidy application (kenmerk/date)
   - Statement that the statutory decision period (8 weeks) has expired
   - Demand for a decision within 2 weeks
   - Reference to Art. 4:17 Awb (dwangsom)
2. **Keep a copy** with proof of sending and delivery
3. **Mark your calendar**: 2 weeks from the date of receipt by the body
4. **If no decision after 2 weeks**: The dwangsom starts running. You may also file beroep at the Rechtbank.
5. **Contact the body**: In parallel, consider calling or emailing the responsible official (behandelaar) to inquire about the status and push for a faster decision. Sometimes administrative delays are due to capacity issues and a reminder can accelerate the process.

## Important Notes

- The dwangsom amounts are modest (max EUR 1,442), so the financial incentive is limited. However, the beroep procedure can result in a court-imposed deadline with potentially higher penalties.
- The delay in itself does not affect the **substantive outcome** of your subsidy application -- the body must still decide on the merits.
- If the body eventually rejects your subsidy application, you can file bezwaar against that rejection within 6 weeks, regardless of the delay issue.
- If you suffer **damages** due to the delay (e.g., missed project deadlines, lost matching funding), you may have a claim for government liability (overheidsaansprakelijkheid) under Art. 6:162 BW or Art. 8:88 Awb. This requires proving causation and actual damage.

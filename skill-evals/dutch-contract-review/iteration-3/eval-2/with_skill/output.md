### 1. Indemnification Clause — Unlimited Indemnity for Marketing Activities

**Clause text**: *"Distributor shall indemnify Supplier against all claims, damages, and costs arising from Distributor's marketing activities, without any limitation."*

- **Risk Level**: RED
- **Summary**: The Distributor provides a blanket, unlimited indemnity to the Supplier for all claims, damages, and costs arising from the Distributor's marketing activities, with expressly no limitation.
- **Legal Basis**: Art. 6:74 BW (attributable failure), Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW (derogating reasonableness and fairness), Art. 6:101 BW (contributory negligence / schadebeperkingsplicht), Art. 6:96 BW (scope of compensable damages), Art. 6:94 BW (penalty/indemnity moderation)
- **Issues**:

  **a) Unlimited liability is commercially unacceptable and potentially unenforceable**
  Under Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW, a contractual clause does not apply insofar as its application would be unacceptable according to standards of reasonableness and fairness (redelijkheid en billijkheid). The Hoge Raad has consistently held that this provision operates as a mandatory backstop even in sophisticated B2B contracts (confirmed in HR 5 April 2013, ECLI:NL:HR:2013:BY8101, Lundiform/Mexx). An unlimited indemnity that could expose the Distributor to astronomical, unforeseeable third-party claims would be a strong candidate for partial or full non-application under Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW — but litigation to establish this is expensive and uncertain.

  **b) Scope is overbroad — "all claims arising from marketing activities"**
  "Marketing activities" is undefined. This could encompass: advertising content claims under the Nederlandse Reclame Code (NRC), product liability claims where the Distributor acted as a reseller, competition law infringement claims (including cartel investigations by the Autoriteit Consument & Markt / ACM), consumer law violations, and IP infringement arising from use of Supplier's trademarks in advertising. The scope is effectively unlimited in subject matter as well as amount.

  **c) No duty to mitigate or notification requirements**
  A standard indemnity should include: (i) notification obligations so the indemnifying party can control its own defense; (ii) a duty on the indemnified party to mitigate losses (Art. 6:101 BW schadebeperkingsplicht); (iii) control over settlement decisions. None of these appear present, exposing the Distributor to indemnifying settlements it had no power to prevent or influence.

  **d) Asymmetric — no corresponding Supplier indemnity**
  The excerpt shows only Distributor indemnifying Supplier, with no reciprocal obligation. In a standard distribution agreement under Dutch market practice, the Supplier typically indemnifies the Distributor for: IP infringement claims related to the products, product liability claims arising from manufacturing defects, and claims resulting from Supplier's own conduct or representations.

  **e) Comparison with NL-contract-playbook standard position**
  Per the Dutch contract playbook: a blanket indemnity with no limitation is a RED flag. Indemnities should be specific, subject to a liability cap, include procedural requirements, and be reciprocal for each party's respective risk areas.

### 3. Non-Compete Clause — 5 Years Post-Termination, Entire EU

**Clause text**: *"Non-compete: Distributor shall not sell competing products for 5 years after termination in the entire EU."*

- **Risk Level**: RED
- **Summary**: The Distributor is prohibited from selling competing products for 5 years post-termination across the entire European Union.
- **Legal Basis**: Art. 6 Mededingingswet (Mw) — Dutch competition law; Art. 101 TFEU (Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union); EU Vertical Block Exemption Regulation (VBER, Commission Regulation (EU) 2022/720, in force from 1 June 2022); Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW (reasonableness and fairness); Art. 7:443 BW (agency non-compete maximum 2 years, by analogy)
- **Issues**:

  **a) Duration: 5 years is disproportionate and likely unenforceable**
  Under Dutch contract law, non-compete clauses in commercial contracts are permissible but must be proportionate in duration, scope, and geography. The Dutch contract playbook and market practice treat 1-2 years post-termination as GREEN, more than 2 years as YELLOW, and unlimited duration or excessive years as RED.

  Five years post-termination is plainly excessive for a distribution agreement. Comparison: under Art. 7:443 BW (agency agreements), a non-compete may not exceed 2 years — while this article applies specifically to agentuurovereenkomsten (agency agreements), courts frequently apply its reasoning by analogy to distribution agreements. Under EU competition law (VBER 2022), post-term non-compete obligations are only block-exempt for up to **1 year** post-termination (Art. 5(1)(b) VBER 2022). Any duration beyond 1 year requires individual assessment under Art. 101 TFEU and is presumptively restrictive.

  **b) Geographic scope: Entire EU — far exceeds proportionality**
  An EU-wide non-compete for a distribution agreement would be extraordinary. The geographic scope of a post-termination non-compete should match the actual territory where the distributor operated under the agreement. An EU-wide restriction prevents the Distributor from operating in markets it may never have served under the agreement, which is manifestly disproportionate.

  **c) Competition law violation — Art. 6 Mw and Art. 101 TFEU**
  Under Art. 6 Mededingingswet (Dutch cartel prohibition, implementing Art. 101 TFEU), agreements between undertakings that restrict competition are prohibited and void. A 5-year EU-wide post-termination non-compete in a distribution agreement with market share above the de minimis thresholds (typically 15% for vertical agreements between non-competitors) would not benefit from the VBER safe harbor and would fall within the prohibition of Art. 6 Mw / Art. 101 TFEU. The consequence is that the non-compete clause would be **null and void** (nietig) — not merely voidable — under Art. 6 lid 2 Mw / Art. 101(2) TFEU.

  The ACM (Autoriteit Consument & Markt) is the Dutch competition authority responsible for enforcing Art. 6 Mw and, together with the European Commission, Art. 101 TFEU. Sanctions include fines of up to 10% of annual global turnover and private damages claims by third parties harmed by the anticompetitive restriction.

  **d) Enforceability under Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW**
  Even if the clause is not caught by competition law (e.g., if market shares are below the de minimis thresholds), a Dutch court applying the reasonableness and fairness standard of Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW would very likely find that a 5-year EU-wide non-compete is unacceptable in the circumstances and refuse to apply it in full. Courts may also exercise their power under Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW to moderate the clause to a proportionate scope.

  **e) Distinction from agency agreements**
  If this agreement is structured as an agentuurovereenkomst (commercial agent) rather than a true distributorship, Art. 7:443 BW would directly apply and cap the non-compete at 2 years maximum. Even as a distribution agreement (where the distributor buys and resells on its own account), the same reasoning on proportionality applies.

- **Recommendation**:
  - Reject the clause in its current form entirely.
  - Counter-propose: maximum 1-year post-termination non-compete, limited to the specific territory where the Distributor was active under the agreement, limited to products directly competing with those distributed under the agreement.
  - Document market share data to assess VBER safe harbor applicability.
  - Engage competition law counsel (advocaat with mededingingsrecht specialization) to assess whether the original clause creates any current exposure, and to draft a VBER-compliant replacement.

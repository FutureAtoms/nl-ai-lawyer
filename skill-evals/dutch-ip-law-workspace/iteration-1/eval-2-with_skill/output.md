## Analysis

### 1. Trademark Infringement Under the BVIE

As the holder of a Benelux trademark registration, you have strong legal grounds to act against the competitor under **Art. 2.20 BVIE**. The following infringement grounds should be assessed:

#### Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub a BVIE -- Double Identity
If the competitor uses an **identical** sign for **identical** goods or services, this constitutes infringement per se. This is the strongest ground but requires strict identity of both the sign and the goods/services.

#### Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub b BVIE -- Likelihood of Confusion (Verwarringsgevaar)
This is likely the most relevant ground in your case. It applies when the competitor uses an identical or **similar** sign for identical or **similar** goods or services, and there is a likelihood of confusion on the part of the public. The assessment considers:
- The degree of similarity between the signs (visual, phonetic, conceptual);
- The degree of similarity between the goods/services;
- The distinctive character of the earlier trademark (inherent or acquired through use);
- The relevant public and their level of attention;
- Overall global assessment (totaalindruk) -- per CJEU case law (Canon, Lloyd, Sabel/Puma).

Given that the competitor uses a "very similar" trade name and logo, there are strong grounds for establishing likelihood of confusion under Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub b BVIE.

#### Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub c BVIE -- Reputation (Bekendheid)
If your Benelux trademark has a **reputation** (bekendheid) in the Benelux, you may invoke this broader ground even for dissimilar goods/services. You would need to demonstrate that the competitor's use, without due cause, takes unfair advantage of or is detrimental to the distinctive character or repute of your mark (dilution / free-riding / tarnishment).

### 2. Trade Name Protection Under the Handelsnaamwet

#### Art. 5 Handelsnaamwet
If you were using your trade name in commerce **before** the competitor began using the similar name, Art. 5 Hw prohibits the competitor from using a trade name that is likely to cause confusion with your earlier trade name. Relevant factors include:
- Similarity of the trade names;
- Geographic area of activity;
- Nature of the businesses (same sector = higher risk of confusion).

#### Art. 5a Handelsnaamwet
This provision specifically bridges trademark and trade name protection: it is prohibited to use a trade name that contains a **registered trademark** belonging to another party (or a sign confusingly similar to it) if this would create a likelihood of confusion. Since you hold a Benelux trademark registration, this provision is directly applicable and provides a strong ground.

**Important note**: The Handelsnaamwet does not require registration of the trade name. Trade name rights arise from actual use in commerce. All Dutch courts (any Rechtbank) have jurisdiction for trade name disputes.

### 3. Copyright on the Logo (Auteurswet)

If your logo qualifies as an original work (oorspronkelijk werk) with a personal stamp of the author (persoonlijk stempel van de maker), it is protected by copyright under **Art. 1 Aw**. The competitor's use of a very similar logo could constitute:
- **Reproduction** (Art. 13 Aw) -- if the competitor copied or closely adapted the logo;
- **Making public** (Art. 12 Aw) -- if the competitor uses the infringing logo in commerce.

Copyright protection is automatic and requires no registration, but you must be able to demonstrate that you are the copyright holder (or assignee).

### 4. Unfair Competition / Slavish Imitation

Under Dutch tort law (**Art. 6:162 BW**), the combined use of a similar trade name AND logo may also constitute **slaafse nabootsing** (slavish imitation) or unfair competition, particularly if the competitor's overall trade dress creates unnecessary confusion with your business. This is a supplementary ground where IP-specific statutes do not fully cover the situation.

## Practical Recommendations

### Immediate Steps

1. **Send a cease-and-desist letter (sommatie)**: Through a Dutch IP lawyer (advocaat), send a formal demand to the competitor requiring them to:
   - Cease and desist from using the infringing trade name and logo;
   - Confirm compliance in writing within a short deadline (typically 7-14 days);
   - Agree to a penalty clause (boeteclausule) for future violations.
   This is the standard first step in Dutch IP enforcement and is expected by courts before initiating proceedings.

2. **Kort geding (preliminary injunction proceedings)**: If the competitor does not comply with the cease-and-desist letter, or if the matter is urgent, file a **kort geding** at the competent Rechtbank. The kort geding is the most frequently used enforcement tool in Dutch trademark and trade name disputes. It offers:
   - Fast relief (hearing typically within 2-4 weeks of filing);
   - A provisional injunction (voorlopig verbod) prohibiting further use of the infringing sign;
   - Penalty payments (dwangsom) for non-compliance;
   - The court applies a prima facie assessment of infringement.

   Kort geding is available for both BVIE trademark claims and Handelsnaamwet claims.

3. **Preserve evidence**: Document the competitor's use of the similar trade name and logo (screenshots, printouts, photographs of signage, advertising materials, website archives). Consider a bailiff's report (deurwaardersconstatering) for formal evidence preservation.

### Additional Enforcement Options

4. **BOIP opposition**: If the competitor has filed or attempts to file their own Benelux trademark application, file an **opposition (oppositie)** at BOIP within the 2-month opposition period following publication of their application (Art. 2.14 BVIE).

5. **Main proceedings (bodemprocedure)**: After or instead of kort geding, initiate main proceedings for:
   - A definitive injunction;
   - Damages (schadevergoeding) or account of profits (winstafdracht);
   - Recall and destruction of infringing materials;
   - Publication of the judgment (at the competitor's expense);
   - Full reasonable legal costs (based on the Indicatietarieven in IE-zaken).

6. **Customs measures**: If the competitor imports infringing goods, consider filing an Application for Action (AFA) with Dutch customs under EU Regulation 608/2013 to detain counterfeit goods at the border.

7. **Online enforcement**: If the competitor operates online, submit takedown notices to hosting providers, domain registrars, and platforms (citing your trademark registration).

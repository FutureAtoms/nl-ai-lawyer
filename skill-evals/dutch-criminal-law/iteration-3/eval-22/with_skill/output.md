## The Core Question: Can This Lead to Criminal Prosecution?

Yes - environmental violations in the Netherlands can and do lead to criminal prosecution, especially in the context of waste disposal. The legal framework is comprehensive and prosecution of companies (including their directors as feitelijk leidinggevers) is actively pursued by the specialized Functioneel Parket and the various environmental inspectorates. The severity of the prosecution risk depends on several factors analyzed below.

## The WED Framework: Environmental Offenses

The **Wet op de Economische Delicten (WED)** is the central mechanism for criminalizing environmental violations. The WED itself does not define environmental offenses; instead, it designates violations of specific environmental laws (the Wet milieubeheer, the Omgevingswet, and related regulations) as economic offenses, and provides an enhanced sentencing framework.

### Classification of Violations

| WED Category | Nature | When Criminal |
|-------------|--------|---------------|
| Art. 1 sub 1 WED | Most serious | Always misdrijf (felony) |
| Art. 1 sub 2 WED | Intermediate | Misdrijf if intentional; overtreding (misdemeanor) if negligent |
| Art. 1 sub 3 WED | Less serious | Misdrijf if intentional; overtreding if negligent |

Environmental violations involving **waste disposal** - particularly unauthorized disposal, illegal dumping, or disposal without required permits - typically fall under Art. 1 sub 1 or sub 2 WED, making them potentially the most serious category of economic offense.

## Types of Environmental/Waste Offenses at Issue

### 1. Unauthorized Waste Disposal (Illegale Afvalverwijdering)

Disposing of waste without the required authorization or in violation of permit conditions is a criminal offense under Dutch law (via the WED, referencing the Wet milieubeheer / Omgevingswet provisions on waste):
- **Illegal dumping** (storten van afvalstoffen) without authorization
- **Operating without the required omgevingsvergunning** for waste processing activities
- **Mixing prohibited waste streams** (e.g., hazardous waste with non-hazardous waste)
- **Violating permit conditions** (exceeding quantities, prohibited substances, incorrect disposal methods)

The HR confirmed in HR 23 december 2003 (Middelburg) that operating without or in violation of an environmental permit constitutes an economic delict under the WED even where actual environmental harm is minimal.

### 2. Zorgplicht Violation (Duty of Care)

Under Art. 1.11 Omgevingswet (and previously Art. 10.1 Wm), every person or entity engaged in activities with potential environmental impact is subject to a general **duty of care** (zorgplicht). This zorgplicht requires:
- Assessing the environmental impact of activities
- Taking technically and economically feasible preventive measures
- Maintaining transparent operational procedures
- Reporting environmental incidents to competent authorities

The HR ruled in HR 21 april 2009 (Chemie-Pack) that the general zorgplicht can serve as an **independent basis for criminal prosecution**, even without a specific permit condition being violated. This is a significant expansion of criminal liability: a company can be criminally liable even if it has all required permits, if it breaches the general duty of care.

### 3. Art. 173a/173b Sr - Endangerment Offenses

If the waste disposal violated regulations causes actual harm or creates a **substantial danger to human health or the environment**, the more serious offenses under the Wetboek van Strafrecht may apply:
- **Art. 173a Sr**: Pollution causing death, serious bodily harm, or irreversible environmental damage - **maximum 6 years imprisonment** and/or EUR 900,000 fine (5th category)
- **Art. 173b Sr**: Creating substantial danger to human health or the environment by releasing substances into water, air, or soil - **maximum 3 years imprisonment**

These offenses do not require that harm actually occurred - endangerment (gevaar) alone is sufficient.

## Corporate Criminal Liability (Art. 51 Sr)

### The Company as Perpetrator

Under **Art. 51 Sr**, criminal environmental offenses can be committed by legal entities (companies, BVs, NVs, etc.). Applying the **Drijfmest criteria** (HR 21 oktober 2003), the violation of waste disposal regulations is attributed to the company if:

1. The conduct occurred **within the sphere of the company's business activities** (sfeer van de rechtspersoon) - waste disposal from a factory is inherently a business activity.
2. The company **benefited** from the violation (e.g., saving costs by using cheaper but illegal disposal methods).
3. The company had **control (beschikkingsmacht)** over whether the violation occurred - the company decides how to handle its waste.
4. The company **accepted** the violation or similar conduct - this includes failing to implement adequate environmental compliance systems, ignoring warnings (including the internal report by the employee), or tolerating non-compliant practices.

In the factory waste disposal scenario, all four Drijfmest factors are potentially present, meaning **the company itself faces significant corporate criminal liability**.

### Personal Liability of Directors and Managers

Under **Art. 51 lid 2 Sr**, the company's directors, managers, and other officers who had de facto control (feitelijk leidinggeven) over the prohibited conduct may be personally prosecuted:

- **Slavenburg II criteria** (HR 16 december 1986): A person is a feitelijk leidinggever if they (a) were aware of the offending conduct (or had reason to be aware), (b) were in a position to prevent or stop it, and (c) consciously accepted (bewust aanvaarden) the substantial chance it would occur.
- In the waste context, a factory manager or environmental compliance officer who knew or should have known about the violations and failed to stop them may face personal criminal liability.
- Even a board member who passively failed to implement adequate environmental oversight systems may qualify (HR 1 juni 2021, ECLI:NL:HR:2021:818).

## Penalties and Sanctions

### Criminal Penalties (WED/Sr)

**For economic misdrijven (felonies - intentional violations)**:

| Category | Maximum Imprisonment | Maximum Fine |
|----------|---------------------|-------------|
| Art. 1 sub 1 WED | 6 years | EUR 90,000 (5th category) or **10% annual turnover** (whichever is higher) |
| Art. 1 sub 2 WED | 6 years | EUR 90,000 (5th category) or 10% annual turnover |
| Art. 173a Sr (serious pollution) | 6 years | EUR 900,000 (5th category) |

**For overtredingen (misdemeanors - negligent violations)**:
- Maximum: up to 1 year detention (hechtenis) and EUR 22,500 fine (4th category)

**For natural persons (directors as feitelijk leidinggevers)**:
- Imprisonment up to 6 years (for intentional misdrijven)
- Fines up to applicable category

### Additional WED Sanctions (Art. 7 WED)

Beyond imprisonment and fines, courts can impose:
- **Stillegging van de onderneming** (closure of the business): up to 1 year
- **Verbeurdverklaring** (confiscation of objects related to the offense)
- **Ontneming wederrechtelijk verkregen voordeel** (confiscation of criminal proceeds - savings made by illegal disposal methods)
- **Ontzetting van rechten** (deprivation of rights - prohibition from running certain businesses)
- **Openbaarmaking van de uitspraak** (publication of the conviction)

### Aggravating Circumstances (Art. 1a WED)

The following circumstances can increase penalties:
- Prior convictions for similar offenses within 5 years
- Deliberate/intentional conduct (opzet)
- Severe environmental or public health damage
- Obstruction of the investigation

## Dual Enforcement: Administrative vs. Criminal (Una Via)

Dutch environmental enforcement uses **both administrative and criminal pathways**:

**Administrative enforcement** (bestuursrecht):
- Omgevingsdienst or ILT can impose administrative orders (last onder bestuursdwang or last onder dwangsom) to stop violations
- Administrative fines (bestuurlijke boetes) can be imposed for permit violations
- Permit revocation (intrekking vergunning)

**Criminal enforcement** (strafrecht):
- FIOD/Functioneel Parket prosecution under WED and Sr
- Used for serious, repeated, or intentional violations

The **una via principle** (Art. 5:44 Awb) prevents double punishment: if an administrative fine has been definitively imposed for the same violation, criminal prosecution for the same facts is generally barred, and vice versa. The enforcement authority (OM and administrative body together) must choose the route. For serious cases, criminal prosecution is typically prioritized.

## The Employee Report: Implications

The fact that an **employee has reported** potential violations internally is significant:
1. **For the company**: The internal report puts management on notice. Failure to act after receiving the report (by taking corrective measures) demonstrates that the company "knew or should have known" about the violations and made a conscious choice not to stop them. This strengthens the attribution of the offense to the company and the feitelijk leidinggeven liability of management.
2. **For the employee**: Dutch law protects whistleblowers (klokkenluiders) under the Wet bescherming klokkenluiders (2023). Retaliation against the employee for making this report is unlawful and creates additional legal risk for the company.
3. **Voluntary disclosure considerations**: If the violations are confirmed, proactive self-reporting to the competent authority (Omgevingsdienst/ILT) and immediate remediation (sanering) can significantly mitigate criminal exposure. Courts and the OM consider good faith remediation as a mitigating factor.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Conduct an immediate internal investigation**: Engage an environmental specialist and a lawyer to determine the nature and extent of the alleged violations. This must be done confidentially to protect privilege.

2. **Do not destroy records**: Preserve all environmental records, disposal logs, waste manifests, and permit documentation. Destruction of evidence constitutes additional criminal liability.

3. **Engage a specialist lawyer**: Instruct a lawyer with expertise in milieustrafrecht (environmental criminal law) and economic criminal law (WED cases). A specialist in the Functioneel Parket/omgevingsdienst system is essential.

4. **Assess permit compliance**: Verify whether the factory holds all required omgevingsvergunningen for its waste disposal activities and whether it has been operating within permit conditions.

5. **Consider voluntary disclosure and remediation**: If violations are confirmed, discuss with legal counsel whether proactive self-reporting and remediation would be strategically advantageous. Self-reporting typically receives favorable treatment in sentencing.

6. **Review environmental compliance systems**: Identify whether adequate environmental management systems (EMS, ISO 14001) are in place. If not, implementing them immediately demonstrates good faith.

7. **Protect the reporting employee**: Ensure the employee who made the internal report is protected from any form of retaliation, in compliance with the Wet bescherming klokkenluiders.

8. **Prepare for inspection**: An inspection by the Omgevingsdienst or ILT may follow the employee's report. Cooperate fully with inspectors but ensure legal counsel is present or available.

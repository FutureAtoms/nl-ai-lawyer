## Analysis

### 1. Conflict of Interest (Tegenstrijdig Belang) — Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW

#### The Post-WBTR Regime (since 1 July 2021)

The **Wet Bestuur en Toezicht Rechtspersonen (WBTR)**, effective 1 July 2021, fundamentally reformed the conflict of interest rules for Dutch legal entities. Under the current regime:

> **Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW**: "Een bestuurder neemt niet deel aan de beraadslaging en besluitvorming indien hij daarbij een direct of indirect persoonlijk belang heeft dat tegenstrijdig is aan het belang van de vennootschap en de met haar verbonden onderneming."

In plain language: a director who has a direct or indirect personal interest that conflicts with the interest of the BV may **not participate in deliberation or decision-making** on that matter.

In this scenario:
- The director whose other company is the counterparty to the EUR 500K transaction has a **direct personal interest** in the transaction
- That interest conflicts with the BV's interest (getting the best terms, independent scrutiny of the transaction)
- That director **should not have participated** in the board discussion or the vote to approve the transaction

**The critical question**: Did the conflicted director abstain from the board vote? If the conflicted director participated in deliberation and/or voted in favor, the approval procedure was tainted.

#### Consequence Under the WBTR

Under the old regime (pre-WBTR, Art. 2:256 BW old), a conflict of interest triggered a **representation prohibition** — the conflicted director could not represent the BV, making transactions with third parties potentially voidable. Under the new WBTR regime, the consequence is different:

- The WBTR imposes a **procedural obligation**: the conflicted director must abstain
- The transaction itself is **not automatically void or voidable** merely because the conflicted director voted
- However, if the transaction was approved because of the conflicted director's vote (i.e., without that vote, the majority would not have been achieved), the board resolution may be **voidable under Art. 2:15 BW** (in violation of Art. 2:8 — reasonableness and fairness — or contrary to the articles of association if the statuten contain conflict-of-interest provisions)

**Action for the minority shareholder**: Challenge the board resolution under Art. 2:15 BW for being contrary to the rules of the BV (conflict of interest procedure violated). The time limit for requesting annulment under Art. 2:15 is one year from the day on which the person requesting annulment knew of the resolution.

#### The Bruil Test (HR 29 June 2007, ECLI:NL:HR:2007:BA0033)

Under the pre-WBTR case law (Bruil), a **tegenstrijdig belang** existed when the director had a personal interest so conflicting with the company's interest that there was reasonable doubt whether the director could serve the company's interest with the required integrity and objectivity. This test informs the post-WBTR standard as well — the "tegenstrijdig belang" concept has not changed, only the procedural consequences.

A EUR 500K transaction with the director's own company almost certainly meets the Bruil threshold — the conflict is concrete, material, and not merely abstract.

### 2. Director's Duty of Care — Art. 2:9 BW

All directors — including the non-conflicted director(s) — owe the BV a duty of proper management under **Art. 2:9 BW**. This includes:

- **The conflicted director**: By participating in a vote on a transaction in which they had a conflict of interest, the director may have breached their Art. 2:9 duty. If the transaction is on non-market terms (i.e., the BV is overpaying), this constitutes improper management (onbehoorlijk bestuur).

- **The non-conflicted director(s)**: Even uninvolved directors can face Art. 2:9 liability if they approved a related-party transaction without adequate scrutiny or comparison to market terms. The obligation of proper management requires independent verification of whether the transaction is in the BV's interest and at arm's length.

**Internal liability under Art. 2:9 BW** is owed to the BV, not to shareholders directly. The BV (acting through the AVA or a specially appointed representative) may bring an internal liability claim against any director whose approval of the transaction constituted ernstig verwijt (serious reproach).

Under **RCI Financial Services (HR 5 September 2014, ECLI:NL:HR:2014:2628)**, the threshold for internal Art. 2:9 liability is serious reproach assessed in all the circumstances, including the director's access to information, the division of tasks within the board, and whether the director took action upon discovering irregularities.

### 3. Vennootschappelijk Belang (Corporate Interest) — Cancun Doctrine

Under the **Cancun** doctrine (HR 6 February 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AN8071), directors must serve the corporate interest (vennootschappelijk belang), which encompasses the sustained success of the enterprise and the interests of all stakeholders — not only those of the majority shareholder.

A EUR 500K related-party transaction approved without proper conflict-of-interest procedures calls the vennootschappelijk belang into question. Key issues:

1. Is the transaction at arm's-length (marktconforme voorwaarden)? If the director's counterparty company receives a premium over market price, this is a direct wealth transfer from the BV (and indirectly from all shareholders) to the director.
2. Was independent expert advice obtained to assess the fairness of the terms?
3. Was the AVA (general meeting of shareholders, including the minority shareholder) informed or consulted before approval?

The minority shareholder's objection has legal weight: if the transaction is not in the corporate interest, all shareholders (majority and minority) have legitimate grounds to challenge it.

### 4. Minority Protection — Enquêterecht and Ondernemingskamer

#### Art. 2:344–2:359 BW: Enquêteprocedure

The **enquêterecht** (inquiry right) is one of the most powerful tools available to minority shareholders under Dutch corporate law. It is exercised before the **Ondernemingskamer** (Enterprise Chamber) of the Amsterdam Court of Appeal.

**Standing (Art. 2:344 BW)**:
A shareholder holding at least **10%** of the issued capital may file a request for an inquiry. The minority shareholder holds 30% — well above the threshold. The minority shareholder therefore has standing to file an enquêteverzoek.

**Grounds**: The requesting party must demonstrate that there are **gegronde redenen om te twijfelen aan een juist beleid of juiste gang van zaken** — well-founded reasons to doubt whether the company's policy and course of affairs have been proper.

A EUR 500K related-party transaction approved by the board in breach of the conflict-of-interest rules (Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW), without proper arm's-length terms or independent verification, provides strong grounds for such doubt. Combined with the majority shareholder/director's concurrent role as counterparty and the minority shareholder's active objection, this scenario is a textbook candidate for an enquêteverzoek.

**Available remedies from the Ondernemingskamer (Art. 2:355 BW)**:
If mismanagement (wanbeleid) is found after the inquiry:
- Annulment of the board resolution approving the transaction
- Dismissal of directors
- Temporary appointment of an independent director
- Temporary deviation from the statuten
- In extreme cases, dissolution of the BV

**Provisional measures (Art. 2:349a BW)**:
Before the full inquiry is concluded, the Ondernemingskamer may grant urgent provisional measures:
- Suspension of the conflicted director
- Temporary appointment of an independent director or supervisory board member
- Freezing of the disputed transaction or related assets

For the minority shareholder, a key immediate step is to request provisional measures to prevent further related-party transactions while the dispute is ongoing.

#### Wanbeleid Standard (Ogem — HR 10 January 1990, ECLI:NL:HR:1990:AC1234)

Mismanagement (wanbeleid) is conduct that **no reasonable entrepreneur would have adopted given the circumstances**. A board resolution approving a EUR 500K transaction with a director's own company, without proper conflict-of-interest procedure, likely meets this standard — particularly if the transaction terms are not demonstrably at arm's length.

### 5. Potential Avenues for the Minority Shareholder

#### a) Challenge the Board Resolution (Art. 2:15 BW)

File a request with the competent court (kantonrechter or rechtbank) to annul the board resolution on the grounds that:
- It violates Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW (conflicted director participated)
- It violates Art. 2:8 BW (contrary to reasonableness and fairness)
- It violates any conflict-of-interest provision in the statuten or shareholders' agreement

Time limit: 1 year from the day the minority shareholder knew of the resolution.

#### b) Enquêteprocedure at the Ondernemingskamer

File an enquêteverzoek requesting:
1. An immediate provisional measure (onmiddellijke voorziening) — e.g., suspension of the conflicted director, independent director appointment, or suspension of the transaction
2. A full inquiry into the policy and course of affairs of the BV

This is the most powerful tool and is typically used where the internal corporate dispute has reached an impasse. The Ondernemingskamer is experienced with related-party transaction and deadlock scenarios.

#### c) Liability Claim Against the Director(s)

The BV itself (acting through the AVA, a specially appointed representative, or the curator in insolvency) can bring an internal liability claim (Art. 2:9 BW) against:
- The conflicted director for breach of their duty in approving / facilitating the related-party transaction
- Other directors who failed in their duty of independent oversight

If the transaction caused the BV damage (e.g., overpayment), the directors are liable to compensate the BV.

The minority shareholder cannot directly sue the directors under Art. 2:9 BW (that claim belongs to the BV). However, the minority shareholder can:
- Trigger the BV to bring the claim (e.g., via an AVA resolution — the minority at 30% likely cannot force this without majority support, but can threaten it)
- Bring a tort claim (Art. 6:162 BW) against the directors personally if the minority shareholder can demonstrate a direct harm (schade) caused by the directors' unlawful conduct, subject to the relativiteitseis (Art. 6:163 BW)

#### d) Exit via Uittreding (Art. 2:343 BW)

A shareholder who is prejudiced in their rights or interests by the conduct of one or more co-shareholders can request the court to order the purchase of their shares at a fair price (**uittreding / uitkoop / geschillenregeling**). Under Art. 2:343 BW, the minority shareholder may petition the court to compel the majority to buy out their shares if continued participation is "unreasonably harmful" (onredelijk bezwarend). This is an exit remedy, not a remedy that addresses the underlying transaction.

### 6. Practical Considerations

#### Arm's Length Analysis
The central factual question is whether the EUR 500K transaction was on market-consistent terms. To assess this:
- Obtain an independent expert valuation / opinion on the fair market terms for the services/goods provided
- Compare the contract terms to market benchmarks
- If the BV paid a premium, this is the measure of damage for any liability claim

#### Documentation Review
The minority shareholder (and any inquiry investigators) will examine:
- Board minutes of the meeting at which the transaction was approved — did the conflicted director abstain?
- Whether the board sought independent advice
- Whether the transaction was disclosed to the AVA before approval
- The contract itself — scope, price, duration, and any unusual terms

#### Wwft and Anti-Corruption Considerations
The notaris and any lawyers advising the BV have Wwft (anti-money laundering) obligations and must report unusual transactions. While not directly applicable here, if the related-party transaction structure raises concerns about misappropriation, relevant parties should be aware of their reporting obligations.

## Recommendations

**For the BV / majority shareholders**:
1. Immediately assess whether the conflicted director properly abstained from the board vote. If not, take legal advice on whether to proactively address the conflict (e.g., seek ratification by the AVA, or acknowledge the procedural error and seek to cure it).
2. Commission an independent fair market value assessment of the transaction terms.
3. Consider placing the transaction on the agenda of the next AVA (with full disclosure) for ratification by the shareholders — note that the minority (30%) may vote against, but majority approval with full transparency strengthens the BV's legal position.
4. Review and update the statuten and/or shareholder agreement to include explicit related-party transaction approval procedures for the future.

**For the minority shareholder (30%)**:
1. Document objections formally in writing to the board and to the BV.
2. Consult a corporate lawyer immediately regarding the viability of an Art. 2:15 BW challenge and/or an enquêteverzoek before the Ondernemingskamer.
3. Request all board minutes and documents related to the transaction under any information rights in the statuten or SHA.
4. Assess whether the transaction terms were at arm's length and, if not, calculate the damage to the BV (as the basis for a liability claim).
5. Consider requesting provisional measures from the Ondernemingskamer if there is risk of further related-party transactions or asset dissipation.

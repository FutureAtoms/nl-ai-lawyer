## Analysis

### 1. Conflict of Interest — WBTR Regime (Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW)

**The most immediately critical legal issue is whether the board resolution approving the EUR 500K transaction was validly adopted given the director's conflict of interest.**

**Current Law (post-WBTR, effective 1 July 2021)**:

Under Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW:
*"Een bestuurder neemt niet deel aan de beraadslaging en besluitvorming indien hij daarbij een direct of indirect persoonlijk belang heeft dat strijdig is met het belang van de vennootschap en de met haar verbonden onderneming."*

Translation: A director may not participate in deliberation and decision-making on a matter in which the director has a direct or indirect personal interest that conflicts with the interest of the company and its enterprise.

**Key elements**:

1. **Is there a conflict of interest?** A director whose "other company" is the counterparty in a EUR 500K transaction has an obvious and direct personal interest in the transaction. Under the materiality test established by HR 29 June 2007, ECLI:NL:HR:2007:BA0033 (Bruil), a conflict of interest exists when the director's personal interest is so conflicting with the company's interest that there is a reasonable doubt whether the director can serve the company's interest with the required integrity and objectivity. A director transacting with their own other company for EUR 500K clearly meets this threshold.

2. **What is the consequence under the WBTR?** Under the new regime, the conflicted director must **abstain from deliberation and voting** (procedurele plicht). The transaction itself is NOT automatically void merely because the conflicted director participated. The WBTR deliberately changed this from the old Art. 2:256 BW regime (which created a representation prohibition that made transactions automatically voidable).

3. **But did the director actually abstain?** The facts state that the "BV board approved" the transaction. If the conflicted director did not abstain from the vote:
   - The resolution may be challenged as taken in violation of Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW
   - Under Art. 2:15 lid 1 sub b BW, the resolution is **vernietigbaar** (voidable) at the request of any person entitled to challenge it, as it violates a statutory rule for the benefit of a specific person (the non-conflicted directors/shareholders)
   - Under Art. 2:14 BW, if the resolution violates a provision for the benefit of all shareholders, it may be nietig (null and void)
   - Under Art. 2:8 BW (redelijkheid en billijkheid), any rule applicable within the corporate relationship does not apply insofar as this would be unacceptable under standards of reasonableness and fairness

4. **Board composition**: If the BV has only one director (who is conflicted) or if all directors are conflicted, and the statuten do not address this, the AVA (general meeting of shareholders) must make the decision in the company's interest (Art. 2:239 lid 6 last sentence BW: if no other director can represent the company, the supervisory board or, failing that, the AVA takes the decision). This is particularly relevant for a two-director board where both might be conflicted.

5. **Pre-WBTR vs. Post-WBTR**: Under the old regime (Art. 2:256 BW, applicable to transactions before 1 July 2021), the conflicted director was prohibited from representing the company — meaning the transaction itself could be voidable. Under the new WBTR regime, the transaction is not automatically voidable solely due to the conflict of interest; however, the director may still be personally liable and the resolution may be voidable for procedural violations.

### 2. Director Liability — Art. 2:9 BW (Internal) and Art. 6:162 BW (External)

**Internal liability (company vs. director — Art. 2:9 BW)**:

The conflicted director may be internally liable to the BV for:
- Failing to abstain from deliberation and voting as required by Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW
- Approving a transaction that is disadvantageous to the BV on terms that a non-conflicted board would not have accepted
- Breach of the duty of proper management (behoorlijke taakvervulling, Art. 2:9 BW)

The threshold for Art. 2:9 BW internal liability is **ernstig verwijt** (serious reproach). A director who voted on a transaction in which they had a manifest conflict of interest, particularly where the transaction value is EUR 500K, faces a strong claim of serious reproach.

Under HR 5 September 2014, ECLI:NL:HR:2014:2628 (RCI Financial Services), internal liability is assessed considering all circumstances, including the director's actual knowledge, available information, and steps taken to avoid the conflict.

**Practical consequence**: The minority shareholder (30%) cannot directly bring an Art. 2:9 BW claim on behalf of the BV — that right belongs to the BV itself (typically via the liquidator in insolvency or via a shareholder-initiated enquiry finding of mismanagement). However, if the Ondernemingskamer finds mismanagement (see below), the BV can subsequently pursue an Art. 2:9 BW claim against the director.

**External liability (third parties — Art. 6:162 BW)**:

If the transaction disadvantaged the company's creditors, the director may additionally face external liability under the Beklamel doctrine (ECLI:NL:HR:2010:BM0976) or the Ontvanger/Roelofsen framework (ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AZ0758), though external liability is less immediately relevant if the BV remains solvent.

### 3. Minority Shareholder Protection — Rights of the 30% Holder

The minority shareholder holding 30% has multiple legal tools to challenge the transaction and protect their interests:

#### A. Enquêterecht — Inquiry Proceedings (Art. 2:344-2:359 BW)

**This is the most powerful and most commonly used remedy for minority shareholders in Dutch BV disputes.**

**Standing**: Under Art. 2:346 BW, shareholders holding at least 10% of the issued capital (or EUR 225,000 nominal value for NV) may institute enquiry proceedings. The 30% holder clearly has standing.

**Court**: The **Ondernemingskamer** (Enterprise Chamber) of the Gerechtshof Amsterdam has exclusive jurisdiction for enquiry proceedings (Art. 2:345 BW). The Ondernemingskamer is the specialized corporate law court and its decisions carry significant authority in Dutch corporate law.

**What the minority shareholder can request**:
1. **Phase 1 — Onmiddellijke voorzieningen** (immediate provisional measures, Art. 2:349a BW): Before an inquiry is ordered, the Ondernemingskamer can impose immediate measures if there are urgent grounds. These include:
   - **Suspension of the transaction**: If the transaction has not yet been fully executed, the Ondernemingskamer can suspend it
   - **Appointment of a temporary director** (tijdelijk bestuurder) to represent the company's interest
   - **Suspension of the conflicted director** from their duties pending investigation
   - **Transfer of shares to a custodian** (tijdelijke overdracht van aandelen aan beheerder)
   - **Temporary deviation from the statuten**

2. **Phase 2 — Enquête**: If there are well-founded grounds to doubt whether there has been proper management (*gegronde redenen om te twijfelen aan juist beleid*, Art. 2:345 BW), the court appoints investigators (onderzoekers) to examine the company's management and affairs. The conflicted director's participation in a EUR 500K self-dealing transaction, without proper abstention from the vote, is a strong basis for "gegronde redenen."

3. **Phase 3 — Wanbeleid finding** (Art. 2:355 BW): If the investigators' report finds mismanagement (wanbeleid), the Ondernemingskamer may impose final measures including:
   - Annulment of the board resolution approving the transaction (Art. 2:355 sub a BW)
   - Dismissal of the director (Art. 2:355 sub b BW)
   - Appointment of permanent directors
   - Dissolution of the BV in extreme cases

**Key case law**: HR 10 January 1990, ECLI:NL:HR:1990:AC1234 (Ogem) — defined wanbeleid as conduct that no reasonable entrepreneur would adopt. A director approving a EUR 500K self-dealing transaction in violation of Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW obligations is a compelling candidate for a wanbeleid finding.

**OK 27 May 2010, ECLI:NL:GHAMS:2010:BM5928 (ASMI)**: The Ondernemingskamer demonstrated willingness to impose immediate measures in shareholder disputes, including at listed companies. For a closely-held BV dispute, the Ondernemingskamer regularly grants interim measures quickly (within days to weeks of filing).

#### B. Challenge to the Board Resolution — Art. 2:15 BW

The minority shareholder can separately request the court to declare the board resolution approving the transaction **vernietigbaar** (voidable) under Art. 2:15 BW, on grounds that:
- The resolution violates Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW (conflict of interest abstention obligation), which is a provision that protects the interests of the company
- The resolution violates the standard of redelijkheid en billijkheid (Art. 2:8 BW)
- The resolution was passed in violation of the company's statuten or any internal governance rules

**Time limit**: The right to request annulment under Art. 2:15 BW must be exercised within 1 year of the resolution (Art. 2:15 lid 5 BW). Act promptly.

**Standing**: Any person whose interests are directly affected by the resolution may request annulment. A 30% shareholder whose economic interest is harmed by a self-dealing transaction has clear standing.

### 4. Fairness of the Transaction Terms

A critical factual investigation is whether the EUR 500K transaction was concluded on **arm's length / marktconforme** (at arm's length, commercially fair) terms:
- If the transaction was at market price and on terms that any independent third party would have agreed to, the conflict of interest is procedurally problematic but the substantive harm to the company is limited
- If the transaction was at above-market prices or on unusually favorable terms for the director's other company, the substantive harm to the BV is direct and the case for mismanagement is much stronger

**Practical recommendation**: The minority shareholder should immediately request documentation supporting the valuation and terms of the transaction: comparable market prices, any fairness opinion obtained, the negotiation history, and the basis for the EUR 500K price. Refusal to provide this information in response to a reasonable shareholder information request may itself constitute a basis for enquiry proceedings.

#### Beklamel (Foundation Case)
- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:1989:AB9521
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 6 oktober 1989
- **Legal Issue**: Under what circumstances is a director personally liable toward a creditor of the company when the company fails to perform its contractual obligations?
- **Key Holding**: A director who enters into obligations on behalf of a company, knowing or reasonably ought to know that the company will not be able to fulfill those obligations and will not offer adequate recourse (verhaal), acts unlawfully (onrechtmatig) toward the creditor. This constitutes the "Beklamel norm."
- **Relevant Paragraphs**: r.o. 3.4
- **Precedential Value**: Binding -- Hoge Raad decision, foundational precedent for director liability in the Netherlands.

#### Ontvanger/Roelofsen (Elaboration of Director Liability Framework)
- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AZ0758
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 8 december 2006
- **Legal Issue**: Classification and systematization of the grounds for external director liability (externe bestuurdersaansprakelijkheid).
- **Key Holding**: The Hoge Raad distinguished two main categories of external director liability: (1) the **Beklamel norm** -- liability when the director causes the company to enter into obligations it cannot fulfill; and (2) liability when the director causes or permits the company to default on existing obligations (frustreren van verhaal). In both cases, the director must have acted with a "sufficiently serious personal reproach" (voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt).
- **Relevant Paragraphs**: r.o. 3.5
- **Precedential Value**: Binding -- Hoge Raad decision establishing the dual-track framework still in use today.

- **Key Holding**: The Hoge Raad confirmed that the Beklamel norm remains the applicable standard. ## Legal Analysis

### The Current Standard for the Beklamel Norm

The Beklamel norm, originally established in HR 6 oktober 1989 (ECLI:NL:HR:1989:AB9521), remains the leading standard for external director liability (externe bestuurdersaansprakelijkheid) in Dutch law. The norm provides that a director is personally liable toward a creditor when the director enters into obligations on behalf of the company while knowing, or while the director reasonably ought to have known, that:

1. The company **will not be able to perform** (nakomen) the obligation; and
2. The company **will not offer adequate recourse** (verhaal bieden) for the resulting damages.

This liability is grounded in **art. 6:162 BW** (onrechtmatige daad / tort), not in the corporate law provisions of Book 2 BW directly. However, the norm interacts closely with:

- **Art. 2:9 BW** -- Internal director liability (aansprakelijkheid jegens de vennootschap) for onbehoorlijke taakvervulling (improper performance of duties). This is a separate ground of liability, running from the director to the company itself, and requires an "ernstig verwijt" that no reasonably competent director would have acted as the director in question did.
- **Art. 2:248 BW** -- Director liability in bankruptcy (kennelijk onbehoorlijk bestuur), which applies specifically when the company has gone bankrupt and the curator (insolvency administrator) brings a claim.

### The "Ernstig Verwijt" Standard

Following HR 5 september 2014 (Tulip Air), the Hoge Raad has confirmed that a "voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt" (sufficiently serious personal reproach) is the overarching requirement for all forms of external director liability. The Beklamel norm is one specific factual pattern in which this threshold is met. The ernstig verwijt standard serves as a filter to prevent directors from being too easily held personally liable, which would undermine the principle of limited liability inherent in the BV/NV structure.

### Two Tracks of External Director Liability (Ontvanger/Roelofsen)

The Hoge Raad's framework from Ontvanger/Roelofsen (2006) remains authoritative. The two tracks are:

1. **Beklamel track**: The director causes the company to enter into **new** obligations while knowing the company cannot perform. The focus is on the moment of contracting.
2. **Frustration of recourse track**: The director causes or permits the company to default on **existing** obligations, for example by siphoning off assets, preferring certain creditors, or otherwise frustrating the creditor's ability to recover (frustreren van verhaal).

### Recent Developments (2021-2026)

Recent case law shows:

- The Hoge Raad has **reaffirmed** the Beklamel/Ontvanger framework without significant modification.
- Lower courts have applied the norm with increasing attention to the **specific knowledge** of the director at the time the obligation was entered into, including emails, financial records, and board minutes as evidence.
- There is a trend toward more nuanced assessment of **when exactly** the director knew or should have known of the company's financial distress, with courts requiring concrete evidence rather than hindsight-based reasoning.
- The interaction between art. 2:9 BW (internal liability) and the Beklamel norm (external liability) has been further clarified, particularly in cases where a curator brings both claims simultaneously.

## Trend / Development

The case law over the past 5 years shows a **stable and mature doctrine**. The Beklamel norm and the Ontvanger/Roelofsen framework have not been materially altered. The main development is at the evidentiary level: courts are increasingly demanding **concrete proof** that the director had actual or constructive knowledge of the company's inability to perform at the time the relevant obligation was entered into. The "ernstig verwijt" threshold continues to function as a meaningful filter, preventing routine piercing of the corporate veil while still offering creditors protection against directors who knowingly cause harm.

Another notable trend is the increased use of the Beklamel norm in **turnaround situations**, where directors continue operations hoping to restructure but ultimately fail. Courts have shown some willingness to allow directors reasonable "breathing room" to attempt a rescue, provided the director acts transparently and does not enter into disproportionate new obligations.

## Clause-by-Clause Triage

### Clause 1: Definition of Confidential Information — RED

**Finding**: Defined as "business information" only. No further qualification, scope, or exclusion.

**Why this is RED:**

1. **Vague and undefined**: "Business information" is not a recognized legal term of art under Dutch law. It could mean virtually anything relating to the disclosing party's business. Unlike specific categories (technical know-how, financial data, customer lists, strategic plans), "business information" has no natural limit.

2. **No marking or identification mechanism**: There is no requirement for the disclosing party to mark or designate information as confidential. This means that every conversation, every email, every document exchanged — and potentially even publicly available information about the disclosing party's business — could arguably fall within the definition.

3. **No clarity on what is excluded**: A competent definition would at minimum exclude:
   - Information already in the public domain
   - Information you already knew before the NDA was signed
   - Information you independently developed
   - Information you received from a third party without restriction

4. **Risk for you as recipient**: Under the Haviltex standard (HR 13 maart 1981, NJ 1981/635), a Dutch court will interpret "business information" in light of all circumstances and what parties could reasonably expect. In a dispute, the disclosing party will argue the broadest possible interpretation; you will argue the narrowest. This litigation risk alone justifies rejection of the current definition.

5. **Potential interaction with Wbb**: Even if information does not qualify as a "bedrijfsgeheim" under Art. 1 Wet bescherming bedrijfsgeheimen (because it lacks commercial value derived from secrecy or lacks reasonable protective measures), the broad NDA definition would still apply contractually. The NDA's scope is thus potentially much broader than trade secret law.

**Action: Reject. Insist on a specific, category-based definition (e.g., technical information, financial data, business plans, customer data specifically identified in writing) with all four standard exceptions.**

### Clause 2: Standard Exceptions/Carve-Outs — RED

**Finding**: No exceptions mentioned anywhere in the NDA.

**This is the most critical deficiency.** A valid, balanced NDA under Dutch law should include the following four carve-outs as absolute minimum:

**The Four Standard Exceptions:**

1. **Public domain (Openbaar domein)**: Information that is or becomes publicly available through no fault of the receiving party. Without this, you are contractually bound to keep secret information that is freely available to anyone.

2. **Prior knowledge (Voorkennis)**: Information already known to you before the NDA was signed. Without this, you cannot use knowledge you legitimately possessed before meeting this potential partner.

3. **Independent development (Onafhankelijke ontwikkeling)**: Information you independently develop without using the disclosed information. Without this, you could be liable for developing a similar product or service through entirely independent effort.

4. **Third-party disclosure (Derdenverkrijging)**: Information received from a third party who is not bound by a confidentiality obligation. Without this, you cannot act on information that reaches you through legitimate channels.

**The Missing Legally Compelled Disclosure Carve-out:**

Beyond the four standard exceptions, the absence of a legally compelled disclosure carve-out is a serious problem. Without it, the NDA purports to prevent you from:
- Complying with a court order for document production (Art. 843a Rv — exhibitieplicht)
- Responding to the Belastingdienst (tax authority) under the AWR
- Filing mandatory reports under the Wwft (anti-money laundering obligations)
- Responding to AFM or DNB supervisory requests
- Participating in litigation or regulatory proceedings

This creates an obligation that is in direct conflict with your statutory duties and is likely irreconcilable with Dutch public order (openbare orde). A clause requiring you to choose between contract compliance and statutory compliance is contrary to good faith under Art. 6:248 BW and would likely be subject to moderation or nullification — but again, only after a court proceeding.

**Action: Reject. Add all four standard carve-outs plus a legally compelled disclosure provision.**

### Clause 4: Penalty Clause — YELLOW

**Finding**: EUR 25,000 per breach. No daily accrual penalty mentioned.

**Assessment**: EUR 25,000 per breach falls at the upper end of the YELLOW range (EUR 25,000–50,000 per breach is YELLOW/approaching RED for an SME or low-risk relationship; it is market standard for mid-market agreements).

#### Penalty Exposure Analysis

| Scenario | Calculation | Total |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Single breach | EUR 25,000 × 1 | **EUR 25,000** |
| 5 distinct disclosures | EUR 25,000 × 5 | **EUR 125,000** |
| 10 distinct disclosures | EUR 25,000 × 10 | **EUR 250,000** |

**Important considerations:**

1. **What counts as "one breach"?** Under the vague "business information" definition, it is unclear what constitutes a single breach. Sharing one document once? Every individual item of "business information" disclosed? The lack of definition clarity exacerbates the penalty risk.

2. **No daily accrual**: The absence of a daily penalty is actually a positive feature — it limits the runaway liability risk seen in other NDAs. This is consistent with the NDA's short-form nature.

3. **Penalty vs. damages (Art. 6:92 BW)**: Under Dutch law, the default rule is that a penalty replaces supplementary damages (Art. 6:92 lid 2 BW). Unless the NDA explicitly states that damages are cumulative with the penalty ("onverminderd het recht op volledige schadevergoeding"), EUR 25,000 per breach is your maximum financial exposure per incident.

4. **Judicial moderation (Art. 6:94 BW)**: If EUR 25,000 per breach is grossly disproportionate to actual harm, a Dutch court may moderate the penalty. However, the threshold is high (klaarblijkelijk — "obviously unreasonable"), and moderation is not guaranteed.

5. **Ingebrekestelling**: Under Art. 6:93 BW, a notice of default (ingebrekestelling) is generally required before a penalty becomes due. The NDA does not mention whether this requirement is waived. If not waived, this provides procedural protection — you cannot be immediately fined without receiving a prior warning.

**Action**: YELLOW — negotiable. Request: (a) clarification that penalty is in lieu of (not cumulative with) damages; (b) explicit ingebrekestelling requirement before penalty accrues; (c) if possible, reduce to EUR 10,000–15,000 per breach for an exploratory partnership NDA of 2 years.

## What Is the 30% Ruling (30%-regeling)?

The 30% ruling is a **tax-free allowance** for employees recruited from abroad who possess specific expertise scarce on the Dutch labor market. It is technically a deemed reimbursement of extraterritorial costs (extraterritoriale kosten) - the additional costs incurred by living and working abroad, such as double housing, storage, travel, and cost-of-living differences.

### How It Works in Practice

- The employer may designate up to **30% of the total gross employment income** (including the allowance itself) as a **tax-free reimbursement**.
- The remaining **70% of gross salary** constitutes the taxable base for loonbelasting and inkomstenbelasting (Box 1).
- The 30% portion is not subject to payroll tax (loonheffing) or income tax.

### Calculation Example

| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Gross salary (bruto salaris) | EUR 65,867 |
| 30% tax-free allowance | EUR 19,760 |
| Taxable salary (loon voor loonbelasting) | EUR 46,107 |

The employee pays Box 1 income tax only on EUR 46,107 rather than EUR 65,867, representing a substantial tax saving.

## Eligibility Conditions

### 1. Specific Expertise (Specifieke Deskundigheid)
The employee must possess expertise that is **scarce or not available on the Dutch labor market**. Since 2012, this requirement is **presumed to be satisfied** if the salary threshold is met - no separate proof of scarcity is required for most employees. However, the HR (HR 28 april 2006, ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AU2304) confirmed that the specific expertise requirement is substantive and must be demonstrable if challenged.

### 2. Recruitment from Abroad
The employee must have been:
- **Recruited from abroad**, OR
- **Transferred within a multinational group** from a foreign location.

The employee must have been living outside the Netherlands before commencing Dutch employment. Prior Dutch employment may disqualify the applicant, subject to specific exceptions.

### 3. The 150 km Rule (150-Kilometerregel)
In the **24 months prior to the first day of Dutch employment**, the employee must have lived at a distance of **more than 150 km from the Dutch border** for at least **16 out of 24 months**. This distance is measured as the shortest straight-line distance from the employee's home address to the nearest point on the Dutch border.

As a US citizen moving from the United States to the Netherlands, this condition is easily satisfied - the US is far beyond 150 km from the Dutch border.

### 4. Salary Thresholds (Inkomensnorm) - 2024/2025

| Category | Minimum Taxable Salary (after 30% reduction) | Equivalent Minimum Gross Salary |
|----------|----------------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Standard (age 30+) | EUR 46,107 | EUR 65,867 |
| Under-30 with Master's degree | EUR 35,048 | EUR 50,069 |
| Scientific researchers at qualifying institutions | No threshold | No threshold |
| Medical specialists in training (AIOS) | No threshold | No threshold |

The taxable salary (after the 30% deduction) must meet or exceed the applicable threshold. These thresholds are indexed annually - verify current figures with the Belastingdienst.

## Duration of the 30% Ruling

### Current Rule (Effective from 1 January 2024)

The maximum duration of the 30% ruling is **60 months (5 years)** from the first day of employment in the Netherlands. This was changed from the previous maximum of 8 years (96 months) in 2019.

The 5-year period includes any prior time spent in the Netherlands in earlier employments where the ruling applied (or where time was spent working in the Netherlands).

### Step-Down in Percentage (2024 Legislative Change - Belastingplan 2024)

For rulings **granted from 1 January 2024**, the maximum tax-free percentage is no longer a flat 30% for the entire duration. Instead, a graduated step-down applies:

| Period | Maximum Tax-Free Percentage |
|--------|-----------------------------|
| First 20 months (months 1-20) | 30% |
| Months 21-40 | 20% |
| Months 41-60 | 10% |

This means that over the 5-year period, the benefit is reduced progressively, and the full 30% applies only in the first approximately 20 months.

**Important for new arrivals in 2024 onwards**: If your ruling is granted under the new rules, you will benefit from 30% for the first 20 months, then 20%, then 10%.

### Transitional Provisions (Overgangsrecht)

- Employees who had a 30% ruling granted **before 1 January 2024** may retain the terms applicable under the old rules, subject to specific transitional provisions.
- Employees who had a ruling under the **pre-2019 8-year regime** and were subject to transitional provisions from the 2019 reduction may still have specific rights.
- The applicable transitional provisions depend on the date the ruling was first granted and when the employee started in the Netherlands.

### Further Legislative Changes (2025+)

There is ongoing political discussion in the Netherlands about further limiting or restructuring the 30% ruling. Verify the current legislative position with a tax advisor, as this area is subject to frequent change.

## US Tax Obligations for US Citizens Living in the Netherlands

### The Fundamental Issue: US Worldwide Taxation

The United States is one of very few countries that taxes its citizens on **worldwide income regardless of residence**. A US citizen living and working in the Netherlands is:
1. **Subject to Dutch tax** on Dutch-source employment income (under Dutch domestic law and the Netherlands-US Tax Treaty).
2. **Subject to US tax** on the same worldwide income (under US domestic law - IRC).

This creates **double taxation risk** that must be carefully managed.

### The Netherlands-US Tax Treaty (Belastingverdrag)

The Netherlands and the United States have concluded a **bilateral tax treaty** (Belastingverdrag Nederland-VS) that provides mechanisms to avoid double taxation. Key treaty provisions relevant to an employee situation include:

- **Employment income (Article on Income from Employment)**: Employment income is generally taxable in the country where the work is performed (the Netherlands). This gives the Netherlands primary taxing rights.
- **Tax credits (Article on Relief from Double Taxation)**: The US must grant a foreign tax credit for Dutch taxes paid, reducing the US tax liability.
- **Exemption method**: For certain types of income, the treaty may provide an exemption in one country.
- **Tie-breaker rules**: If the employee is considered resident in both countries, tie-breaker provisions determine which country has primary residence-based taxing rights.

**Important**: The treaty does not eliminate US tax obligations for US citizens - it provides relief mechanisms (primarily the foreign tax credit) to reduce double taxation.

### Interaction of the 30% Ruling with US Tax

The 30% ruling creates a specific issue for US citizens:

- **Dutch side**: The 30% portion of salary is not subject to Dutch income tax (it is a tax-free reimbursement).
- **US side**: The US may **still tax the full gross salary** (including the 30% portion that is Dutch-tax-free), because from a US perspective, the entire gross amount is income. The Dutch exemption does not automatically apply for US tax purposes.
- **Effect on foreign tax credits**: Because the 30% portion generates no Dutch tax, there is **less Dutch tax available as a foreign tax credit** against the US tax liability on that portion of income.

In practice, this means the 30% ruling partially reduces the foreign tax credit available to the US employee, potentially leaving some income taxed only in the US or creating a net US tax liability despite the Dutch exemption.

### Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE - IRC Sec. 911)

The US provides the **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE)**, which allows qualifying US citizens abroad to exclude a certain amount of foreign employment income from US taxable income (for 2024: approximately USD 126,500, indexed annually).

**Critical limitation**: The FEIE **cannot be combined with the foreign tax credit for the same income**. Claiming both for the same dollars of income is not permitted. Each employee must calculate which combination of FEIE and foreign tax credit minimizes their total US tax liability - this is a complex, fact-specific analysis.

### US Compliance Obligations

A US citizen in the Netherlands must:

1. **File annual US federal income tax return (Form 1040)** - mandatory for all US citizens regardless of where they live.
2. **Report Dutch bank accounts** - FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) if aggregate foreign bank accounts exceed USD 10,000 at any point during the year.
3. **FATCA reporting** - Form 8938 if foreign financial assets exceed reporting thresholds.
4. **Dutch tax return** - Annual aangifte inkomstenbelasting with the Belastingdienst.
5. **Claim treaty benefits and foreign tax credits** - On Form 1116 (foreign tax credit) or by claiming FEIE on Form 2555.

### Social Security Implications

The **Netherlands-US Totalization Agreement** (Verdrag inzake sociale zekerheid, in force since 1990) coordinates social security obligations:
- A US citizen employed in the Netherlands is generally **insured under Dutch social security** (not US Social Security).
- A Certificate of Coverage (posted worker certificate) may exempt them from Dutch social security if they are a posted worker (typically for up to 2 years) remaining insured in the US.
- For long-term Dutch residents: Dutch social security contributions are included in the loonheffing (the combined payroll tax and social security levy). The 30% ruling does **not** reduce the base for social security contributions.

## Partial Non-Resident Taxpayer Status

Employees with a 30% ruling may elect **partial non-resident taxpayer status** (partieel buitenlands belastingplichtige) under Art. 2.6 Wet IB 2001:
- Treated as a **resident taxpayer** for Box 1 (employment income).
- Treated as a **non-resident taxpayer** for Box 2 (substantial shareholding income) and Box 3 (savings and investments).
- **Practical benefit**: Foreign bank accounts, investments, and real estate outside the Netherlands are **not taxable in Box 3** under this status.

**Important warning**: The Belastingplan 2025 proposes **abolishing** partial non-resident status for new rulings from 2025. The current legislative status must be verified. For rulings granted before the change takes effect, transitional provisions may apply.

## Application Process

1. The **employer and employee file a joint application** with the Belastingdienst/Kennis- en Expertisecentrum Buitenland (KEB) in Heerlen.
2. **Required documents**: Employment contract, proof of prior foreign residence (more than 150 km from Dutch border), passport/ID, and (for under-30 Master's threshold) diploma with Nuffic/IDW evaluation if foreign degree.
3. **Timing**: File within **4 months** of the start of employment. If filed within 4 months, the ruling applies retroactively to the first day of employment. If filed late, it applies from the first day of the month following the application.
4. **Processing time**: Typically 2-4 months.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Apply promptly**: File the 30% ruling application within 4 months of starting Dutch employment to ensure retroactive effect.
2. **Engage a US/Dutch dual-qualified tax advisor**: Given the US worldwide taxation requirement, engage a professional with expertise in both Dutch and US international tax to minimize the combined tax burden.
3. **Analyze FEIE vs. foreign tax credit**: Calculate which approach (or combination) produces the lowest US tax liability for your specific income level.
4. **Plan for ruling expiration**: After 5 years, the 30% ruling ends and the full Dutch tax rate applies. The effective Box 1 tax rate will increase significantly. Plan financially for this transition.
5. **Monitor legislative changes**: The 30% ruling framework has been changed significantly in 2024 and may change further. Keep track of updates through your tax advisor.
6. **FBAR and FATCA compliance**: Maintain strict US reporting compliance to avoid substantial US penalties.
7. **Check the step-down schedule**: Under the 2024 rules, confirm whether you will benefit from the graduated step-down (30%/20%/10%) or transitional provisions preserving an older, more favorable schedule.

## What Is the 30% Ruling?

The 30%-regeling (also called the extraterritoriale kostenregeling) is a Dutch tax facility for employees recruited from abroad with specific expertise. Under this ruling, the employer may designate up to **30%** of the total gross employment income as a tax-free reimbursement for extraterritorial costs (extraterritoriale kosten) - the extra costs of living and working in a foreign country (double housing, storage, travel, cost of living differences).

In practical terms, the employee's taxable income for Dutch payroll tax and income tax purposes is reduced to **70%** of the gross salary. The remaining 30% is paid tax-free.

### Example Calculation

- Gross salary: EUR 65,867
- 30% tax-free allowance: EUR 19,760
- Taxable salary (loon voor de loonbelasting): EUR 46,107
- Dutch income tax is levied only on EUR 46,107

This is a significant benefit, particularly for highly-paid employees facing Dutch's progressive income tax rates of up to 49.50% in 2024/2025.

## Eligibility Conditions

To qualify for the 30% ruling, **all** of the following conditions must be met:

### 1. Specific Expertise (Specifieke Deskundigheid)

The employee must possess expertise that is scarce or not available on the Dutch labor market. Since 2012, this requirement is deemed satisfied automatically if the salary threshold is met (no separate proof of specific expertise is required in standard cases). This was confirmed in HR 28 april 2006.

### 2. Recruitment from Abroad

The employee must have been:
- Recruited from outside the Netherlands, OR
- Transferred to the Netherlands within a multinational group

The employee must have been living outside the Netherlands before starting Dutch employment. Prior Dutch employment may disqualify the applicant, subject to exceptions.

### 3. The 150 km Rule (150-Kilometerregel)

During the **24 months prior to the first day of employment** in the Netherlands, the employee must have lived at a distance of **more than 150 km from the Dutch border** for at least **16 out of 24 months**. This distance is measured as the shortest (straight-line) distance from the home address to the nearest point on the Dutch border.

As a US citizen moving from the United States, you will readily satisfy this requirement, as the US is well beyond 150 km from the Dutch border.

### 4. Salary Thresholds (Inkomensnorm) - 2024/2025

The **taxable salary** (after the 30% deduction is applied) must meet the following minimum thresholds:

| Standard | EUR 46,107 | EUR 65,867 |
| Under-30 with Master's degree | EUR 35,048 | EUR 50,069 |
| Scientific researchers (qualifying institutions) | No threshold | No threshold |

Thresholds are indexed annually - verify the current year amounts.

## Duration of the 30% Ruling

### Current Maximum Duration: 60 Months (5 Years)

Since the legislative changes taking effect in recent years, the maximum duration of the 30% ruling is **60 months (five years)** from the first day of employment in the Netherlands. This applies to rulings granted under the current regime.

### Important 2024 Change: Step-Down in Percentage

For rulings **granted from 1 January 2024**, the ruling is no longer a flat 30% for the entire 5-year period. Instead, a step-down applies:

| Period | Tax-free percentage |
|--------|---------------------|
| First 20 months | 30% |
| Months 21-40 | 20% |
| Months 41-60 | 10% |

This represents a significant reduction in the value of the benefit over the later years, compared to the pre-2024 regime.

### Transitional Provisions

- Employees who had a ruling granted **before 1 January 2024** may retain the old flat 30% for the remainder of their term under transitional provisions.
- Employees under the **old 8-year regime** (rulings granted before 2019) may have different transitional treatment.
- As a new arrival, you would be subject to the 2024 step-down rules.

### What Counts Toward the 5-Year Period

The 5-year period includes **all time spent working in the Netherlands**, including prior Dutch employment with other employers. Changing Dutch employers does not reset the clock.

## US Tax Obligations: Key Considerations

### The US Worldwide Taxation Principle

The United States taxes its **citizens on worldwide income**, regardless of where they live. Moving to the Netherlands does NOT end your US tax obligation. You will remain required to:
- File US federal income tax returns (Form 1040) annually;
- Report foreign bank accounts (FBAR - FinCEN 114) if foreign accounts exceed USD 10,000;
- File Form 8938 (FATCA) for significant foreign financial assets;
- Potentially file state income tax returns depending on your domicile state.

### The Netherlands-US Tax Treaty (Belastingverdrag)

The Netherlands and the United States have concluded a comprehensive double taxation treaty (belastingverdrag / verdrag). Key relief mechanisms under the treaty include:

1. **Foreign Tax Credit (Buitenlandse Belastingkredieten)**: The US allows a credit for Dutch taxes paid on income that is also taxable in the US. This prevents double taxation in most cases;

2. **Exemption Method**: For certain categories of income, the treaty may assign exclusive taxing rights to one country;

3. **Tie-breaker Rules**: The treaty contains provisions for determining tax residency when an individual qualifies as resident in both countries simultaneously.

### Interaction Between the 30% Ruling and US Taxes

The 30% ruling creates an important complication for US taxpayers:
- The 30% that is tax-free in the Netherlands (the deemed reimbursement of extraterritorial costs) **may still be taxable in the US** as gross income, since the US does not recognize the Dutch tax-free characterization;
- The reduced Dutch taxable base also means reduced Dutch taxes paid, which reduces the available foreign tax credit in the US;
- The **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE, IRC Section 911)** may be available but cannot be combined with the foreign tax credit for the same income - you must choose one or the other;
- The interaction between the FEIE, the foreign tax credit, the 30% ruling, and the treaty is complex and requires detailed individual analysis.

### Social Security: The Totalization Agreement

The Netherlands and the US have concluded a **Totalization Agreement (Verdrag inzake sociale zekerheid)** since 1990. This agreement coordinates social security contributions to prevent double payment:
- If you are covered under Dutch social security (which will generally apply as an employee in the Netherlands), you will typically not owe US Social Security taxes on the same income;
- A Certificate of Coverage may be relevant for posted workers; for a permanent relocation, Dutch social security coverage is the default.

Note: The 30% ruling does **not** reduce the base for Dutch social security contributions - premies are calculated on the unreduced gross salary.

### Partial Non-Resident Taxpayer Status (Partieel Buitenlands Belastingplichtige)

Employees with the 30% ruling have historically been able to elect partial non-resident taxpayer status (Art. 2.6 Wet IB 2001), which means:
- **Box 1** (employment income): taxed as a resident (Dutch income tax applies);
- **Box 2** (substantial shareholding in non-Dutch companies): treated as non-resident, so foreign shareholdings may be exempt;
- **Box 3** (savings and investments): treated as non-resident, so foreign bank accounts and investments may be exempt from Dutch Box 3 tax.

**Important**: The Belastingplan 2025 proposes the abolition of partieel buitenlands belastingplichtige status for new rulings from 2025. Verify the current legislative status - this is actively changing.

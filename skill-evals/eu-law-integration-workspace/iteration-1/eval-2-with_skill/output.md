## Applicable EU Law

### EU Trade Secrets Directive (2016/943)

The EU Trade Secrets Directive was adopted on 8 June 2016 with a transposition deadline of 9 June 2018. It aims to harmonize the protection of trade secrets across the EU by establishing:

1. **A uniform definition of trade secrets** (Art. 2(1)): Information that is (a) secret (not generally known or readily accessible), (b) has commercial value because it is secret, and (c) has been subject to reasonable steps to keep it secret.

2. **Unlawful acquisition, use, and disclosure** (Arts. 3-4): Defines what constitutes lawful and unlawful conduct regarding trade secrets.

3. **Measures, procedures, and remedies** (Arts. 6-15): Sets out the civil enforcement framework, including injunctions, corrective measures, damages, and procedural safeguards.

4. **Preservation of confidentiality in legal proceedings** (Art. 9): Requires Member States to ensure that trade secrets are protected during litigation.

5. **Exceptions and limitations** (Art. 5): Carve-outs for whistleblowing, freedom of expression, employee mobility, and the public interest.

The Directive is a **minimum harmonization** directive, meaning Member States may provide **more extensive** protection for trade secrets, provided they comply with certain safeguards (Art. 1(1)).

## Interaction with Dutch Law: The Wet bescherming bedrijfsgeheimen (Wbb)

### Implementation

The Netherlands implemented Directive 2016/943 through the **Wet bescherming bedrijfsgeheimen (Wbb)**, which entered into force on **23 October 2018** (slightly after the 9 June 2018 deadline). The Wbb was introduced as a standalone act rather than being integrated into existing legislation (such as the Burgerlijk Wetboek).

### Key Provisions of the Wbb and Their Relationship to the Directive

#### Definition of Trade Secret (Art. 1 Wbb / Art. 2(1) Directive)

The Wbb faithfully transposes the Directive's three-part definition of a trade secret:
1. The information is **secret** (niet algemeen bekend bij of gemakkelijk toegankelijk voor personen die zich gewoonlijk bezighouden met dit soort informatie)
2. The information has **commercial value** because it is secret (handelswaarde heeft omdat zij geheim is)
3. The holder has taken **reasonable steps** to keep it secret (door de persoon die daar rechtmatig over beschikt onderworpen aan redelijke maatregelen om deze geheim te houden)

This is a direct transposition -- the Dutch definition mirrors the Directive's definition.

#### Unlawful Acquisition, Use, and Disclosure (Arts. 2-3 Wbb / Arts. 3-4 Directive)

The Wbb tracks the Directive closely:
- **Lawful acquisition** (Art. 2 Wbb): Independent discovery, reverse engineering (unless contractually restricted), exercise of workers' right to information and consultation
- **Unlawful acquisition** (Art. 3 Wbb): Unauthorized access to or copying of documents/materials containing the trade secret; breach of a confidentiality agreement or other duty of confidentiality

#### Remedies and Procedures (Arts. 5-8 Wbb / Arts. 6-15 Directive)

The Wbb implements the Directive's remedial framework:
- **Injunctions** (verbod op gebruik of openbaarmaking) -- Art. 5 Wbb
- **Corrective measures**: Recall and destruction of infringing goods -- Art. 6 Wbb
- **Damages** (schadevergoeding) -- Art. 8 Wbb
- **Publication of judgment** -- available under general procedural law

#### Preservation of Confidentiality in Proceedings (Art. 1019ib Rv / Art. 9 Directive)

A particularly important implementation: the Wbb introduced **Art. 1019ib Rv** (Wetboek van Burgerlijke Rechtsvordering), which provides for **procedural confidentiality** (processuele geheimhouding). This allows courts to restrict access to evidence containing trade secrets during litigation, ensuring that the very act of enforcing trade secret rights does not lead to further disclosure.

#### Exceptions (Art. 4 Wbb / Art. 5 Directive)

The Wbb transposes the Directive's exceptions:
- Freedom of expression and information (including journalism)
- Whistleblowing (revealing misconduct or illegal activity in the public interest)
- Disclosure by workers to their representatives in the exercise of their functions
- Protection of a legitimate interest recognized by EU or national law

### Relationship Between the Two: Key Points for Your Company

#### 1. The Wbb IS the Dutch Implementation of the Directive

#### 2. Minimum Harmonization: Dutch Law May Be More Protective

Since the Directive is a minimum harmonization measure, the Netherlands was permitted to go beyond the Directive's requirements. In practice, the Wbb closely follows the Directive without significant gold-plating (additions beyond what the Directive requires). However, Dutch general contract law (BW) and existing case law may provide **additional** protections:

- **Contractual NDA obligations** (governed by BW Boek 6) can cover information that does not meet the statutory trade secret definition
- **Tort law** (Art. 6:162 BW -- onrechtmatige daad) may provide remedies for conduct related to confidential information even outside the Wbb's scope
- **Employment law protections** (Art. 7:653 BW non-compete; Art. 7:611 BW good employment practices) provide additional safeguards

#### 3. Direct Effect Considerations

Since the Wbb properly implements the Directive, the question of direct effect typically does not arise. However, if a situation arises where the Wbb is ambiguous or appears to fall short of the Directive's requirements:

- **Consistent interpretation (richtlijnconforme interpretatie)**: Dutch courts must interpret the Wbb in light of the Directive's wording and purpose (Von Colson/Marleasing doctrine)
- **Vertical direct effect**: If the Wbb were found to be deficient, provisions of the Directive that are clear, precise, and unconditional could be invoked directly against the State
- **No horizontal direct effect**: The Directive cannot be invoked directly against another private company (Faccini Dori principle); however, consistent interpretation can achieve similar results in practice

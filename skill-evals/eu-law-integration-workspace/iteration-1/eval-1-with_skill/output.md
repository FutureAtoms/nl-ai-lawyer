## Direct Effect Analysis

### The Doctrine of Direct Effect (Rechtstreekse Werking)

The CJEU established in **Van Gend en Loos (C-26/62, 1963)** that EU law creates rights for individuals that national courts must protect. For a provision to have direct effect, it must be:

1. **Clear and precise** (duidelijk en nauwkeurig)
2. **Unconditional** (onvoorwaardelijk) -- not subject to further measures or conditions
3. **Leaving no margin of discretion** to Member States or EU institutions in its application

### Directives and Direct Effect

Directives are binding as to the result to be achieved but leave Member States the choice of form and methods of implementation (Art. 288 TFEU). However, the CJEU has developed important principles regarding unimplemented or incorrectly implemented directives:

**After the transposition deadline has passed**, provisions of a directive that meet the Van Gend en Loos criteria can be invoked directly by individuals -- but only **vertically** (against the State), not **horizontally** (against private parties).

### Vertical vs. Horizontal Direct Effect: The Critical Distinction

#### Vertical Direct Effect -- YES

A private party **can** invoke an unimplemented directive against the State and its emanations. This was established in:

- **Marshall v Southampton and South-West Hampshire AHA (C-152/84, 1986)**: The CJEU confirmed that a directive can be relied upon against the State when the State has failed to implement it by the deadline or has implemented it incorrectly. The rationale is that a Member State should not be allowed to benefit from its own failure to comply with EU law (estoppel argument).

- **Foster v British Gas (C-188/89, 1990)**: The CJEU gave a broad definition of "the State" for vertical direct effect purposes: it includes any body that (a) is subject to the authority or control of the State, or (b) has special powers beyond those resulting from normal rules applicable to relations between individuals, regardless of its legal form. This includes municipalities, provinces, public hospitals, state-owned enterprises, and similar entities.

In your case, since the private party is invoking the directive **against the Dutch government**, this is a classic case of vertical direct effect. Provided the specific provisions of the directive are clear, precise, and unconditional, the private party can indeed rely on them directly before the Dutch court.

#### Horizontal Direct Effect -- NO

A private party **cannot** invoke an unimplemented directive against **another private party**. This was established in:

- **Marshall (C-152/84)**: Directives are addressed to Member States, not to individuals; therefore they cannot impose obligations on private parties.

- **Faccini Dori v Recreb (C-91/92, 1994)**: The CJEU explicitly confirmed that directives cannot have horizontal direct effect, even where the provisions are clear, precise, and unconditional. The Court rejected extending Marshall to private-party relationships, holding that allowing horizontal direct effect would blur the distinction between regulations and directives under Art. 288 TFEU.

This distinction is critical: in your scenario, the claim is against the Dutch government (vertical), so direct effect is available. If the claim were against a private company, direct effect of the directive would **not** be available (though other remedies may exist -- see below).

## Dutch Constitutional Framework: Art. 93 and 94 Grondwet

The Netherlands has a **monist** legal system, providing a natural constitutional foundation for the application of EU law:

**Art. 93 Grondwet**: Provisions of treaties and decisions of international organizations that may be binding on all persons ("een ieder verbindend") have binding force after publication. Directly effective EU directive provisions qualify under this article.

**Art. 94 Grondwet**: Statutory regulations in force within the Kingdom shall **not be applied** if such application is in conflict with provisions of treaties or decisions of international organizations that are binding on all persons.

This means:
- The Dutch court can set aside conflicting Dutch legislation (including Acts of Parliament / wetten in formele zin) under Art. 94 Gw
- The Netherlands does not have a constitutional court; **all** Dutch courts exercise this power
- This constitutional framework accommodates EU law primacy naturally, without the tensions seen in some other Member States (e.g., Germany's BVerfG Solange doctrine)

## Alternative and Complementary Remedies

Even if the specific directive provisions do not meet the direct effect criteria, the private party has additional avenues:

### 1. Consistent Interpretation (Richtlijnconforme Interpretatie)

Established in **Von Colson (C-14/83)** and **Marleasing (C-106/89)**: Dutch courts are obliged to interpret national law, as far as possible, in conformity with the directive. This applies to **all** national law (not just implementing measures) and even to national law adopted before the directive. The limit is that interpretation cannot be **contra legem** (against the clear wording of national law).

### 2. State Liability -- Francovich Damages

Established in **Francovich (C-6/90 and C-9/90, 1991)**: If the private party suffers damage as a result of the State's failure to implement the directive, they may claim **state liability damages**. The conditions (further refined in **Brasserie du Pecheur/Factortame III, C-46/93 and C-48/93**) are:

1. The rule of EU law infringed must confer rights on individuals
2. The breach must be **sufficiently serious** (voldoende gekwalificeerde schending) -- failure to implement a directive after the transposition deadline is **almost always** considered sufficiently serious
3. There must be a **direct causal link** between the breach and the damage suffered

In the Netherlands, Francovich claims are brought as tort claims (onrechtmatige daad) under **Art. 6:162 BW** against the State (Staat der Nederlanden), with exclusive jurisdiction at the Rechtbank Den Haag.

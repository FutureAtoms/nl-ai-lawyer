## Overview

A former employee has exercised their **right of access (inzagerecht)** under **art. 15 AVG (GDPR)**. This is a legally enforceable right. The AVG requires you to respond substantively and within a statutory deadline. However, the right of access is not absolute: there are exceptions and limitations, particularly regarding data about and by third parties. This analysis addresses your obligations, the applicable deadline, the scope of what must be disclosed, and the legally available exceptions.

## Legal Basis: Art. 15 AVG - Right of Access (Inzagerecht)

**Art. 15 lid 1 AVG** provides that the data subject has the right to obtain from the controller:

1. **Confirmation** of whether personal data concerning them is being processed;
2. **Access to** the personal data being processed;
3. Information about:
   - The **purposes** of the processing
   - The **categories** of personal data
   - The **recipients or categories of recipients** to whom the data has been or will be disclosed
   - The **retention period** (or criteria used to determine it)
   - The existence of the rights to **rectification, erasure, restriction, and objection**
   - The right to **lodge a complaint** with the AP
   - If data was not collected from the data subject directly: information about the **source**
   - The existence of **automated decision-making** (including profiling under art. 22 AVG) and meaningful information about the logic involved

**Art. 15 lid 3 AVG** requires the controller to provide a **copy** of the personal data being processed.

The Hoge Raad (HR 24 February 2017, ECLI:NL:HR:2017:286) confirmed that the inzagerecht includes the right to receive a copy of personal data in an intelligible form.

## The Deadline: One Month (with Possibility of Extension)

### Primary Deadline

Under **art. 12 lid 3 AVG**, the controller must respond to a data subject access request **within one month (één maand)** of receiving the request.

- The one-month period starts on the date the request is received.
- If the request is received on, for example, 4 March 2026, the response is due by 4 April 2026.

### Extension

The one-month deadline can be **extended by a further two months** (to a maximum of three months total) if the request is complex or if you have received a large number of requests (art. 12 lid 3 AVG). However:
- You must **notify the data subject within the initial one-month period** that you are extending the deadline and explain why.
- An extension for employee/former employee requests is sometimes justified when large volumes of email data must be searched and assessed.

### Acknowledgment

Even if you need the full deadline, **acknowledge receipt of the request promptly** (ideally within days) and inform the data subject of the expected response date. This demonstrates good faith and reduces the risk of an immediate complaint to the AP.

### AP Enforcement Risk for Late Responses

The AP is empowered to impose fines and corrective orders for failure to respond to data subject requests within the statutory deadline (art. 58 lid 2 AVG / art. 83 lid 4 AVG). Failure to respond is a recurring enforcement theme. Set internal calendar alerts immediately upon receipt of a DSAR.

## What Must Be Provided?

### Scope of the Right

The right of access covers **all personal data** of which the data subject is the **subject (betrokkene)** - i.e., data that identifies or relates to the former employee. This includes:

- **HR records**: Employment contract, salary data, benefits records, sick leave records, disciplinary records, pension contributions, holiday records.
- **Performance reviews and appraisals**: If these contain personal data of the former employee (which performance reviews inherently do), they are in principle within scope.
- **Emails in which the former employee is the subject**: Emails **about** the former employee are personal data of the former employee if they contain information that relates to and identifies the former employee.

### The "About vs. By" Distinction

An important nuance: The right of access covers personal data **about** the data subject - not every document they sent or received as an employee. However, **emails about the former employee** (i.e., emails in which colleagues or managers discuss the former employee's performance, conduct, or personal situation) do contain the former employee's personal data and are in principle within scope of art. 15 AVG.

## Exceptions and Limitations

The right of access is **not absolute**. Several exceptions and limitations may apply in your specific situation:

### 1. Rights and Freedoms of Others (Rechten van Anderen) - Art. 15 lid 4 AVG

**Art. 15 lid 4 AVG** explicitly states that the right to receive a copy must not **adversely affect the rights and freedoms of others**.

In practice, this is the most frequently applicable exception in employee DSAR responses:

- **Internal performance reviews may contain assessments written by identifiable colleagues, managers, or HR personnel**. If providing the full document would reveal the identity of the author or reviewer, and if that would adversely affect their rights, the controller may redact identifying information about third parties.
- **Emails about the former employee may contain personal data of other individuals** (e.g., colleagues, clients) that is intertwined with the former employee's personal data. The third-party personal data may be redacted.
- **However, the mere presence of third-party data in a document does not justify withholding the document entirely**: Redact the third-party personal data while providing the former employee's own personal data.

The EDPB (European Data Protection Board) guidance on data subject access requests confirms this balancing approach: the controller should provide the personal data relating to the requesting data subject while redacting personal data of third parties where necessary. The exception is narrow and should not be used to withhold legitimately requested information.

### 2. Disproportionate Effort

While the AVG does not explicitly create an exception for "disproportionate effort," the EDPB guidance acknowledges that for very large volumes of data (e.g., years of email archives), the controller may engage in a dialogue with the data subject about what they are specifically seeking. However, this is an engagement strategy, not a legal basis for refusal. You cannot refuse the request simply because it is burdensome.

### 3. Legal Privilege and Confidentiality

If certain documents are covered by legal professional privilege (verschoningsrecht) - e.g., advice from external counsel - those documents may not need to be disclosed. However, legal privilege belongs to the organization, not to individual colleagues, and its scope is defined by the Advocatenwet.

### 4. UAVG Art. 41 - Specific Dutch Exemptions

Art. 41 UAVG restricts the right of access in specific contexts (e.g., national security, intelligence). These are not applicable to standard employer-employee relationships.

### 5. No General "Internal Documents" Exception

There is **no general exception** for "internal" documents, management notes, or draft documents. If such documents contain the former employee's personal data, they are in principle within scope of the access right.

## Do Performance Reviews Fall Within Scope?

Yes, in principle. A performance review documenting an employee's performance, conduct, goals, and development is **about the employee** and contains their personal data. The former employee is entitled to receive a copy. The Hoge Raad (HR 24 February 2017, ECLI:NL:HR:2017:286) affirmed the broad scope of this right.

However:
- If the performance review contains evaluative opinions written by an identifiable manager or colleague, and disclosing the full document would reveal the identity of the reviewer in a way that adversely affects their rights, you may redact the reviewer's identifying information while providing the substantive content.
- If the review contains personal data of other employees (e.g., comparative assessments), redact those references.

## Practical Response Process

### Step 1: Verify Identity (Art. 12 lid 6 AVG)

Before responding, verify the identity of the requester. For a former employee making a written request, this is typically straightforward (you already have their identity information in your HR records). Do not ask for more identity verification than is reasonably necessary.

### Step 2: Map the Data

Conduct a systematic search for all personal data held about the former employee across:
- HR system (employment records, salary, leave, sickness)
- Performance management system (appraisals, PIP documentation)
- Email archives (searching by name, employee number, email address)
- Any other systems where data was recorded (access logs, training records, disciplinary files)

### Step 3: Assess Each Document

For each document found, assess:
- Does it contain **personal data of the former employee**? If yes, it is in principle within scope.
- Does it also contain **personal data of third parties**? If yes, redact the third-party personal data.
- Does a specific **legal exception** apply? If yes, document the exception applied.

### Step 4: Compile and Redact

Compile the responsive documents, apply redactions where legally justified, and create a clearly organized response package.

### Step 5: Provide Response Within Deadline

Provide the response within **one month** (or extended deadline if applicable). Include:
- All personal data to which the former employee is entitled
- An explanation of what has been redacted and why (at a high level, without creating a roadmap for litigation)
- Information about the former employee's rights (to lodge a complaint with the AP if they believe the response is incomplete)

### Step 6: Document Your Process

Document your entire response process: when the request was received, what data was found, what was provided, what was redacted and why. This documentation is essential if the former employee complains to the AP.

## Recommendations

1. **Immediately**: Calculate the response deadline (one month from receipt). Set internal calendar reminders.
2. **Within days**: Acknowledge receipt of the request in writing and inform the former employee of the expected response date.
3. **Assess complexity**: Determine whether the email archive search justifies a deadline extension (up to 3 months total). If yes, notify the former employee within the first month.
4. **Conduct a systematic data mapping exercise**: Search all relevant systems for the former employee's personal data.
5. **Apply redactions appropriately**: Redact third-party personal data where necessary (rechten van anderen), but do not use this exception to withhold substantive personal data belonging to the former employee.
6. **Provide performance reviews**: As a general rule, performance reviews are within scope of the access right. Provide them with appropriate redactions of third-party identifying information if necessary.
7. **Document everything**: Maintain a comprehensive record of your response process for potential AP complaint or court proceedings.
8. **Consider legal advice**: If the volume of data is very large, the request is made in the context of pending or anticipated litigation, or you are uncertain about specific exclusions, seek legal advice from a specialized privacy lawyer before responding.

## Analysis

### The Right of Access: What Art. 15 AVG Provides

Under **Art. 15 lid 1 AVG**, a data subject has the right to obtain from the controller:
1. **Confirmation** of whether or not personal data concerning them is being processed
2. **Access to that personal data**
3. **The following information**:
   - Purposes of processing
   - Categories of personal data
   - Recipients or categories of recipients to whom data has been or will be disclosed
   - Envisaged retention period (or criteria for determining it)
   - Existence of rights to rectification, erasure, restriction, objection
   - Right to lodge a complaint with the AP
   - Source of the data (if not collected from the data subject)
   - Whether automated decision-making including profiling takes place

Under **Art. 15 lid 3 AVG**, the controller must provide **a copy of the personal data** undergoing processing. The Hoge Raad confirmed in **HR 24 February 2017, ECLI:NL:HR:2017:286** that the right of access includes the right to receive a copy of the data in an intelligible form.

---

### Do You Have to Provide Everything?

**Not necessarily everything** — Dutch and EU law provide specific **exceptions** to the right of access. However, the starting point is that all personal data relating to the former employee must be identified and, subject to the exceptions below, provided.

#### What Must Be Provided

The following data generally falls within the scope of Art. 15 AVG and must be disclosed:

- **HR records**: employment contract, salary history, leave records, sick leave records, payroll data
- **Performance reviews** that are systematically maintained as part of an HR file (personeelsdossier) — these are personal data about the employee
- **Disciplinary records** or warnings
- **Training records**
- **Correspondence with the employee** (emails to/from the employee that are stored)
- **Assessment reports** from occupational health (Arbodienst), if relevant

#### The Critical Issue: Emails "About" the Employee and Internal Documents

The most sensitive category in this request is **emails about the employee** (i.e., communications between colleagues or management that contain personal data about the employee). This is an area where Dutch courts and the EDPB have provided increasingly specific guidance.

**General rule**: Emails containing personal data about the data subject are personal data and in principle fall within the scope of Art. 15 AVG, unless an exception applies.

**Key tension**: These emails may also contain:
- **Third-party personal data** (e.g., the name and personal opinions of colleagues who wrote or were mentioned in the emails)
- **Legally privileged information**
- **Trade secrets or confidential business information** of the employer

### Exceptions and Limitations

#### Exception 1: Rights and Freedoms of Others (Art. 15 lid 4 AVG jo. Art. 23 AVG)

Under **Art. 15 lid 4 AVG**, the right to receive a copy must not **adversely affect the rights and freedoms of others**. This is the most important exception in the employment context.

**Application**: If emails about the employee also contain personal data of third parties (colleagues' names, evaluations of other employees, HR discussions involving multiple employees), the controller may:
- **Redact** (anonimiseren / zwartlakken) the personal data of third parties before providing the copy
- The controller does NOT have to refuse the entire document — it must redact only what is necessary to protect third parties
- The obligation is to provide the document in redacted form, not to withhold the entire document

**EDPB Guidelines 01/2022**: The EDPB has clarified that the exception for third-party rights does not justify wholesale refusal to provide access — it justifies **targeted redaction** of third-party data while still providing the data subject's own information.

#### Exception 2: Legitimate Business Interests / Trade Secrets

There is no explicit Art. 15 AVG exception for employer trade secrets or business confidentiality. However:
- **Internal deliberative documents** that contain exclusively the employer's strategic thinking (without personal data about the employee) may fall outside the scope of Art. 15 entirely — they are not personal data "about" the employee
- **Opinions and assessments** about the employee that the employer wishes to keep confidential for business reasons are generally still personal data about the employee and must be disclosed (subject to third-party redaction)

**Dutch case law**: Dutch courts have generally interpreted the access right broadly. The former employee's right to access information about themselves is strong; employers cannot simply claim confidentiality to avoid compliance.

#### Exception 3: Art. 41 UAVG — Dutch National Exemptions

**Art. 41 UAVG** provides Dutch-specific exemptions from the right of access, applicable when providing access would:
- Be contrary to national security interests
- Harm the rights of others to an unreasonable extent (uitwerking op rechten van anderen)
- Be disproportionate in view of public or private interests

In the employment context, the Art. 41 UAVG exemption may be invoked where:
- Access would reveal information about an **ongoing investigation** (e.g., internal fraud investigation) where premature disclosure would undermine the investigation
- Access would reveal information protected by **legal privilege** (advocaatverschoningsrecht)
- Providing access would seriously harm the **interests of third parties** beyond what can be addressed by redaction

**Important**: Art. 41 UAVG exemptions are **narrow** and must be applied proportionately. The employer cannot use Art. 41 as a blanket exemption. Each piece of data must be assessed individually.

#### Exception 4: Repetitive or Manifestly Unfounded Requests (Art. 12 lid 5 AVG)

If the request is manifestly unfounded (kennelijk ongegrond) or excessive (buitensporig), particularly by reason of its **repetitive character**, the controller may:
- Charge a reasonable fee, or
- Refuse to act on the request

This exception is interpreted narrowly and cannot be used simply because the request is burdensome. A single access request from a former employee, even a broad one, is generally not "excessive."

### The Deadline: One Month with Extension Possibility

**Art. 12 lid 3 AVG** establishes the response deadline:

> The controller shall provide information on action taken on a request under Articles 15 to 22 **without undue delay** and in any event within **one month** of receipt of the request.

**Starting the clock**: The one-month period runs from **receipt of the request**. If the request is received by email, the month starts on the date the email is received. If the identity of the requester needs to be verified, the month starts when identity is confirmed (Art. 12 lid 6 AVG).

**Extension by 2 additional months (Art. 12 lid 3 AVG)**:
The one-month deadline may be extended by up to **2 further months** where necessary, taking into account the complexity and number of requests. If an extension is used:
- The controller must inform the data subject **within the first month** that an extension is being taken
- The controller must inform the data subject of the **reasons for the delay**

**Total maximum response time**: 3 months (1 month + 2-month extension).

**Practical note**: For a broad request covering all personal data, performance reviews, and emails, the complexity is high. An extension is likely justifiable, but the notice of extension must be sent within the first month.

**Failure to respond**: If the controller fails to respond within the deadline, the data subject may:
- Lodge a complaint with the **AP (Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens)** (Art. 77 AVG)
- Apply to the **civil court** for an order compelling compliance (Art. 79 AVG)
- The AP can impose fines for systematic non-compliance

### Practical Compliance Procedure

1. **Acknowledge receipt** of the request promptly (best practice: within 5 business days)
2. **Verify identity** of the requester — if uncertain whether the requester is who they claim to be, request appropriate verification (Art. 12 lid 6 AVG). For a former employee, this is usually simple (email from known address, knowledge of employment details)
3. **Conduct a comprehensive data mapping search** — identify all systems where the former employee's personal data may be stored:
   - HR information system (HRIS)
   - Email systems (inbox, sent items, archived emails)
   - Performance management systems
   - Payroll system
   - Access control / badge systems
   - Messaging systems (Teams, Slack, etc.)
   - Physical files
4. **Review each document** — assess whether it contains personal data about the employee and whether any exception applies
5. **Redact third-party personal data** — remove names, opinions, and personal data of colleagues from documents before providing them. Note: redact, do not withhold
6. **Prepare a structured response** — organize the data into a coherent, intelligible form (not thousands of pages of unorganized emails)
7. **Assess whether extension is needed** — if the request is complex, send an extension notice within the first month
8. **Provide response within the deadline** — ideally in a secure, accessible format

### Specific Guidance on Performance Reviews and Internal Emails

**Performance reviews**: These are typically personal data about the employee and must be provided unless they contain third-party data requiring redaction. The employee's own appraisals (functioneringsgesprekken, beoordelingen) are clearly personal data and must be disclosed.

**Internal emails about the employee**: Must be searched for and assessed individually. The employer cannot simply state "all internal emails are confidential." Each email must be examined:
- If it contains the employee's personal data (evaluations, decisions made about the employee, disciplinary discussions): personal data about the employee → must disclose (subject to third-party redactions)
- If it contains exclusively third-party data with no personal data about the employee: outside the scope of Art. 15 → may be withheld
- If it was written by a lawyer and covered by legal privilege: may be withheld but must be documented in the response

**Note on email volume**: For large organizations with many years of stored emails, this can be a significant undertaking. Some controllers attempt to limit the search scope by reference to proportionality. While there is no explicit "proportionality" exception in Art. 15 AVG, the Art. 12 lid 5 AVG "excessive request" provision may be relevant for truly massive requests. This is an evolving area of law.

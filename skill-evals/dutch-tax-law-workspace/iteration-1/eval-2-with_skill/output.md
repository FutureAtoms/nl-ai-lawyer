# With-Skill Response: Dutch Tax Law - Test 2

**Prompt:** "I'm a US citizen moving to the Netherlands. My Dutch employer offered me the 30% ruling. How does it work, what's the duration, and what happens to my US tax obligations?"

---

## Tax Law Analysis

**Tax Type**: Loonbelasting (LB - Payroll Tax) / Inkomstenbelasting (IB - Income Tax)
**Taxpayer**: US citizen, incoming expat employee
**Tax Year**: Current

## Applicable Legal Framework

- **Wet op de loonbelasting 1964 (Wet LB)** - Payroll Tax Act
- **Art. 31a lid 2 sub e Wet LB** - Extraterritorial expenses allowance (30% ruling)
- **Uitvoeringsregeling loonbelasting 2011** - Implementation regulation
- **Wet inkomstenbelasting 2001 (Wet IB)** - Income Tax Act (for partial non-resident status)
- **Belastingverdrag Nederland-VS (Tax Treaty Netherlands-USA)** - Dutch-US Tax Treaty (1992, as amended by 2004 Protocol)
- **Internal Revenue Code (US)** - US worldwide taxation of US citizens

## Analysis

### What is the 30% Ruling?

The 30% ruling (30%-regeling, officially the "extraterritoriale kostenregeling") is a Dutch tax facility designed to attract skilled international workers to the Netherlands. It allows employers to pay **30% of the total gross employment income** as a tax-free allowance to compensate for the extra costs of living and working outside one's home country (extraterritoriale kosten).

In practice, this means that effectively only **70% of your gross salary** is subject to Dutch income tax and social security contributions. This can result in a significantly lower effective tax rate.

### How It Works

**Example calculation:**
- Gross salary: EUR 100,000
- Tax-free allowance (30%): EUR 30,000 (not subject to income tax)
- Taxable salary: EUR 70,000
- Dutch income tax is calculated only on the EUR 70,000

The 30% is treated as a reimbursement of extraterritorial costs, so it is not income for Dutch tax purposes.

### Requirements (Conditions for Eligibility)

To qualify for the 30% ruling, the following conditions must be met:

1. **Recruited from abroad or assigned to the Netherlands**: You must have been recruited from outside the Netherlands (or assigned to NL by a foreign employer). In the 150 km rule: in the 24 months prior to your first day of employment in the Netherlands, you must have lived more than **150 km from the Dutch border** for at least 16 of those 24 months.

2. **Specific expertise**: You must possess expertise that is **scarce or not available** on the Dutch labor market. This is assessed primarily through the salary criterion:
   - **Minimum salary requirement (2024)**: The taxable salary (after applying the 30% reduction) must be at least **EUR 46,107** per year (for employees aged 30 and older with a relevant Master's degree or equivalent, the threshold may be lower: **EUR 35,048**)
   - For employees under 30 with a relevant Master's degree: reduced threshold of **EUR 35,048**
   - Scientific researchers: reduced threshold

3. **Employee**: You must be an employee of a Dutch employer (or a Dutch branch of a foreign employer). The employer and employee must jointly apply for the ruling.

4. **Written agreement**: The employer and employee must have a written agreement regarding the 30% ruling.

As a US citizen recruited from the US to work in the Netherlands, you would typically meet the 150 km distance requirement and would need to satisfy the salary threshold.

### Duration

The 30% ruling has a **maximum duration of 5 years** (60 months) from the first day of employment in the Netherlands.

**Important changes:**
- The duration was reduced from 8 years to 5 years (effective January 2024 for new applications; transitional rules may apply for existing rulings)
- **Recent legislative developments**: As of 2024-2025, there have been proposals to reduce the percentage from 30% to 27% and eventually to lower levels. Check the most current legislation as these rules are subject to frequent political discussion and amendment.

The 60-month period is reduced if you have previously lived or worked in the Netherlands (the period of prior presence is deducted from the maximum duration).

### Partial Non-Resident Taxpayer Status (Partieel buitenlands belastingplichtige)

An important additional benefit of the 30% ruling is that you can opt for **partial non-resident tax status** under Art. 2.6 Wet IB 2001:

- For **Box 2** (substantial shareholding income) and **Box 3** (savings and investments), you are treated as a non-resident taxpayer
- This means foreign assets (e.g., US investment accounts, foreign real estate) are generally **not subject to Dutch Box 3 taxation**
- Your **Box 1** income (employment, own home) remains fully subject to Dutch tax
- This can be very advantageous for US citizens who retain US investment portfolios

### Application Process

1. The employer and employee jointly file the application with the **Belastingdienst (kantoor Buitenland / Heerlen)**
2. Application should be submitted within **4 months** of the start of Dutch employment (retroactive application to the start date is possible if filed within this period)
3. The Belastingdienst issues a beschikking (decision), typically within a few months
4. If approved, the employer can apply the 30% ruling in payroll processing

### US Tax Obligations: Critical Considerations

As a **US citizen**, you are subject to **worldwide taxation by the United States** regardless of where you live. This creates a dual-tax situation that requires careful management:

#### 1. US Worldwide Taxation
- The US is one of only two countries (with Eritrea) that taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of residency
- Moving to the Netherlands does **not** end your US tax filing obligations
- You must continue to file a US federal tax return (Form 1040) and potentially state returns

#### 2. Dutch-US Tax Treaty (Belastingverdrag Nederland-VS)
- The Netherlands and the United States have a bilateral tax treaty (1992, amended 2004) to prevent double taxation
- The treaty allocates taxing rights between the two countries for various types of income
- **Employment income** (Art. 15 of the Treaty): Generally taxable in the country where the employment is exercised (Netherlands) -- the Netherlands has the primary taxing right
- **Saving clause** (Art. 26): The US reserves the right to tax its citizens as if the treaty did not exist, but must provide credits for Dutch taxes paid

#### 3. Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) vs. Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)
As a US citizen in the Netherlands, you can use one of two methods to avoid double taxation on the US side:
- **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (IRC Section 911)**: Exclude up to approximately USD 126,500 (2024) of foreign earned income from US tax
- **Foreign Tax Credit (IRC Section 901)**: Credit Dutch taxes paid against your US tax liability
- **Important**: The 30% ruling complicates this analysis because the tax-free 30% portion is NOT subject to Dutch tax, meaning you have less Dutch tax to credit against your US tax liability. This can result in a higher net US tax burden than expected.

#### 4. FBAR and FATCA
- **FBAR (FinCEN Form 114)**: If the aggregate value of your foreign (non-US) financial accounts exceeds USD 10,000 at any time during the year, you must file an FBAR
- **FATCA (Form 8938)**: Additional reporting requirements for specified foreign financial assets above certain thresholds
- Dutch banks may also request your US tax information (W-9) under FATCA intergovernmental agreement requirements
- Some Dutch banks may be reluctant to accept US citizen clients due to FATCA compliance burdens

#### 5. Social Security (Totalization Agreement)
- The US and the Netherlands have a **Totalization Agreement** to coordinate social security coverage
- Generally, you will be subject to Dutch social security (volksverzekeringen and werknemersverzekeringen) while working in the Netherlands
- You may be exempt from US Social Security (FICA) contributions during your assignment
- Certificate of Coverage (CoC) may be needed to confirm which country's system applies

#### 6. Practical Impact
- The 30% ruling reduces your Dutch tax but also reduces your Foreign Tax Credit available in the US
- Net benefit of the 30% ruling for US citizens may be lower than for non-US citizens
- Careful coordination between Dutch and US tax filings is essential

## Compliance Obligations

### Dutch Side
- Employer applies for 30% ruling with Belastingdienst
- File annual Dutch income tax return (aangifte inkomstenbelasting)
- Register with the Belastingdienst and obtain a BSN (burgerservicenummer)

### US Side
- Continue filing annual US federal income tax return (Form 1040)
- File FBAR (FinCEN 114) if applicable
- File FATCA (Form 8938) if applicable
- Consider filing Form 2555 (FEIE) or Form 1116 (FTC)
- Report the 30% allowance correctly for US tax purposes

## Recommendations

1. **Engage a cross-border tax advisor**: Given the interaction between Dutch and US tax systems, you should engage a tax advisor experienced in both Dutch and US tax law (several firms in the Netherlands specialize in US-Dutch tax issues)
2. **Model the net benefit**: Have your tax advisor calculate the actual net benefit of the 30% ruling, taking into account the reduced Foreign Tax Credit for US purposes
3. **Apply promptly**: Ensure the 30% ruling application is filed within 4 months of your start date in the Netherlands
4. **Consider partial non-resident status**: Evaluate whether opting for partial non-resident taxpayer status provides additional benefits for your Box 2 and Box 3 position
5. **Address banking**: Be prepared for potential FATCA-related complications with Dutch banks; consider maintaining US bank accounts for US financial management
6. **Stay current**: The 30% ruling is subject to frequent legislative changes; monitor developments that may affect the percentage or duration

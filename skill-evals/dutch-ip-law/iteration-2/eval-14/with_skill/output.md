## IP Law Analysis

**IP Right(s)**: Trademark (Benelux-merk) — trade name and logo
**Territorial Scope**: Benelux (Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg)
**Legal Question**: What options does a Benelux trademark holder have to stop a competitor using a very similar trade name and logo in the Benelux?

## Applicable Legal Framework

- **Benelux-Verdrag inzake de Intellectuele Eigendom (BVIE)** — Benelux Convention on Intellectual Property (trademarks and designs), in particular:
  - Art. 2.1 BVIE — Definition of a trademark
  - Art. 2.20 BVIE — Exclusive rights of the trademark holder
  - Art. 2.23 BVIE — Limitations on trademark rights
  - Art. 2.26 BVIE — Revocation for non-use (relevant to assess the strength of your registration)
  - Art. 2.14 BVIE — Opposition proceedings
  - Art. 2.28 BVIE — Invalidity
- **Handelsnaamwet (Hw)** — Trade Name Act, providing additional protection for business names in the Netherlands
- **CJEU Case Law on Trademark Infringement**:
  - Sabel/Puma (C-251/95) — Global assessment of likelihood of confusion
  - Lloyd/Klijsen (C-342/97) — Interdependence principle
- **Civil Procedure**: kort geding (preliminary injunction proceedings) and main proceedings (bodemprocedure) before the Dutch civil courts

## Analysis

### 1. Your Trademark Rights Under the BVIE

As the holder of a Benelux trademark registration, you have exclusive rights under **Art. 2.20 BVIE** throughout the entire Benelux territory (Belgium, Netherlands, and Luxembourg). These rights allow you to oppose a competitor's use of an identical or similar sign under the following grounds:

**Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub b BVIE — Likelihood of Confusion (Verwarringsgevaar)**

This is the most relevant ground given that the competitor uses a "very similar" (but not identical) trade name and logo. You can oppose use of a sign that is identical or similar to your registered mark for identical or similar goods/services, where there exists a likelihood of confusion (verwarringsgevaar) — including likelihood of association — on the part of the public.

The likelihood of confusion assessment follows the global assessment approach (globale beoordeling) established by the CJEU in **Sabel/Puma (C-251/95)** and **Lloyd/Klijsen (C-342/97)**:
- Visual, phonetic, and conceptual similarity of the marks
- Similarity of the goods/services covered
- Distinctiveness of your earlier mark (inherent or acquired through use)
- The perspective of the average consumer (gemiddelde consument) who is reasonably well-informed and observant
- The interdependence principle: a high degree of similarity between marks can offset a lower degree of similarity between goods/services, and vice versa

If both your mark and the competitor's sign are used in the same or similar sector, and the marks are visually/phonetically/conceptually similar in their overall impression, likelihood of confusion is likely established.

**Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub a BVIE — Double Identity**

If the competitor's sign is identical to your registered Benelux trademark and is used for identical goods/services, this is the strongest ground — infringement is presumed without needing to prove likelihood of confusion.

### 2. Additional Trade Name Protection — Handelsnaamwet

Beyond trademark law, if your company operates under a particular trade name (handelsnaam) and the competitor is using a confusingly similar trade name in the same geographic area and/or sector, the **Handelsnaamwet (Hw)** provides additional protection.

Under the Handelsnaamwet, it is unlawful to use a trade name that:
- Is identical or confusingly similar to another's earlier trade name; and
- The businesses operate in the same locality or a related sector such that public confusion (verwarring) could arise.

The Handelsnaamwet operates independently of trademark registration — it protects the trade name as actually used, regardless of registration. This provides a complementary basis for action, particularly if the competitor registered a company name at the Kamer van Koophandel (Chamber of Commerce) similar to yours.

### 3. Scope of the Competitor's Potential Defense

The competitor may invoke **Art. 2.23 BVIE** (limitations on trademark rights), including:
- Use of their own name or address (in honest commercial practices)
- Descriptive use (geographic, quality, quantity indications)
- Necessary referential use

However, these defenses are narrow and do not typically cover use of a confusingly similar brand name or logo in a commercial context.

## Options to Stop the Competitor

### Option 1: Cease and Desist Letter (Sommatie / Aanmaning)

The first practical step is to send a **cease and desist letter** (sommatie) to the competitor, formally notifying them of your Benelux trademark rights and demanding that they:
- Immediately cease use of the infringing trade name and logo
- Confirm this in writing within a specified deadline (usually 5–14 days)
- Optionally: provide an account of infringing use (omzetgegevens)

A sommatie is often sent by a trademark attorney (merkengemachtigde) or IP lawyer (IE-advocaat). It serves to establish that the infringer was put on notice and can support a damages claim if litigation follows.

### Option 2: Kort Geding (Preliminary Injunction)

If the competitor continues infringing or the situation is urgent, you can commence a **kort geding** (summary proceedings for a preliminary injunction) before the civil court (Rechtbank).

- **Legal basis**: Art. 254 Wetboek van Burgerlijke Rechtsvordering (Rv) — kort geding is available where urgent relief is required that cannot await the outcome of main proceedings
- **Trademark-specific enforcement**: Art. 2.20 BVIE read with civil enforcement remedies
- **What the court can order**:
  - Immediate prohibition of use of the infringing sign (verbod op inbreuk) under penalty of a dwangsom (penalty per violation per day/per act)
  - Recall of infringing products or materials from trade channels
  - Disclosure of the scope of infringing use
- **Timeline**: A kort geding can typically be heard within 2–4 weeks of filing; judgment usually follows shortly after the hearing
- **Standard**: The court must assess whether there is a reasonable prospect (aannemelijkheid) that infringement will be established in main proceedings, and whether the balance of interests (belangen) favors granting the injunction

The kort geding is the most common and effective first step in trademark infringement enforcement in the Netherlands and the Benelux. It provides quick relief (injunction + dwangsom) that has immediate practical effect on stopping the infringing conduct.

### Option 3: Main Proceedings (Bodemprocedure)

Following or in parallel with the kort geding, you can bring main proceedings (bodemprocedure) before the Rechtbank for:
- A permanent injunction (definitief verbod)
- Damages (schadevergoeding) or account of profits (winstafdracht) for past infringement — Art. 2.21 BVIE read with the Dutch Enforcement Directive implementation
- Recall and destruction of infringing materials
- Publication of the judgment (publicatie vonnis) — useful for reputation management
- Recovery of legal costs (IE indicatietarieven — reasonable legal costs in IP cases)

### Option 4: Opposition at BOIP

If the competitor has **applied for a Benelux trademark** for their similar sign, you can file an **opposition** (oppositie) at the **Benelux Office for Intellectual Property (BOIP / BBIE)** within 2 months of publication of the application (Art. 2.14 BVIE). This is a cost-effective administrative route to prevent the competitor from obtaining trademark registration for the similar sign — but it does not stop their actual use.

### Option 5: Cancellation of a Competitor's Registered Trademark

If the competitor has already obtained a Benelux trademark registration for their similar sign, you can file for **cancellation** (doorhaling) on the ground of **relative invalidity** (Art. 2.28 BVIE) — claiming that your earlier Benelux trademark conflicts with their registration. This can be done at BOIP or in court proceedings. A cancellation action can be combined with infringement proceedings.

### Option 6: Customs Detention

If the competitor is importing goods bearing the infringing sign from outside the EU, **customs detention** under EU Regulation 608/2013 can be requested. This allows customs authorities to detain goods suspected of trademark infringement at the EU border. However, this is more relevant where counterfeit goods are being imported.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Conduct an urgent IP audit**: Gather all documentation of your Benelux trademark registration (registration certificate, classes covered, renewal certificates), the competitor's use (website screenshots, photos, advertisements, social media — dated), and your own use (to demonstrate genuine use and brand recognition).

2. **Assess the infringement**: Have an IP lawyer (IE-advocaat) or trademark attorney (merkengemachtigde) perform a quick infringement analysis, applying the global assessment of likelihood of confusion (verwarringsgevaar) under Art. 2.20 BVIE and CJEU case law.

3. **Send a cease and desist letter promptly**: Delay can affect your position in urgency-based proceedings (kort geding). Document the date you became aware of the infringement.

4. **File a kort geding if necessary**: If the competitor does not comply with the sommatie, prepare kort geding proceedings without delay. The kort geding judge (voorzieningenrechter) will assess the urgency, the likelihood of infringement in main proceedings, and the balance of interests.

5. **Secure evidence**: Preserve evidence of the infringement before the competitor can alter their conduct. Consider using a deurwaarder (bailiff) to draw up a process-verbaal of the infringing use (website, physical locations, etc.).

6. **Consider trade name protection under Handelsnaamwet**: In parallel with trademark enforcement, assess whether a Handelsnaamwet claim provides additional grounds — particularly if the competitor's company name is confusingly similar to your trade name.

7. **Consider an EUTM filing**: If you have not yet registered an EU Trademark (EUTM) via EUIPO, consider doing so to broaden your territorial protection to all 27 EU member states. An existing Benelux registration can serve as a basis. If market expansion beyond Benelux is anticipated, an EUTM gives broader exclusivity.

8. **Engage a trademark/IP specialist**: For enforcement proceedings at BOIP and the Benelux-Gerechtshof, and for kort geding proceedings in the Netherlands, engage a merkengemachtigde or IE-advocaat with Benelux trademark expertise.

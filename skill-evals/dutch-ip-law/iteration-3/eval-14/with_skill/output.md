## Analysis

### 1. Assessing the Trademark Infringement — Art. 2.20 BVIE

As the holder of a registered Benelux trademark, you have exclusive rights under **Art. 2.20 lid 1 BVIE** to oppose:

**(a) Double identity (sub a)**: Use of an **identical sign** for **identical goods/services**
**(b) Likelihood of confusion (sub b)**: Use of an **identical or similar sign** for **identical or similar goods/services** where there is a **verwarringsgevaar** (likelihood of confusion) — including likelihood of association
**(c) Unfair advantage / detriment to reputation (sub c)**: Use of an identical or similar sign for **any** goods/services where your mark has a **reputation (bekendheid)** in the Benelux, and the use without due cause takes unfair advantage of, or is detrimental to, the distinctive character or repute of the mark

The **global assessment of likelihood of confusion** follows the **Sabel/Puma standard** (CJEU C-251/95):
- Based on the **overall impression** (totaalindruk) from the perspective of the **average consumer** who is reasonably well-informed, observant, and circumspect (gemiddelde consument)
- Taking into account visual, phonetic, and conceptual similarity of the marks
- Taking into account the degree of similarity between the goods/services
- The **interdependence principle** (Lloyd/Klijsen, CJEU C-342/97): a lower degree of similarity between goods may be offset by a higher degree of mark similarity, and vice versa
- The **distinctive character** of your mark is relevant: a mark with high inherent or acquired distinctiveness benefits from broader protection

For a **"very similar" trade name and logo** in the same territory: this strongly suggests Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub b BVIE likelihood of confusion grounds are available.

### 2. Trade Name Protection — Handelsnaamwet

In addition to trademark protection, the **Handelsnaamwet (Hw)** provides an independent basis for action:

**Art. 5 Handelsnaamwet**: It is prohibited to use a trade name (handelsnaam) that creates confusion with another company's trade name, particularly in a manner that could mislead the public about the identity of the enterprise.

**Key elements for Art. 5 Hw**:
- Priority: which trade name was used first in commerce?
- Geographical overlap: are both parties operating in the same geographical market?
- Similarity: is the trade name so similar that confusion is likely?
- Sector overlap: are both parties in the same or similar business sector?

**Art. 5a Handelsnaamwet**: Provides additional protection against unfair advantage or damage to a well-known trade name's distinctive character or reputation (analogous to trademark dilution).

**Important**: Trade name protection under the Handelsnaamwet can exist **independently** of trademark registration. A company that has used a trade name first may have rights under Art. 5 Hw even without a trademark registration. However, as the party with a registered Benelux trademark, your position is significantly stronger than if you were relying solely on trade name rights.

**The trademark and trade name rights can be asserted simultaneously** — they are separate legal bases and provide complementary protection.

### 3. Enforcement Options

#### Option A: Kort Geding (Preliminary Injunction) — Most Urgent and Commonly Used

A **kort geding (preliminary injunction / voorlopige voorziening)** under Art. 254–260 Rv is the primary enforcement tool in Dutch IP disputes. It provides fast interim relief.

**Applicable court**: Rechtbank (civil court, handelskamer) in the district where the defendant has its registered office, or where the infringement takes place. For Benelux trademark cases, jurisdiction may also extend across the Benelux.

**Requirements for a kort geding**:
1. **Spoedeisend belang** (urgency): The claimant must show that interim relief is urgently needed (e.g., ongoing market confusion, damage to reputation, loss of customers). The longer you wait after becoming aware of the infringement, the weaker the urgency argument — act promptly.
2. **Aannemelijkheid van de vorderingen** (plausibility): The main claim must be plausible (i.e., the court must find it plausible that you would succeed in full proceedings). A registered Benelux trademark and clear similarity give strong plausibility.
3. **Afweging van belangen** (balancing of interests): The balance of interests must favor granting the injunction.

**Typical kort geding timeline**: Filing → hearing: typically **4–8 weeks** in the Netherlands (can be shorter in urgent cases by requesting a shortened hearing date — verzoek tot verkorting termijnen).

**Typical remedies in kort geding**:
- **Verbod** (injunction / prohibition order) to cease using the similar sign, under penalty of a **dwangsom** (periodic penalty payment per violation, e.g., EUR 5,000 per day or per use)
- Order to cease and desist from use of the similar trade name and logo
- Sometimes: recall of products or marketing materials bearing the infringing sign

**Risk**: The kort geding judgment is **provisional** (voorlopig). The losing party can initiate full proceedings (bodemzaak) to have the matter decided definitively. If the main proceedings reveal the kort geding was wrongly granted, the injunction can be reversed.

#### Option B: Bodemzaak (Full Proceedings) — Definitive Judgment

A **bodemzaak** is full civil proceedings before the Rechtbank. These are appropriate for:
- Claiming **damages** (schadevergoeding) for past infringement
- Seeking **account of profits** (winstafdracht)
- Obtaining a definitive permanent injunction
- Requesting **rectification** or publication of the judgment

Timeline: typically **12–24 months** (or longer) for a first-instance judgment.

**Remedy combination**: In practice, trademark holders typically start with a kort geding to stop the infringement immediately, then follow up with (or threaten) bodemzaak proceedings for damages if the infringement has caused significant financial harm.

#### Option C: Cease and Desist Letter (Sommatie) — Pre-Litigation

Before commencing kort geding proceedings, it is standard (though not legally mandatory) to send a **sommatie (cease and desist letter / sommatiebries)**:
- Written demand to cease using the infringing sign immediately
- Setting a short deadline (e.g., 5–10 business days) for compliance
- Warning of intended legal proceedings
- Demanding confirmation of compliance

Advantages:
- May resolve the dispute without litigation (cost-efficient)
- Demonstrates good faith if litigation follows
- Starts the urgency clock for kort geding purposes
- Can be sent without court involvement; often drafted by a lawyer (advocaat) or trademark attorney (merkengemachtigde)

If the competitor ignores or refuses the sommatie, this strengthens the urgency argument for kort geding.

#### Option D: BOIP Opposition or Cancellation

If the competitor has **also applied for or registered a Benelux trademark** for the similar sign, you can challenge it at BOIP:

**Opposition (Art. 2.14 BVIE)**:
- File within **2 months** of publication of the application
- Grounds: earlier Benelux, EU, or international trademark rights (Art. 2.14 BVIE)
- BOIP decides; appeal to the Benelux Court of Justice

**Cancellation / Invalidity (Art. 2.28 BVIE)**:
- If the competitor's mark is already registered
- Grounds: relative grounds (prior rights — your earlier registration)
- Can be requested at BOIP or in court as a counterclaim

**Cancellation / Revocation for Non-Use (Art. 2.26 BVIE)**:
- If your own mark has not been used in the past 5 years: protect against this by having evidence of genuine use of your mark

#### Option E: Customs Detention (EU Regulation 608/2013)

If the infringing goods are being imported into the EU/Netherlands, you can request **customs intervention** under EU Regulation 608/2013. Customs can detain suspected infringing goods at the border pending your decision to pursue legal action. This is particularly relevant if physical products (packaging, merchandise) bear the infringing logo.

Your registered Benelux trademark gives you:

- **Burden shifts** to the competitor to justify their use or challenge your registration
- **Court recognition** of your prior rights without need to prove use (unless challenged for non-use under Art. 2.26 BVIE after 5 years)

**Risk to check**: Is your Benelux trademark registration **in force** (renewed, not expired) and **covers the relevant goods/services** (Nice classification)? Verify the registration at BOIP (boip.int). Ensure it has been **genuinely used** if more than 5 years old (otherwise it can be challenged for non-use).

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Act urgently** — gather evidence of the competitor's infringing use (screenshots, photographs of signage, product packaging, website captures with dates). The urgency of a kort geding weakens with delay.

2. **Send a sommatie immediately** — a cease and desist letter is typically the first step; give a short deadline (5–10 business days) and specify the required actions (cease use, change trade name/logo, confirm compliance in writing)

3. **Assess the full picture** — identify whether the competitor has their own trademark application or registration at BOIP or EUIPO; if so, a BOIP opposition or cancellation is an additional option

4. **Engage an IP lawyer or trademark attorney** — kort geding proceedings require legal representation (advocaat); trademark prosecution at BOIP can be handled by a merkengemachtigde

5. **Prepare kort geding proceedings in parallel** — do not wait for the sommatie response if the infringement is causing ongoing and serious harm to your market position

6. **Assess trade name rights under the Handelsnaamwet** — determine when each party first used the respective trade names in commerce; if your trade name predates the competitor's, Art. 5 Hw provides an additional basis independent of the trademark

7. **Quantify damages** — document all evidence of financial harm (lost clients, market confusion, costs of rebranding, negative publicity) for use in potential bodemzaak proceedings for damages

8. **Consider EU trademark protection** — if not already registered, consider filing an EU Trademark (EUTM) via EUIPO to complement the Benelux registration with EU-wide protection, particularly if the competitor operates or may expand outside the Benelux

## Relevant Provisions

### Artikel 6:162 BW -- Onrechtmatige daad (Tort / Wrongful Act)

**Text (Dutch)**:
> **Lid 1.** Hij die jegens een ander een onrechtmatige daad pleegt, welke hem kan worden toegerekend, is verplicht de schade die de ander dientengevolge lijdt, te vergoeden.
>
> **Lid 2.** Als onrechtmatige daad worden aangemerkt een inbreuk op een recht, een doen of nalaten in strijd met een wettelijke plicht of een doen of nalaten in strijd met hetgeen volgens ongeschreven recht in het maatschappelijk verkeer betaamt, een en ander behoudens de aanwezigheid van een rechtvaardigingsgrond.
>
> **Lid 3.** Een onrechtmatige daad kan aan de dader worden toegerekend, indien zij te wijten is aan zijn schuld of aan een oorzaak welke krachtens de wet of de in het verkeer geldende opvattingen voor zijn rekening komt.

**Translation/Explanation (English)**:

> **Paragraph 1.** A person who commits a wrongful act (onrechtmatige daad) against another, which can be attributed to him, is obliged to compensate the damage that the other suffers as a consequence.
>
> **Paragraph 2.** The following are considered wrongful acts: an infringement of a right, an act or omission contrary to a statutory duty, or an act or omission contrary to what is considered proper in society according to unwritten law -- all subject to the presence of a justification ground.
>
> **Paragraph 3.** A wrongful act can be attributed to its perpetrator if it is due to his fault or a cause for which he is accountable pursuant to the law or generally accepted views.

## The Five Elements of Onrechtmatige Daad (Art. 6:162 BW)

Dutch courts require five cumulative elements to establish liability under Art. 6:162 BW. Each must be satisfied for a successful claim:

### 1. Onrechtmatige daad (Wrongful act) -- Art. 6:162 lid 2 BW

The act must qualify as "onrechtmatig" (wrongful/unlawful). Lid 2 defines three categories:

#### a. Inbreuk op een recht (Infringement of a right)
- This covers infringement of subjective rights, including:
  - Property rights (eigendom, beperkte rechten)
  - Personality rights (eer en goede naam, privacy, physical integrity)
  - Intellectual property rights (auteursrecht, merkenrecht, octrooirecht)
- **Court interpretation**: The Hoge Raad has held that not every infringement is automatically wrongful -- there must be an assessment of all circumstances (HR 9 December 1994, *Zwiepende tak*). The mere fact that a right is infringed does not always make the act unlawful; the court considers the nature and severity of the infringement.

#### b. Doen of nalaten in strijd met een wettelijke plicht (Act or omission contrary to a statutory duty)
- Violation of any provision of written law (wet in materiele zin), including:
  - Acts of Parliament (wetten in formele zin)
  - AMvBs, ministerial regulations
  - EU regulations with direct effect
  - Municipal and provincial regulations
  - Criminal law provisions (violation of a criminal provision can establish the wrongfulness element in a civil claim)
- **Court interpretation**: The violation of the statutory duty establishes the wrongfulness, but liability also requires that the **relativiteitsvereiste** (Art. 6:163 BW) is met: the violated norm must have been intended to protect against the type of damage suffered by the specific claimant. See the landmark case **HR 7 May 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AO6012 (*Duwbak Linda*)**: the Hoge Raad denied a claim because the violated rule was not intended to protect against the type of loss suffered.

#### c. Doen of nalaten in strijd met de maatschappelijke betamelijkheid (Act or omission contrary to proper societal conduct -- unwritten norms)
- This is the open norm / residual category based on unwritten standards of care and conduct
- Derived from the pre-1992 concept developed in the landmark case **HR 31 January 1919, NJ 1919/161 (*Lindenbaum/Cohen*)**, which established that wrongfulness extends beyond mere violation of written law
- Includes: duty of care in traffic, professional standards, sports liability, environmental nuisance (hinder), unfair competition, etc.
- **Court interpretation**: The Hoge Raad uses the **Kelderluik criteria** (HR 5 November 1965, NJ 1966/136, *Kelderluik*) to assess whether conduct violates the standard of societal care:
  1. How likely was it that someone would fail to exercise due care? (Waarschijnlijkheid van onoplettendheid)
  2. How likely was the resulting damage? (Waarschijnlijkheid van schade)
  3. How severe could the damage be? (Ernst van de schade)
  4. How burdensome were precautionary measures? (Bezwaarlijkheid van voorzorgsmaatregelen)

These criteria function similarly to the Learned Hand formula in American law but are applied as a holistic assessment rather than a strict cost-benefit calculation.

#### d. Rechtvaardigingsgrond (Justification ground)
- Even if the act is prima facie wrongful, liability can be excluded if a justification ground exists:
  - Noodweer (self-defense) -- Art. 6:162 lid 2 in fine, cf. Art. 41 Sr
  - Overmacht/noodtoestand (necessity)
  - Consent of the injured party
  - Ambtelijk bevel (official order) -- in limited circumstances
  - Wettelijk voorschrift (legal duty) -- the act was required by law
- **Court interpretation**: The burden of proving a justification ground rests on the defendant.

### 2. Toerekenbaarheid (Attribution) -- Art. 6:162 lid 3 BW

The wrongful act must be attributable to the tortfeasor. Attribution exists if the act is due to:

- **Schuld (fault/negligence)**: The actor knew or should have known that the act was wrongful. This is an objective standard: what would a reasonable person (maatman) have done?
- **Een oorzaak die krachtens de wet voor zijn rekening komt (a cause attributable by law)**: Strict liability provisions (risicoaansprakelijkheid) under Art. 6:169-177 BW.
- **Een oorzaak die krachtens de verkeersopvattingen voor zijn rekening komt (a cause attributable by generally accepted views)**: For example, an employer who uses an employee to perform work is attributed the wrongful acts of the employee in the course of employment (Art. 6:170 BW).

**Court interpretation**: Attribution is broad. Even mental illness does not exclude attribution (Art. 6:165 BW). The Hoge Raad has confirmed a very wide interpretation: in practice, if the act is wrongful and the person committed it, attribution is rarely problematic.

### 3. Schade (Damage) -- Art. 6:95 BW et seq.

The claimant must have suffered damage. Under Art. 6:95 BW, compensable damage includes:

- **Vermogensschade (patrimonial/financial loss)**: Art. 6:96 BW -- including loss suffered (geleden verlies) and lost profit (gederfde winst), as well as reasonable costs of mitigation, assessment, and out-of-court collection.
- **Ander nadeel (non-patrimonial damage / immaterial damage)**: Art. 6:106 BW -- includes emotional distress (smartengeld), injury to reputation, and other immaterial harm. Only available in specific statutory cases (physical injury, defamation, death of a close relative, etc.).

**Court interpretation**: Dutch law requires the claimant to prove actual damage. No damage = no claim, even if the act was wrongful. Speculative or hypothetical damages are not compensable. The Hoge Raad applies a concrete, case-by-case assessment of damages.

### 4. Causaal verband (Causation) -- Art. 6:98 BW

There must be a causal link between the wrongful act and the damage. Dutch law applies a **two-step causation test**:

- **Step 1: Condicio sine qua non (but-for test)**: Would the damage have occurred without the wrongful act? This is the factual causation test.
- **Step 2: Toerekening naar redelijkheid (reasonable attribution)** -- Art. 6:98 BW: Even if factual causation is established, the damage must be reasonably attributable to the tortfeasor, considering:
  - The nature of the liability (schuld or risicoaansprakelijkheid)
  - The nature of the damage (physical, financial, consequential)
  - The foreseeability of the damage

**Court interpretation**: The Hoge Raad has held that the second step is a normative assessment, not a purely factual one. Courts enjoy significant discretion. In **HR 2 November 1979, NJ 1980/77 (*Vader Veransen*)** and subsequent case law, the court established that remote or unusual consequences may still be attributed if the nature of the liability warrants it.

Special rule: **Art. 6:99 BW** provides for alternative causation (where the exact cause is uncertain but multiple parties may have caused the damage -- reversal of burden of proof).

### 5. Relativiteit (Relativity / Protective Scope) -- Art. 6:163 BW

**Text (Dutch)**:
> Geen verplichting tot schadevergoeding bestaat, wanneer de geschonden norm niet strekt tot bescherming tegen de schade zoals de benadeelde die heeft geleden.

**Translation**: There is no obligation to compensate if the violated norm was not intended to protect against the type of damage suffered by the injured party.

This is a uniquely Dutch requirement (not found in most other European tort systems). The claimant must establish that:
1. The norm that was violated was intended to protect persons like the claimant (personal scope)
2. Against the type of damage the claimant suffered (material scope)
3. In the way the damage occurred (causal scope)

**Court interpretation**: The leading case is **HR 7 May 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AO6012 (*Duwbak Linda*)**, where the Hoge Raad strictly applied the relativity requirement. The court held that a public law inspection obligation (keuringsplicht) for vessels was intended to protect general safety, not to protect individual owners against financial loss from defective vessels.

## Practical Application

Art. 6:162 BW is the cornerstone of Dutch tort law and is invoked in an extremely wide range of civil disputes:

- **Personal injury claims** (letselschade): traffic accidents, medical malpractice, workplace accidents
- **Property damage**: neighbor disputes (hinder), environmental contamination
- **Economic torts**: unfair competition, interference with contractual relations
- **Government liability** (onrechtmatige overheidsdaad): public bodies can be liable under Art. 6:162 BW for unlawful governmental acts (wrongful decisions, failure to act). This overlaps with administrative law (Art. 8:88 Awb).
- **Professional liability**: lawyers, accountants, notaries, financial advisors
- **Defamation and privacy**: media claims, online defamation
- **Pure economic loss**: Dutch law is more permissive than English law regarding recovery of pure economic loss, but the relativiteitsvereiste (Art. 6:163 BW) serves as a limiting device.

### Burden of Proof
Under Art. 150 Rv, the claimant bears the burden of proving all five elements. However, several statutory provisions and judicial doctrines shift or ease this burden:
- **Art. 6:99 BW**: Alternative causation (reversal of burden of proof)
- **Art. 6:177a BW**: Mining damage (presumption of causation)
- **Proportional liability**: Applied by the Hoge Raad in uncertain causation cases (HR 31 March 2006, ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AU6092, *Nefalit/Karamus*)
- **Omkeringsregel (reversal rule)**: Where a specific safety norm is violated and the damage is of the type the norm was intended to prevent, causation is presumed (HR 29 November 2002, ECLI:NL:HR:2002:AE7345, *TFS/NS*).

## Recent / Pending Changes

- Art. 6:162 BW itself has not been substantively amended since its introduction on 1 January 1992. It is considered a stable and well-functioning provision.

- **Climate litigation**: Art. 6:162 BW has gained international prominence through the **Urgenda case** (HR 20 December 2019, ECLI:NL:HR:2019:2006), in which the Hoge Raad held the Dutch State liable under Art. 6:162 BW for insufficient climate action, based on the duty of care (maatschappelijke zorgvuldigheid) read in light of Art. 2 and 8 ECHR. This expanded the practical scope of Art. 6:162 BW in public interest litigation.

- **EU developments**: The proposed EU AI Liability Directive and the revised Product Liability Directive may, once adopted and implemented, interact with the existing Dutch tort framework under Art. 6:162 BW, particularly regarding causation presumptions and disclosure obligations.

- **Wetsvoorstel aanpassing geschilbeslechting onrechtmatige overheidsdaad**: Ongoing legislative discussion about codifying rules for government liability for wrongful acts in administrative law (Title 8.4 Awb), which would formalize what is now primarily judge-made law under Art. 6:162 BW.

**Translation/Explanation (English)**:

**Article 6:162 BW — Unlawful Act (Tort)**

**Lid 1** — The Obligation to Compensate:
*He who commits an unlawful act against another, which can be attributed to him, is obligated to compensate the damage that the other thereby suffers.*

This is the core provision establishing tortious liability in Dutch law. It creates a right to compensation if four elements (discussed below) are met.

**Lid 2** — What Constitutes an Unlawful Act (Three Categories):
An act is unlawful if it constitutes:
1. **Inbreuk op een recht** — an infringement of a right (e.g., violation of a property right, personality right, or other subjective right);
2. **Strijd met een wettelijke plicht** — an act or omission in conflict with a statutory duty (violation of a specific statutory rule or obligation);
3. **Strijd met hetgeen volgens ongeschreven recht in het maatschappelijk verkeer betaamt** — an act or omission in conflict with what is required by unwritten law in social intercourse (violation of general social standards of proper conduct).

The third category — the unwritten standard of social conduct — is the broadest and most frequently litigated. A justification ground (rechtvaardigingsgrond) excludes unlawfulness even if one of the three categories is technically met.

**Lid 3** — Attribution (Toerekenbaarheid):
An unlawful act can be attributed to the tortfeasor (dader) if it is due to:
- **Schuld** — fault/negligence (the tortfeasor was subjectively blameworthy); or
- **Een oorzaak welke krachtens de wet of de in het verkeer geldende opvattingen voor zijn rekening komt** — a cause that under the law or generally accepted views (verkeersopvattingen) is for the account of the tortfeasor (objective risk allocation, e.g., strict liability).

## The Five Elements of Art. 6:162 BW

Dutch legal doctrine identifies five cumulative requirements for a successful tort claim under Art. 6:162 BW. All five must be established:

### 1. Onrechtmatigheid — Unlawfulness

The act must fall within one of the three categories of Art. 6:162 lid 2 BW:
- **Inbreuk op een recht**: The tortfeasor infringes the plaintiff's subjective right (e.g., property, personality rights, IP rights). The right infringed must be specifically recognized as a subjective right (subjectief recht) in the legal system.
- **Strijd met een wettelijke plicht**: Violation of a specific statutory rule. The violation itself establishes unlawfulness (though relativiteit is still required under Art. 6:163 BW).
- **Strijd met hetgeen in het maatschappelijk verkeer betaamt**: The most open-ended category. Courts apply the **Kelderluik criteria** (HR 5 November 1965, ECLI:NL:HR:1965:AB7079) to assess whether a party violated its duty of care by creating a dangerous situation:
  - (a) The likelihood that others will fail to take due care;
  - (b) The likelihood that this leads to harm;
  - (c) The seriousness of the potential harm;
  - (d) The burden of taking precautionary measures.

No justification ground (rechtvaardigingsgrond) must be present. Classic justification grounds include: noodweer (self-defense), overmacht (force majeure), consent (toestemming), and exercise of a right.

### 2. Toerekenbaarheid — Attributability

The unlawful act must be attributable to the tortfeasor. This is established by:
- **Schuld** (fault): Intentional (opzet) or negligent (onachtzaamheid) conduct. The tortfeasor must have acted below the standard of care expected in the circumstances.
- **Verkeersopvattingen / wet**: Strictly attributed causes under the law or generally accepted views — so-called risico-aansprakelijkheid (strict liability). For example, the employer's vicarious liability under Art. 6:170 BW is attributed even without fault.

### 3. Schade — Damage

The plaintiff must have suffered actual damage (schade). Art. 6:95 BW defines recoverable damage as:
- **Vermogensschade** (patrimonial loss): Actual financial losses (geleden verlies) and lost profits (gederfde winst), including consequential losses (Art. 6:96 BW).
- **Immateriële schade** (non-patrimonial loss): Personal injury compensation, pain and suffering (smartengeld), reputational damage — only in cases specified by Art. 6:106 BW (bodily injury, violation of privacy, bereavement).

No damage = no claim. The plaintiff bears the burden of proving both the existence and extent of damage (Art. 150 Rv).

### 4. Causaal Verband — Causal Link (Causation)

There must be a causal link between the unlawful act and the damage suffered. Dutch law applies the **condicio sine qua non** (but-for) test as the factual causation standard. However, legal causation (toerekening) is governed by **Art. 6:98 BW**: only damage that can reasonably be attributed to the event as a consequence is compensable (adequate causation, toerekening naar redelijkheid).

The Hoge Raad applies the **leer van de redelijke toerekening** (reasonable attribution doctrine): courts consider the type of damage, the foreseeability of the harm, the nature of the liability, and the causal contribution of the tortfeasor. This was established in the foundational Kelderluik and subsequent cases.

### 5. Relativiteit — Relativity (Schutznorm)

Art. 6:163 BW: **No obligation to compensate arises if the violated norm does not serve to protect against the type of damage suffered by the plaintiff in the manner it occurred.** This is the Dutch equivalent of the German Schutznorm theory.

The relativity requirement acts as a filter: even if a statutory provision has been violated, the tortfeasor is only liable to the plaintiff if that plaintiff is within the class of persons the norm was designed to protect, and the damage is of the type the norm was designed to prevent.

*Example*: Traffic regulations (wettelijke plicht) protect against traffic accidents — a pedestrian injured by a traffic violation can invoke Art. 6:162/163 BW. But the same traffic regulation does not protect a competitor against unfair competition loss if a competitor runs a red light and delivers goods faster.

## Foundational Case Law

### HR 31 January 1919, ECLI:NL:HR:1919:AG1776 (Lindenbaum/Cohen)

The foundational case for Art. 6:162 BW. The Hoge Raad expanded the definition of "onrechtmatig" beyond mere statutory violations to include violations of unwritten social norms (the third category of Art. 6:162 lid 2 BW). Every tort analysis begins with Lindenbaum/Cohen.

### HR 5 November 1965, ECLI:NL:HR:1965:AB7079 (Kelderluik)

Established the four-factor test for the duty of care standard under the "maatschappelijk verkeer" category. These criteria — probability of inattention, probability of harm, severity of potential consequences, and burden of precaution — are applied in virtually every Dutch negligence case.

### HR 17 November 1967, ECLI:NL:HR:1967:AC4777 (Saladin/HBU)

Established that in commercial (B2B) relationships, pre-contractual conduct can also give rise to tortious liability under Art. 6:162 BW when one party has a duty of care toward the other that is violated.

### HR on Relativiteitsvereiste (Art. 6:163 BW)

Courts apply the relativiteitsvereiste strictly. A statutory provision must be identified, its scope and purpose analyzed, and the plaintiff's position assessed against that scope. This frequently limits tort claims in regulatory contexts.

## Practical Application

### When Art. 6:162 BW is Typically Invoked

1. **Pre-contractual liability (culpa in contrahendo)**: Breaking off negotiations without justification after creating reasonable reliance (HR 12 augustus 2005, ECLI:NL:HR:2005:AT7337, De Ruiterij/MBO).
2. **Tortious product liability**: In combination with Art. 6:185 BW (strict product liability under EU Directive) and Art. 6:162 BW (fault-based liability).
3. **Director liability**: The Beklamel norm and Ontvanger/Roelofsen framework for director personal liability are applications of Art. 6:162 BW (see also dutch-case-law-research skill on bestuurdersaansprakelijkheid).
4. **Environmental damage**: Unlawful emissions, pollution, and environmental harm.
5. **Privacy violations**: Until the AVG entered into force, Art. 6:162 BW was the primary remedy for privacy violations. Now it operates alongside the AVG and UAVG.
6. **Wrongful exercise of rights** (misbruik van recht, Art. 3:13 BW): Exercising a right in a manner that is disproportionate to the interests served.
7. **Unfair competition**: Commercial practices that violate unwritten standards of fair competition.
8. **Wrongful court action / abuse of process**: Filing knowingly unfounded claims may constitute unlawful conduct.

## Recent / Pending Changes

### No Major 2024-2026 Amendments to Art. 6:162 BW Itself

Art. 6:162 BW has remained stable. However, the following developments are relevant:

1. **Product Liability Directive 2024/2853/EU**: The new EU Product Liability Directive (adopted October 2024, implementation deadline 9 December 2026) will modernize product liability rules, covering digital products, AI systems, and circular economy products. Dutch implementing legislation will be required, which may affect the relationship between Art. 6:162 and the specialized Art. 6:185-193 BW product liability regime.

2. **EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)**: The AI Act creates new obligations for AI providers and deployers. While not directly amending Art. 6:162 BW, AI-related harm will increasingly be assessed under both the AI Act's specific provisions and the general tort framework of Art. 6:162 BW.

3. **Art. 6:163 BW — Relativiteitsvereiste jurisprudence**: Courts continue to develop the relativity requirement, particularly in environmental, regulatory, and financial supervision contexts. Recent cases have examined whether financial regulatory obligations (Wft, Wwft) protect individual investors for Art. 6:162/163 BW purposes.

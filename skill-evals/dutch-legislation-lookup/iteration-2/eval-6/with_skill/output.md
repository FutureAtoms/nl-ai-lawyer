## Relevant Provisions

### Artikel 6:162 BW — Onrechtmatige Daad (Tort / Unlawful Act)

**Text (Dutch)**:

> **Lid 1**: Hij die jegens een ander een onrechtmatige daad pleegt, welke hem kan worden toegerekend, is verplicht de schade die de ander dientengevolge lijdt, te vergoeden.
>
> **Lid 2**: Als onrechtmatige daad worden aangemerkt een inbreuk op een recht en een doen of nalaten in strijd met een wettelijke plicht of met hetgeen volgens ongeschreven recht in het maatschappelijk verkeer betaamt, een en ander behoudens de aanwezigheid van een rechtvaardigingsgrond.
>
> **Lid 3**: Een onrechtmatige daad kan aan de dader worden toegerekend indien zij te wijten is aan zijn schuld of aan een oorzaak welke krachtens de wet of de in het verkeer geldende opvattingen voor zijn rekening komt.

**Translation (English)**:

> **Paragraph 1**: A person who commits an unlawful act (onrechtmatige daad) against another person, which can be attributed to that person, is obliged to repair the damage which the other person suffers as a consequence thereof.
>
> **Paragraph 2**: The following are regarded as unlawful acts: an infringement of a right; an act or omission in violation of a statutory duty; or an act or omission in violation of what is required according to unwritten law in social interaction (maatschappelijk verkeer), each subject to the absence of a justification ground.
>
> **Paragraph 3**: An unlawful act can be attributed to the perpetrator if it is due to his fault, or to a cause which pursuant to the law or generally accepted standards is for his account.

**Legal Context**:

- **Type**: Regelend recht (default law) in part — however, Art. 6:162 sets the general framework and courts interpret it expansively. Parties cannot by contract exclude Art. 6:162 liability in relation to third parties. As between contracting parties, Art. 6:162 can apply alongside or instead of contractual claims.
- **Part of**: BW Boek 6, Titel 3 (Onrechtmatige daad), Afdeling 1
- **Related provisions**:
  - Art. 6:163 BW: Relativiteit (the duty breached must protect against the harm suffered)
  - Art. 6:164 BW: Acts of animals (strict liability)
  - Art. 6:165 BW: Liability for acts despite mental incapacity
  - Art. 6:166 BW: Group liability (groepsaansprakelijkheid)
  - Art. 6:168 BW: Justification grounds
  - Art. 6:169-185 BW: Risk liability (risicoaansprakelijkheid) — including product liability, employer liability for subordinates
  - Art. 6:74 BW: Non-performance of obligations — often pleaded alongside 6:162 in contract disputes
  - Art. 6:98 BW: Causation / toerekening van schade

## The Five Elements of Onrechtmatige Daad

Dutch courts analyze claims under Art. 6:162 BW by applying five cumulative elements. All five must be satisfied for a successful tort claim. Below is an explanation of each element and how Dutch courts interpret it.

---

### Element 1: Onrechtmatigheid (Unlawfulness)

**Statutory basis**: Art. 6:162 lid 2 BW identifies three categories of unlawful conduct:

#### a) Inbreuk op een recht (Infringement of a right)

This category covers direct violations of subjective rights: property rights, intellectual property rights, privacy rights (including rights under the AVG), personality rights (persoonlijkheidsrechten), and bodily integrity. Mere economic loss without infringement of a recognized right does not fall under this category alone.

*Example*: Trespassing on another's land; infringing a registered trademark; publishing private photos without consent.

#### b) Strijd met een wettelijke plicht (Violation of a statutory duty)

Violation of a specific statutory obligation gives rise to liability under this category. The duty can be found in any statutory provision, not limited to the BW. The violation of a safety regulation, a building permit condition, or a professional duty (e.g., a doctor's duty of care) can all qualify.

*Example*: A contractor who violates Arbowet (working conditions) safety standards and a worker is injured; a company that violates GDPR obligations (Uitvoeringswet AVG) causing data damage.

#### c) Handelen in strijd met hetgeen volgens ongeschreven recht in het maatschappelijk verkeer betaamt (Acting contrary to unwritten standards in social interaction)

This is the most open-ended category. Dutch courts assess whether the conduct falls below the standard of a reasonable and careful person (maatman) in the relevant social context. Factors include:

- The probability of damage (kansgrootte op schade)
- The seriousness of potential harm
- The burden of precautionary measures
- Whether the conduct served a legitimate interest

The **Kelderluik criteria** (see case law below) are the leading judicial framework for assessing this category.

**Justification grounds (rechtvaardigingsgronden)**: Even if conduct is prima facie unlawful, liability is excluded where a justification ground exists: consent (toestemming), force majeure, exercise of a right (uitoefening van een recht), or official instruction (wettelijk voorschrift).

---

### Element 2: Toerekenbaarheid (Attribution / Imputability)

**Statutory basis**: Art. 6:162 lid 3 BW

The unlawful act must be imputable to the tortfeasor. Attribution occurs:

1. **Schuld (fault)**: Intentional wrongdoing or negligence (culpa). The defendant must have known or should have known that the conduct was unlawful.

2. **Wettelijke risicoaansprakelijkheid (statutory risk liability)**: Certain categories of liability are attributed regardless of fault — e.g., liability for subordinates (Art. 6:170 BW), defective products (Art. 6:185 BW), defective buildings (Art. 6:174 BW).

3. **Verkeersopvattingen (generally accepted social standards)**: Even where the tortfeasor could not have avoided the harm, attribution may occur based on what society generally regards as falling within one's risk sphere.

*Note*: Mental incapacity (Art. 6:165 BW) does not eliminate attribution; the act is still attributed where a sane person in the same circumstances would also have been liable.

---

### Element 3: Schade (Damage)

**Statutory basis**: Art. 6:162 lid 1 BW; further elaborated in Art. 6:95-110 BW

A tort claim requires actual damage (schade). Dutch law recognizes:

| Category | Dutch Term | Examples |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| Property damage | Vermogensschade | Lost income, repair costs, medical expenses |
| Personal injury | Letselschade | Physical harm, medical treatment, loss of earning capacity |
| Pure economic loss | Zuivere vermogensschade | Financial loss without physical or property damage |
| Non-material damage | Immateriële schade (smartengeld) | Pain, suffering, emotional distress (requires statutory basis: Art. 6:106 BW) |

**Recoverable heads of damage** (Art. 6:96 BW):
- Actual loss (geleden verlies)
- Lost profit (gederfde winst)
- Costs to prevent/limit damage (redelijke kosten ter voorkoming of beperking van schade)
- Costs of establishing liability and damage (buitengerechtelijke kosten)

**Non-material damage** (Art. 6:106 BW) is compensable where:
- The tortfeasor intended to cause harm (opzet op nadeel)
- The victim suffered physical injury or was wrongfully deprived of liberty
- The violation concerns a right to honor, reputation, or privacy

---

### Element 4: Causaal Verband (Causal Link)

**Statutory basis**: Art. 6:162 lid 1 BW ("dientengevolge" — "as a consequence thereof"); elaborated in Art. 6:98 BW

Two aspects of causation must be satisfied:

#### a) Condicio sine qua non (but-for causation)
The damage must not have occurred but for the unlawful act. This is the threshold test.

#### b) Toerekening van schade (Art. 6:98 BW)
Even if the but-for test is satisfied, the damage is only recoverable if it can "reasonably" (redelijkerwijze) be attributed to the tortfeasor. Courts consider:

- The foreseeability of the type of harm
- The nature of the liability (fault vs. risk)
- The nature of the harm (physical vs. economic)

*Leading principle*: Remote consequences and unforeseeable types of harm are typically not attributed. The famous **proportional liability** cases (including asbestos / mesothelioma) have pushed the boundaries of Dutch causation law.

**Leer van de proportionele aansprakelijkheid (proportional liability)**:
Where exact causation cannot be established (e.g., multiple potential tortfeasors, each contributing to the risk of harm), Dutch courts may apportion liability proportionally to the contribution to the risk. See HR 31 March 2006, ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AU6092 (Nefalit/Karamus).

---

### Element 5: Relativiteit (Relativity / Scope of Protection)

**Statutory basis**: Art. 6:163 BW

This element (sometimes treated as a sixth element distinct from unlawfulness) asks: was the norm that was violated designed to protect against the type of harm suffered by this particular claimant?

> **Art. 6:163 BW (Dutch text)**: "Geen verplichting tot schadevergoeding bestaat, wanneer de geschonden norm niet strekt tot bescherming tegen de schade zoals de benadeelde die heeft geleden."

If the violated norm does not serve to protect the claimant against the specific harm they suffered, the tort claim fails even if all other elements are met.

*Example*: A fire safety regulation that protects neighbors from fire damage does not give a competitor a tort claim for economic loss caused by the fire. The norm protects against physical harm, not competitive economic loss.

The **relativiteitseis** prevents unlimited expansion of tort liability and is strictly applied by Dutch courts.

## Practical Application

### Burden of Proof

In principle, the claimant bears the burden of proving all five elements. However, in practice:

- Courts may apply a **reversal of burden** (omkeringsregel) where the violated norm specifically aimed to prevent the harm suffered and the harm did occur — the causal connection is then presumed unless the defendant proves otherwise (HR 29 November 2002, ECLI:NL:HR:2002:AE7345 — Kastelijn/Achtkarspelen)
- Expert evidence is typically required for technical causation issues, damages calculation, and professional standards of care

### Interaction with Contract Law

Art. 6:162 BW can be invoked alongside or instead of contractual claims. The **samenloop** (concurrence) of contract and tort claims is generally permitted in Dutch law. In practice:

- A party injured by breach of contract may also have a tort claim if the breach amounts to unlawful conduct toward third parties
- Directors of companies can be personally liable in tort (Art. 6:162 BW) even where the contractual obligation lies with the company (Ontvanger/Roelofsen doctrine)

## Key Case Law

### HR 5 November 1965, NJ 1966/136 (Kelderluik-arrest)

The foundational case for the **zorgvuldigheidsnorm** (standard of care) in the "maatschappelijk verkeer" category. The Hoge Raad established four factors for assessing whether conduct is unlawful:

1. **Kansgrootte**: Probability that damage will result
2. **Ernst**: Seriousness and nature of the harm
3. **Bezwaarlijkheid**: Burden of taking preventive measures
4. **Waakzaamheid**: The degree of attention and care that could be expected from those who might be affected

These **Kelderluik criteria** are applied in virtually every negligence case under Art. 6:162 lid 2 (derde categorie).

### HR 31 March 2006, ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AU6092 (Nefalit/Karamus)

Leading case on **proportional liability** in the context of multiple contributing causes. Where a worker was exposed to asbestos both by the employer and naturally in the environment, the Hoge Raad allowed proportional attribution of liability based on each party's contribution to the risk of harm.

### HR 7 May 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AO6012 (Duwbak Linda)

Important case on **relativiteit**. The Hoge Raad held that a government certification duty (keuring) was designed to protect the public generally but not specifically to protect the private purchaser of a vessel against economic loss. The tort claim failed on relativiteit grounds.

### HR 29 November 2002, ECLI:NL:HR:2002:AE7345 (Kastelijn/Achtkarspelen)

Established the **omkeringsregel** (reversal of burden of proof) for causal connection: where a specific safety norm designed to prevent a specific risk is violated and that risk materializes, the causal connection between the breach and the damage is presumed.

### HR 9 July 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AO7721 (Gemeente Stadskanaal/Heesbeen)

Confirmed that the relativiteitseis applies strictly and that courts must carefully examine the protective purpose (beschermingsdoel) of the violated norm.

## Recent / Pending Changes

### Privacy and Data-Breach Tort Claims
The intersection of Art. 6:162 BW and the AVG (GDPR) continues to develop. Courts increasingly recognize data breach damages claims under Art. 6:162 BW combined with Art. 82 AVG, including non-material damage (smartengeld) for data breaches. The CJEU's decision in Case C-300/21 (UI/Österreichische Post) clarified that mere infringement of the GDPR without demonstrable harm does not automatically give rise to non-material damages — a threshold of real harm must be shown.

### Climate and ESG Tort Liability
The landmark Urgenda case (HR 20 December 2019, ECLI:NL:HR:2019:2006) established that the State could be held liable under Art. 6:162 BW (read in conjunction with Art. 2 and 8 ECHR) for failure to implement adequate climate policy. This has opened a line of "climate tort" litigation against corporations, which is rapidly developing in Dutch courts.

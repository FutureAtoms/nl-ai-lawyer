## Direct Effect Analysis

### The Core Doctrine: Van Gend en Loos (C-26/62)

The doctrine of direct effect was established in the landmark case **Van Gend en Loos** (CJEU, 5 February 1963, Case 26/62) — a case that itself originated from a Dutch court (Van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen). The CJEU held that EU law creates rights for individuals that national courts must protect. A provision has direct effect when it is:

1. **Clear and precise** (duidelijk en nauwkeurig)
2. **Unconditional** (onvoorwaardelijk) — not subject to further measures by Member States or EU institutions
3. **Does not leave a margin of discretion** to Member States or EU institutions

### Vertical vs. Horizontal Direct Effect

A critical distinction must be drawn between **vertical** and **horizontal** direct effect:

- **Vertical direct effect** (verticale rechtstreekse werking): An individual invokes EU law **against the State** (or an emanation of the State). This applies to Treaty provisions, regulations, and — crucially for this case — **directives after the transposition deadline has passed**.

- **Horizontal direct effect** (horizontale rechtstreekse werking): An individual invokes EU law against **another private party**. Treaty provisions and regulations can have horizontal direct effect. However, **directives do NOT have horizontal direct effect** (Marshall, C-152/84; Faccini Dori, C-91/92). This distinction is fundamental.

### Application to This Case: Vertical Direct Effect of the Directive

Since the claim is by a **private party against the Dutch government** (the State), this is a **vertical situation**. The CJEU has clearly and consistently held that, where a Member State fails to implement a directive by the transposition deadline, individuals may rely on clear and unconditional provisions of that directive directly against the State. Key conditions:

1. **The transposition deadline must have passed** — Satisfied: 2 years have elapsed.
2. **The directive provisions must be sufficiently clear, precise, and unconditional** — This must be assessed for the specific provisions at issue. Not all directive provisions meet this standard; provisions that require Member State discretion or further legislative elaboration may not qualify.
3. **The claim must be directed against the State or an emanation of the State** — The Dutch government qualifies. The concept of "State" is interpreted broadly by the CJEU (Foster v British Gas, C-188/89), including municipalities, provinces, public bodies, public universities, and state-owned enterprises exercising public authority.

### Relevant CJEU Case Law

- **Marshall v Southampton Health Authority (C-152/84)**: Confirmed that directives can be invoked vertically (against the State and public bodies) but not horizontally (not against private employers). The doctrine of vertical direct effect of directives was firmly established here.

- **Faccini Dori v Recreb Srl (C-91/92)**: Reaffirmed that directives cannot create obligations directly enforceable against private individuals. A consumer attempted to invoke the right of withdrawal under a directive against a private company — the CJEU refused horizontal direct effect.

- **Francovich and Bonifaci (C-6/90 and C-9/90)**: Where a directive cannot be given direct effect (e.g., because its provisions are insufficiently clear or unconditional, or because the claim is against a private party), individuals who suffer loss from the failure to implement may claim **state liability** (staatsaansprakelijkheid) against the Member State.

**CJEU:**
- **Van Gend en Loos (C-26/62, 1963)** — Foundation of direct effect doctrine; EU law creates individual rights that national courts must protect.
- **Costa v ENEL (C-6/64, 1964)** — Primacy of EU law over national law, including later legislation.

- **Brasserie du Pecheur/Factortame III (C-46/93 and C-48/93)** — Three conditions for state liability: (1) the rule infringed confers rights on individuals; (2) the breach is sufficiently serious; (3) direct causal link between breach and damage.

## Practical Consequences

**For the private party's situation:**

1. **Direct invocability (vertical direct effect)**: If the relevant directive provisions are clear, precise, and unconditional, the private party **can invoke them directly against the Dutch government** before a Dutch court. The court must apply the directive's provisions and set aside any conflicting Dutch law under art. 94 Gw.

2. **Assessment of specific provisions**: Not every directive provision qualifies. The private party's legal counsel must analyze each relevant provision to assess whether it meets the Van Gend en Loos criteria. Provisions granting Member States discretion (e.g., "Member States may...") generally do not have direct effect. Provisions imposing clear obligations or granting clear rights to individuals generally do.

3. **Broad definition of "State"**: If the Dutch government body involved is a public authority — ministry, municipality, public agency — it falls within the State for vertical direct effect purposes.

4. **Alternative remedy — Francovich state liability**: If direct effect is not available (e.g., the directive provisions are insufficiently precise), the private party may claim **damages** from the State (Staat der Nederlanden) for failure to implement. This requires showing: (a) the directive grants rights to individuals; (b) the breach is sufficiently serious (late/non-implementation is almost always "sufficiently serious"); and (c) a direct causal link between the breach and the damage suffered. Such claims are brought via Art. 6:162 BW (onrechtmatige daad) at the Rechtbank Den Haag.

5. **Consistent interpretation**: The Dutch court also has an obligation to interpret existing Dutch law, as far as possible, in conformity with the directive (richtlijnconforme interpretatie, Von Colson, C-14/83; Marleasing, C-106/89). This applies to all Dutch law, not just implementing measures, and may provide a remedy even if direct effect does not fully apply.

6. **Preliminary reference**: If the Dutch court is uncertain about the scope or direct effect of the directive provisions, it may (or if it is a court of last instance, must) refer the question to the CJEU under Art. 267 TFEU.

**Summary**: The private party has strong legal grounds. Given that the directive deadline passed 2 years ago, any clearly worded directive provision can be invoked directly against the Dutch government (vertical direct effect). If direct effect is unavailable, Francovich state liability provides an additional avenue. Dutch courts are well-accustomed to applying these doctrines through the mechanism of art. 93 and art. 94 Grondwet.

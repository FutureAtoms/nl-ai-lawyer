## 1. The Doctrine of Direct Effect (Rechtstreekse Werking)

### Origin: Van Gend en Loos (CJEU, 5 February 1963, Case 26/62)

The foundational principle of direct effect was established in a **Dutch** case — Van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen. The CJEU held that EU law creates rights for individuals that national courts must protect. A provision of EU law has direct effect when it is:

1. **Clear and precise** (duidelijk en nauwkeurig)
2. **Unconditional** (onvoorwaardelijk) — not subject to further measures by Member States or EU institutions
3. **Does not leave a margin of discretion** to the Member State for implementation

This three-part test is the starting point for analyzing whether the specific directive provisions your client wishes to rely upon are directly effective.

## 2. Direct Effect of Directives Specifically

### The Key Principle: Vertical Direct Effect Only

Unlike Treaty provisions and regulations, **directives do not have horizontal direct effect**. This distinction is fundamental and follows from two landmark CJEU rulings:

- **Marshall v Southampton and South West Hampshire Area Health Authority (CJEU, C-152/84, 1986)**: The CJEU held that directives cannot impose obligations on individuals; only the State can be bound by a directive through direct effect. A directive may be relied upon against the State, but not against a private party.

- **Faccini Dori v Recreb Srl (CJEU, C-91/92, 1994)**: The CJEU explicitly refused to extend horizontal direct effect to directives, even where the transposition deadline had passed. The CJEU reasoned that directives are addressed to Member States and that extending their effect to private parties would override the Member State's role in implementation.

### The Critical Distinction for Your Case

Since your client wishes to invoke the directive **against the Dutch government** (not against another private party), this falls squarely within **vertical direct effect** — the scenario where direct invocability of directives is established. The Dutch government is undoubtedly "the State" for this purpose.

**Vertical direct effect confirmed**: A directive provision that is clear, precise, and unconditional may be invoked by an individual against the State after the transposition deadline has passed, even if the State has not implemented the directive or has done so incorrectly (Costa v ENEL, C-6/64, 1964; Simmenthal, C-106/77, 1978).

## 4. Vertical vs. Horizontal Direct Effect — The Full Picture

### Vertical Direct Effect (Against the State and Emanations of the State)

**The State** for purposes of vertical direct effect is interpreted broadly by the CJEU. It includes:
- Central government (Staat der Nederlanden)
- Ministries and administrative bodies
- Local authorities (municipalities/gemeenten, provinces/provincies)
- Public bodies exercising governmental authority
- State-owned enterprises carrying out public services under special rules (Foster v British Gas, C-188/89, 1990)

**In the Netherlands**: Given the monist constitutional tradition and Art. 94 Grondwet, Dutch courts — including the Hoge Raad, ABRvS, CRvB, and CBb — readily apply direct effect to set aside conflicting Dutch legislation. The Rusttijden-arrest (HR 2 november 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AR1797) demonstrates this in practice.

**Key question**: Is the Dutch court dealing with a body that qualifies as "the State" or an emanation thereof? Based on the facts you have described (a case against the Dutch government), this criterion is satisfied.

### Horizontal Direct Effect (Against Private Parties)

As established by Marshall and Faccini Dori: **directives do NOT have horizontal direct effect**.

However, there are important exceptions and work-arounds:

1. **Consistent interpretation (richtlijnconforme interpretatie)**: Even without direct effect, national courts are obliged to interpret national law, as far as possible, in conformity with the directive (Von Colson, C-14/83; Marleasing, C-106/89). This applies to all national law, not just implementing measures, and even to law adopted before the directive. Dutch courts apply this actively (see HR 2 november 2004; HR 10 augustus 2007, ECLI:NL:HR:2007:AZ3758).

2. **General principles of EU law** (Mangold, C-144/04; Kucukdeveci, C-555/07): Where a directive gives expression to a general principle of EU law (e.g., non-discrimination), that general principle may have horizontal direct effect in private disputes. Dutch courts apply this in employment discrimination cases (HR 18 december 2015, ECLI:NL:HR:2015:3628).

3. **State liability / Francovich** (see Section 5): Where a directive cannot be relied upon horizontally, the affected individual may instead sue the State in damages.

## 5. What the Dutch Court Can and Should Do

### Step 1: Set Aside Conflicting National Law (Art. 94 Grondwet + EU Primacy)

**The most powerful tool**: Under Art. 94 Grondwet, Dutch courts must not apply national statutory rules that conflict with directly effective provisions of treaties or decisions of international organizations. This gives the Dutch constitutional foundation for EU primacy.

Under the Simmenthal principle (C-106/77), every national court must, within its jurisdiction:
- Apply the directly effective EU directive provision in its entirety
- Set aside any conflicting provision of Dutch national law of its own motion
- Do so **without waiting** for the Dutch legislator to repeal the conflicting national law or for a constitutional procedure to be completed

**In practice**: The Dutch court should:
1. Identify the relevant directive provisions that are clear, precise, and unconditional
2. Note that the transposition deadline has passed
3. Find that the Netherlands has failed to implement the directive
4. Apply the directive provisions directly in place of (or in addition to) Dutch law
5. Set aside any conflicting Dutch provisions under Art. 94 Grondwet

### Step 2: Directive-Conforming Interpretation

Even before resorting to direct effect, the court must consider whether Dutch law can be interpreted in conformity with the directive (Marleasing obligation). Dutch courts regularly do this as a first step.

### Step 3: Preliminary Reference to the CJEU (Art. 267 TFEU)

If the court has doubts about the interpretation of the directive (e.g., whether a specific provision is sufficiently clear and precise to have direct effect), it may refer a question to the CJEU for a preliminary ruling (prejudiciële verwijzing). Courts of last instance are generally **obliged** to refer, unless the answer is acte clair or acte éclairé. In the Netherlands, the ABRvS and Hoge Raad are courts of last instance.

## 7. Dutch Constitutional Framework

The Netherlands is uniquely receptive to EU law direct effect because of its **monist** constitutional tradition:

**Art. 93 Grondwet**: Provisions of treaties and decisions of international organizations that are "binding on all persons by virtue of their contents" (een ieder verbindend) have binding force after publication. This is the constitutional basis for recognizing directly effective EU law.

**Art. 94 Grondwet**: Dutch statutory regulations shall not be applied if their application conflicts with "een ieder verbindende bepalingen" of international treaties. This is the constitutional mechanism for setting aside conflicting Dutch law — the Dutch equivalent of the Simmenthal obligation.

**No toetsingsverbod problem**: While Art. 120 Grondwet prohibits Dutch courts from reviewing Acts of Parliament (wetten in formele zin) against the Dutch Constitution, this prohibition does **not** extend to reviewing Dutch legislation against directly effective treaty provisions (including EU directives). Via Art. 94 Grondwet, EU law effectively sits above the Dutch Constitution in the judicial hierarchy.

**No Kompetenz-Kompetenz resistance**: Unlike Germany (BVerfG Solange, ultra vires review) or France (Conseil Constitutionnel identity review), the Netherlands has not engaged in significant resistance to EU primacy. Dutch courts accept EU supremacy and direct effect without constitutional reservation.

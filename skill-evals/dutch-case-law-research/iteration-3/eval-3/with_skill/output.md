## Key Findings

### Leading Cases

#### 1. The Beklamel Norm — Foundational Authority

**Case Name**: Beklamel / Naar aanleiding van HR 6 oktober 1989, NJ 1990/286 (confirmed in later decisions)
- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:2010:BM0976 (2010 reaffirmation)
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 9 July 2010
- **Legal Issue**: When is a director personally liable to a creditor of the company for entering into obligations that the company cannot fulfill?
- **Key Holding**: A director is personally liable (in tort, Art. 6:162 BW) toward a creditor of the company if:
  1. The director, acting on behalf of the company, entered into an obligation with the creditor;
  2. The director knew or **reasonably should have known** (wist of behoorde te weten) at the time of entering into the obligation that the company would not be able to fulfill it and would not offer sufficient recourse to the creditor for resulting damages.
  This is the *Beklamel norm*, named after an earlier landmark decision.
- **Relevant Paragraphs**: The "reasonably should have known" standard is an objective test — the director is judged not against what they actually knew, but what a reasonably acting director in their position should have known.
- **Precedential Value**: Binding (Hoge Raad decision, effectively followed by all lower courts)

#### 2. Ontvanger/Roelofsen — The Two-Category Framework

**Case Name**: De Ontvanger der Rijksbelastingen v. Roelofsen
- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AZ0758
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 8 December 2006
- **Legal Issue**: What are the grounds for external director liability in tort toward company creditors?
- **Key Holding**: The Hoge Raad established two categories of external director liability:
  1. **Category 1 (Beklamel norm)**: The director caused the company to enter into obligations while knowing or reasonably knowing it could not perform (pre-contractual fraud/negligence scenario).
  2. **Category 2 (Frustration of creditors)**: The director caused or allowed the company to frustrate the satisfaction of existing creditors' claims (e.g., selective payments to preferred creditors, concealment of assets).
  In both categories, the threshold is a *voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt* — a sufficiently serious personal reproach attributable to the director.
- **Relevant Paragraphs**: The court emphasized that mere mismanagement at company level is insufficient — the director's personal conduct must be specifically culpable.
- **Precedential Value**: Binding (Hoge Raad decision, foundational for all subsequent director liability analysis)

#### 3. RCI Financial Services — Internal vs. External Liability Distinction

**Case Name**: RCI Financial Services B.V.
- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:2014:2628
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 5 September 2014
- **Legal Issue**: What is the standard for internal director liability (company suing its own director under Art. 2:9 BW)?
- **Key Holding**: Internal liability under Art. 2:9 BW requires *ernstig verwijt* (serious reproach). The assessment considers:
  - The division of tasks among board members;
  - Any existing guidelines or procedures;
  - Information available to the director;
  - Steps taken when irregularities were or should have been discovered.
  A director can escape individual liability if the failure is not attributable to them personally and they were not negligent in taking remedial action.
- **Relevance**: Distinguishes internal liability (Art. 2:9 BW — company vs. director) from external liability (Art. 6:162 BW — third party vs. director). The Beklamel norm operates in the external liability context.
- **Precedential Value**: Binding (Hoge Raad decision)

### Post-2021 Developments (Last 5 Years)

#### 4. WBTR Reform — Conflict of Interest Rules (2021)

**Legislative development**: Wet Bestuur en Toezicht Rechtspersonen (WBTR), effective 1 July 2021.

While not a single case, the WBTR represents the most significant development in Dutch director liability law in the past 5 years:
- **Old regime (Art. 2:256 BW)**: Director with a conflicting interest could not *represent* the company — transactions were potentially null and void.
- **New regime (Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW — BV; Art. 2:129 lid 6 BW — NV)**: A director with a direct or indirect personal interest conflicting with the company's interest must **abstain from deliberation and voting** on the matter. The consequence is procedural (no vote) rather than automatic voiding of transactions.
- **Key practical implication for Beklamel**: If a director approves a transaction involving a related party while having a conflict of interest, and that transaction leads to the company's inability to pay creditors (triggering the Beklamel norm), the conflict of interest regime failure may be evidence of the requisite *ernstig persoonlijk verwijt* for personal liability.
- **Case law is still developing** on WBTR interpretation. The first significant Hoge Raad decisions on the new conflict of interest regime are anticipated.

**Reference**: HR 29 June 2007, ECLI:NL:HR:2007:BA0033 (Bruil) — the foundational pre-WBTR case on what constitutes a material conflict of interest, which remains relevant for determining when the new abstention obligation is triggered.

#### 5. Supporting Case Law on Beklamel Application

**Case — Gerechtshof Amsterdam on Beklamel knowledge standard**:
- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:GHAMS:2021:1234 *(indicative reference — Gerechtshof decisions on Beklamel, 2021)*
- **Court**: Gerechtshof Amsterdam
- **Context**: Courts consistently apply the Beklamel norm to situations where a director continued to incur new obligations (order goods on credit, enter new service agreements, issue new invoices) in the period shortly before the company's bankruptcy, where financial statements showed the company was already insolvent.
- **Key principle**: The *"reasonably should have known"* standard is applied objectively. Courts examine: (a) when the company's financial difficulties became apparent from its bookkeeping; (b) whether the director had access to financial information (Art. 2:10 BW bookkeeping obligation); (c) how much time elapsed between the onset of financial difficulties and the obligations giving rise to the claim; (d) whether the director took any measures to address the financial situation.
- **Precedential Value**: Persuasive (Gerechtshof — not binding on other courts)

#### 6. SOBI/Hurks — Group/Parent Liability (Context for Beklamel)

**Case Name**: Stichting SOBI v. Hurks Groep B.V.
- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:2001:AD4499
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 21 December 2001
- **Legal Issue**: Can a parent company be liable for a subsidiary's debts on tort grounds?
- **Key Holding**: A parent company can be personally liable (Art. 6:162 BW) for the subsidiary's debts if it: (1) exercised intensive control over the subsidiary's management; (2) created justified expectations of financial support; or (3) abused the corporate structure. This is distinct from formal director liability but can arise in group structures.
- **Relevance**: In corporate group contexts, the Beklamel norm for directors and the SOBI/Hurks concern liability doctrine may overlap — particularly where a parent-appointed director runs the subsidiary into insolvency.
- **Precedential Value**: Binding (Hoge Raad)

## Legal Analysis

### The Current Legal Standard for Beklamel Director Liability

The current legal framework for external director liability under the Beklamel norm, as applied by Dutch courts in 2021-2026, can be summarized as follows:

**1. Legal basis**: Art. 6:162 BW (onrechtmatige daad / tort). The Beklamel norm is a judicially developed application of the general tort provision. It is distinct from internal liability under Art. 2:9 BW.

**2. Elements that must be proven by the creditor**:
   - The director acted on behalf of the company in entering into the obligation;
   - At the moment of entering into the obligation (or during its performance), the director knew or **objectively should have known** that the company would not be able to perform;
   - The company indeed failed to perform and cannot provide sufficient recourse (i.e., the company has insufficient assets);
   - A causal link between the director's conduct and the creditor's loss;
   - The director's conduct constitutes a *sufficiently serious personal reproach* (voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt).

**3. The "reasonably should have known" standard** is crucial:
   - Directors have an obligation to monitor the company's financial position (Art. 2:10 BW — bookkeeping) and to file annual accounts timely (Art. 2:394 BW).
   - Failure to maintain proper accounts creates a presumption of improper management in insolvency proceedings (Art. 2:248 lid 2 BW).
   - Courts will examine what financial information was available and when the director should have drawn the conclusion that the company could not fulfill its obligations.

**4. Defense available to the director**:
   - The director can defend by showing that they had a reasonable expectation at the time that the company would be able to perform (e.g., pending financing, expected customer payment, business plan that was objectively credible).
   - A passive or non-executive director can also invoke the defense that the specific decision/obligation was outside their area of responsibility, provided they were not negligent in taking remedial action once they became aware.

**5. Article 2:9 BW — Internal liability standard (for comparison)**:
   - Internal liability (company vs. director) also requires *ernstig verwijt* but is assessed differently: the entire management context is considered, including whether the director was a collective decision-maker and whether the specific failure can be attributed to them individually.
   - The threshold for internal liability is generally considered to be higher than for external/Beklamel liability, as internal liability typically arises in the context of mismanagement that is assessed in hindsight.

**6. Post-WBTR (2021) developments**:
   - The new conflict of interest rules (Art. 2:239 lid 6 BW) mean that a director who should have abstained but voted to approve a transaction may, if that transaction triggers the Beklamel norm, face compounded personal liability — both for the conflict of interest breach and for the Beklamel-triggering decision.
   - Courts are still developing the interaction between the new WBTR provisions and the Beklamel norm.

## Trend / Development

**Trend 1 — Stricter application in SME insolvencies**: Dutch courts have applied the Beklamel norm with increasing rigor in insolvency-related litigation, particularly for SME directors. The combination of Art. 2:10 BW (bookkeeping) and Art. 2:394 BW (annual account filing) failures creating a presumption of improper management (Art. 2:248 BW) means that directors with poor financial record-keeping face a near-automatic Beklamel exposure.

**Trend 2 — WBTR interpretation in progress**: The post-2021 period is characterized by courts beginning to interpret the new WBTR conflict of interest regime. The transition from a representation prohibition to an abstention obligation creates new questions about when conflicts must be disclosed, what happens when the remaining directors approve a conflicted transaction, and how this interacts with director liability.

**Trend 3 — Group structures and Beklamel**: Courts are increasingly willing to look through corporate structures where a group of companies is managed as a single enterprise, applying the Beklamel and SOBI/Hurks doctrines to hold parent company directors liable for subsidiary obligations.

**Trend 4 — Prejudiciele vragen**: The Hoge Raad's prejudiciële vragen procedure (available since 2016 for civil matters) has been used in corporate law disputes. New questions on director liability and Beklamel application in specific contexts may be referred to the Hoge Raad by lower courts, which could produce new binding precedent.

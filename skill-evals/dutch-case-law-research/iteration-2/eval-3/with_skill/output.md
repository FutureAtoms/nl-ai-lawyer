### Leading Case(s)

#### Beklamel Norm - Foundational Hoge Raad Decision

- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AZ0758
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 8 December 2006
- **Case Name**: Ontvanger der Rijksbelastingen v. Roelofsen
- **Legal Issue**: Under what circumstances can a director of a BV be held personally liable in tort (onrechtmatige daad) toward a creditor of the company?
- **Key Holding**: The Hoge Raad established a two-track framework for external director liability (bestuurdersaansprakelijkheid jegens derden):
  1. **Track 1 - Beklamel norm (entering into obligations)**: A director is personally liable if he or she, on behalf of the company, entered into obligations with a third party while knowing - or should reasonably have known - that the company would not be able to perform those obligations and would not offer sufficient recourse for the resulting damages. This is the Beklamel norm.
  2. **Track 2 - Frustration of creditors**: A director who caused or allowed the company to frustrate the satisfaction of existing creditors' claims may also be personally liable.
  - In both cases, a **sufficiently serious personal reproach** (voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt) must be established against the director personally. The mere fact that the company failed to perform is insufficient.
- **Relevant Paragraphs**: r.o. 3.4-3.5
- **Precedential Value**: Binding (Hoge Raad - cassation court)

#### Internal Director Liability - Art. 2:9 BW Framework

- **ECLI**: ECLI:NL:HR:1997:ZC2646
- **Court**: Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date**: 10 January 1997
- **Case Name**: Staleman/Van de Ven
- **Legal Issue**: When is a director internally liable toward the company (interne bestuurdersaansprakelijkheid) under art. 2:9 BW?
- **Key Holding**: Under art. 2:9 BW, a director must properly perform his or her duties toward the company. A director is liable to the company for mismanagement (wanbeleid) if a sufficiently serious personal reproach (voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt) can be made. The Hoge Raad identified relevant factors:
  - (a) The seriousness of the risk the director accepted
  - (b) Whether the director's conduct deviated from what an experienced and prudent director would have done
  - (c) Whether the director sought adequate advice
  - (d) The overall circumstances in which the director acted
- **Precedential Value**: Binding (Hoge Raad); foundational for art. 2:9 BW analysis

## Legal Analysis

### The Current Standard for Bestuurdersaansprakelijkheid under the Beklamel Norm

The Beklamel norm governs **external director liability** - personal liability of a director toward third-party creditors of the company. It is distinct from:
- **Internal liability** under art. 2:9 BW (liability toward the company itself)
- **Insolvency-related liability** under art. 2:248 BW (liability of directors toward the bankruptcy estate for mismanagement)

#### The Three-Part Beklamel Test

Based on the consolidated Hoge Raad jurisprudence, particularly ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AZ0758 (Ontvanger/Roelofsen), a creditor must establish:

**1. The director entered into obligations on behalf of the company**
- The director personally caused or authorized the company to incur specific obligations toward the claimant.
- The mere holding of a director title is insufficient; personal involvement is required.

**2. The director knew or should have known of non-performance**
- At the time the obligation was entered into, the director knew or should reasonably have known (behoorde te begrijpen) that the company would not be able to perform and would not offer sufficient recourse (verhaal).
- This is an **objective standard** at the moment of contracting - it is insufficient if the director only learns of the insolvency later.
- The standard requires financial awareness: a director who fails to monitor the company's financial position may satisfy the "should have known" limb even without actual knowledge.

**3. Sufficiently Serious Personal Reproach (Voldoende Ernstig Persoonlijk Verwijt)**
- The conduct must be attributable personally to the director, not merely to the company.
- This is the critical threshold that distinguishes corporate risk from personal liability.
- Factors include: the nature of the obligation entered into, the company's financial condition at the relevant time, whether the director sought advice, and whether the director was acting in good faith on a reasonable business judgment.

#### Boek 2 BW Framework

Under **Boek 2** (Rechtspersonenrecht), the relevant provisions are:
- **Art. 2:9 BW**: Directors must properly perform their duties (behoorlijke taakvervulling). Internal liability toward the company; requires voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt.
- **Art. 2:10 BW**: Duty to maintain proper accounting records. Failure creates presumption under Art. 2:248 BW.
- **Art. 2:248 BW**: Liability of board members (of NVs and BVs) for apparent mismanagement (kennelijk onbehoorlijk bestuur) preceding bankruptcy, toward the bankruptcy estate.
- **Art. 2:394 BW**: Timely filing of annual accounts. Failure triggers Art. 2:248 BW presumption.

The Beklamel norm operates **outside** Boek 2 - it is a tort claim under Art. 6:162 BW - but it is deeply intertwined with Boek 2 standards of proper management (behoorlijk bestuur).

#### Practical Application of the Beklamel Norm (Recent Developments)

Recent Dutch case law (2019-2024) has refined the Beklamel norm in several directions:

1. **Knowledge at the right moment**: Courts consistently apply the Beklamel norm at the **moment of contracting**, not retroactively. A director who made optimistic projections in good faith may not satisfy the "should have known" test even if the company subsequently failed.

2. **Selective payment / fraudulent preference**: Where a director arranged for preferential payment to certain creditors (typically related parties or shareholders) while knowing the company could not pay others, courts have applied the Beklamel norm and the frustration-of-creditors track from Ontvanger/Roelofsen conjunctively.

3. **Indirect directors (indirect bestuurders)**: Courts have extended the Beklamel norm to persons who were not formally appointed directors but who in practice exercised decisive management control (feitelijk bestuurders / beleidsbepaler, Art. 2:248 lid 7 BW). The Hoge Raad has confirmed that de facto directors fall within the same liability framework.

4. **Multiple directors**: When a board has multiple directors, individual liability depends on each director's own acts and knowledge. Collective board decisions do not automatically create collective personal liability - individual directors who dissented or who had no knowledge of specific transactions may escape personal liability.

5. **Separation of corporate and personal risk**: The Hoge Raad has repeatedly emphasized that the Beklamel norm is an exception to the fundamental principle of corporate limited liability. Courts apply the "voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt" threshold strictly to prevent excessive chilling of legitimate entrepreneurial risk-taking.

## Trend / Development

The observable trend in Dutch jurisprudence on bestuurdersaansprakelijkheid and the Beklamel norm over the past five years:

1. **High threshold maintained**: Dutch courts have consistently maintained the high threshold for director liability, reflecting the policy interest in not deterring risk-taking by directors in commercially uncertain situations. The Hoge Raad has rejected attempts to water down the "voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt" requirement.

2. **Growing attention to financial monitoring duties**: Courts increasingly scrutinize whether directors adequately monitored the company's financial position. A director who was "deliberately ignorant" of the company's financial deterioration may satisfy the Beklamel "should have known" limb.

3. **COVID-19 context (2020-2022)**: Several lower court decisions addressed whether directors who continued trading during the COVID-19 pandemic could be held personally liable under the Beklamel norm. Courts generally applied a more lenient standard where the company's financial difficulties were caused by extraordinary and unforeseeable external events (overmacht), recognizing that directors were operating in exceptional uncertainty.

4. **Art. 2:9 BW in supervised entities**: For directors of regulated financial institutions, healthcare organizations, and public utilities, sector-specific supervision creates a higher standard of care, and courts have been less lenient in applying the voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt threshold.

5. **Supervisory board liability (RvC)**: Recent case law has addressed liability of supervisory board members (commissarissen / Raad van Commissarissen) under Art. 2:9 BW analogously. The Hoge Raad confirmed that supervisory directors have a distinct oversight role and cannot be held to the same standard as executive directors, but can be liable for failure to exercise proper oversight (toezichthoudersaansprakelijkheid).

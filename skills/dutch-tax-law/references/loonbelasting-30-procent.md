# 30% Ruling (30%-regeling) and Payroll Tax for Incoming Employees

## Legal Basis
- **Art. 31a lid 2 sub e Wet op de loonbelasting 1964 (Wet LB 1964)**: Legal foundation for the 30% ruling
- **Art. 10e-10ej Uitvoeringsbesluit loonbelasting 1965 (UBLB 1965)**: Detailed conditions
- **Art. 10ea-10ef Uitvoeringsregeling loonbelasting 2011 (URLB 2011)**: Implementation rules
- **Besluit voorkoming dubbele belasting 2001**: Relevant for partial non-resident status

---

## Purpose and Mechanism

### What Is the 30% Ruling?
- A tax-free allowance for incoming employees recruited from abroad with specific expertise
- The employer may designate up to **30% of the total gross employment income** (including the allowance) as a tax-free reimbursement for extraterritorial costs (extraterritoriale kosten)
- In practice: the employee's effective taxable base is reduced to 70% of gross salary
- The ruling is technically a deemed reimbursement of extraterritorial expenses (costs of living abroad: double housing, storage, travel, cost of living differences)

### Calculation Example
- Gross salary: EUR 65,867
- 30% tax-free allowance: EUR 19,760
- Taxable salary (loon voor de loonbelasting): EUR 46,107
- The employee pays income tax only on EUR 46,107

---

## Eligibility Conditions

### 1. Specific Expertise (Specifieke deskundigheid)
- The employee must possess expertise that is scarce or not available on the Dutch labor market
- Since 2012, specific expertise is presumed if the salary threshold is met (no separate proof required)
- For scientific researchers at qualifying institutions: no salary threshold applies

### 2. Recruitment from Abroad
- The employee must have been recruited from abroad OR transferred within a multinational group
- The employee must have been living outside the Netherlands before starting Dutch employment
- Prior Dutch employment may disqualify the applicant (subject to exceptions)

### 3. The 150 km Rule (150-kilometerregel)
- In the 24 months prior to the first day of employment in the Netherlands, the employee must have lived at a distance of **more than 150 km from the Dutch border** for at least 16 out of 24 months
- Measured as the shortest distance from the employee's home address to the nearest point on the Dutch border
- This rule excludes many employees from neighboring countries (Belgium, Germany, Luxembourg)

### 4. Salary Thresholds (Inkomensnorm)

#### 2025 Salary Thresholds (Belastingdienst — updated annually on 1 January)

| Category | Minimum Taxable Salary (after 30% reduction) | Equivalent Minimum Gross Salary |
|----------|----------------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Standard** | EUR 46,107 | EUR 65,867 |
| **Under-30 with Master's degree** | EUR 35,048 | EUR 50,069 |
| **Scientific researchers at qualifying institutions** | No threshold | No threshold |
| **Medical specialists in training (AIOS)** | No threshold | No threshold |

⚠️ **2026 thresholds** will be indexed annually (typically ~3-4% increase based on CBS wage index). Check belastingdienst.nl for confirmed 2026 amounts.

#### Corresponding IND Kennismigrant Thresholds (2025 — separate system)

| Category | Monthly Gross (excl. 8% vakantiegeld) | Annual Gross (approx.) |
|----------|---------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Standard (30+) | EUR 5,493 | EUR 65,916 |
| Under-30 | EUR 4,028 | EUR 48,336 |

- The 30% ruling and IND salary thresholds are **independent**: meeting one does not guarantee meeting the other
- For the 30% ruling: the **taxable salary** (after 30% deduction) must meet the Belastingdienst threshold
- For IND: the **full gross salary** (before any 30% deduction) must meet the IND threshold
- Thresholds are indexed annually by different bodies (Belastingdienst vs. IND/CBS)
- "Master's degree" includes Dutch Master's, foreign equivalent (must be recognized by Nuffic/IDW), or doctorate (PhD)
- The under-30 threshold applies based on the employee's age at the start of the first day of employment

## Duration

### Current Rule (Effective 1 January 2024)
- Maximum duration: **60 months (5 years)** from the first day of employment in the Netherlands
- Changed from the previous maximum of 8 years (96 months)
- The 5-year period includes any time spent in the Netherlands in prior employments

### Percentage Changes (2024-2027 Legislative History)

#### Step-Down Plan — Enacted and Then Reversed
- **Belastingplan 2024** originally enacted a gradual step-down for new rulings from 1 January 2024:
  - First 20 months: 30%, months 21-40: 20%, months 41-60: 10%
- **Belastingplan 2025 (Wet van 18 december 2024, Stb. 2024, 456)** REVERSED this step-down
- The step-down schedule was repealed before it took practical effect

#### Current Law (as of 1 January 2025)
- **2025-2026**: Flat **30%** for the full 60-month duration
- **From 1 January 2027**: Reduced to a flat **27%** for the full 60-month duration (Art. 31a lid 2 sub e Wet LB 1964, as amended by Belastingplan 2025)
- No step-down structure — the percentage is uniform throughout the ruling period

#### Balkenende Norm / Salary Cap (WNT)
- From 2027, the 30% ruling (then 27%) may only be applied up to the WNT norm (Wet normering topinkomens)
- 2025 WNT norm: EUR 233,000 (indicative; adjusted annually)
- Income above the WNT cap: no tax-free reimbursement applies to the excess
- This cap does NOT apply during 2025-2026 (takes effect simultaneously with the 27% rate in 2027)

### Transitional Provisions (Overgangsrecht)

#### 30% → 27% Transition (Belastingplan 2025)
- Rulings granted **before 1 January 2024**: retain 30% for the full remaining duration (no reduction to 27%)
- Rulings granted **from 1 January 2024 through 31 December 2026**: retain 30% for the full 60-month duration
- Rulings granted **from 1 January 2027 onward**: 27% from the start
- The repealed step-down (30%/20%/10%) does NOT apply to any ruling — it was reversed before taking practical effect

#### 5-Year vs. 8-Year Regime
- Employees who had a ruling under the **old 8-year regime** (pre-2019 change):
  - Transitional provisions from the 2019 change may still apply
  - Duration was capped at 5 years with some grandfathering
- Always verify: specific transitional provisions depend on the date the ruling was first granted and the employee's start date in the Netherlands

## Partial Non-Resident Taxpayer Status (Partieel Buitenlands Belastingplichtige)

### Overview (Art. 2.6 Wet IB 2001)
- Employees with a 30% ruling can opt for **partial non-resident taxpayer status**
- This means the employee is treated as:
  - **Resident taxpayer** for Box 1 (employment income, business income, own home)
  - **Non-resident taxpayer** for Box 2 (substantial shareholding) and Box 3 (savings and investments)

### Practical Impact
- **Box 2**: Only Dutch-source substantial shareholding income is taxable (e.g., shares in a Dutch BV). Foreign shareholdings are exempt
- **Box 3**: Only Dutch-sited assets are taxable (Dutch real estate, claims secured by Dutch real estate). Foreign bank accounts, investments, and property are **exempt** from Box 3 taxation
- Very advantageous for employees with significant foreign wealth or investment portfolios

### Important Limitations — Abolition from 2025

- **Abolished for new rulings from 1 January 2025** (Belastingplan 2025, Stb. 2024, 456):
  - Employees who receive their first 30% ruling from 1 January 2025 onward **cannot** opt for partial non-resident status
  - This applies to Box 2 and Box 3 only — Box 1 treatment is unchanged
- **Transitional provision for existing rulings**:
  - Employees who had a 30% ruling granted **before 1 January 2025** retain the right to elect partial non-resident status **through the expiry of their existing ruling**
  - This transitional right applies until the end of the 60-month ruling period (or until the ruling is otherwise terminated)
- The option must be explicitly elected in the annual income tax return (aangifte inkomstenbelasting)
- Does not affect Box 1 taxation — all employment income and eigenwoningforfait remain fully taxable
- **Practical impact**: New expats from 2025 onward with significant foreign investments/wealth lose a major tax advantage. Consider structuring advice before the ruling start date

## Application Process

### Joint Application
- The application is filed jointly by the **employer and employee** with the Belastingdienst
- Filing address: Belastingdienst/Kennis- en Expertisecentrum Buitenland (KEB), Heerlen
- Use form: "Verzoek loonheffingen 30%-regeling"

### Required Documentation
1. Signed employment contract (arbeidsovereenkomst)
2. Proof of employee's prior residence abroad (e.g., deregistration from foreign municipality, utility bills, rental contract)
3. Copy of employee's passport/ID
4. Copy of employee's diploma(s) if claiming the under-30 Master's threshold
5. Credential evaluation by Nuffic/IDW if foreign degree
6. Description of the employee's specific expertise and role

### Timeline
- Application should be filed within **4 months** of the start of employment
- If filed within 4 months: ruling applies retroactively from the first day of employment
- If filed after 4 months: ruling applies from the first day of the month following the application
- Processing time: typically 2-4 months

### Employer Obligations
- The employer applies the 30% ruling through the payroll (loonaangifte)
- Must be processed as a separate component: eindheffingsbestanddeel
- The employer must verify ongoing compliance (e.g., salary threshold continues to be met)
- If the employee changes employers: a new application must be filed. The total 5-year period is not reset

## Interaction with Immigration Law

### Kennismigrant (Highly Skilled Migrant) - Parallel System
- Non-EU/EEA employees typically need a **kennismigrantvergunning** (highly skilled migrant residence permit)
- The IND (Immigratie- en Naturalisatiedienst) salary thresholds differ from the 30% ruling thresholds:

#### IND Kennismigrant Salary Thresholds (2025 — verify annually at ind.nl)
| Category | Monthly Gross Salary (excl. 8% vacation allowance) |
|----------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Standard (30+) | EUR 5,493 |
| Under-30 | EUR 4,028 |
| Orientation year (zoekjaar) | EUR 2,887 |
| Blue Card (Europese Blauwe Kaart) | EUR 6,435 |

- These are **gross salary** thresholds (not reduced by 30% ruling)
- An employee can qualify for BOTH the kennismigrant permit AND the 30% ruling, but the thresholds are independent
- Losing the 30% ruling does NOT affect the residence permit (and vice versa), provided salary thresholds for each scheme are independently met
- ⚠️ 2026 thresholds will be published by IND in Q4 2025 (typically ~3-4% increase)

### EU/EEA Nationals
- No work permit or residence permit required (freedom of movement)
- Can still qualify for the 30% ruling if all other conditions are met

## US and UK Citizen Special Considerations

### US Citizens (Worldwide Taxation)
- The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of residence
- US citizens working in the Netherlands face **double taxation risk**
- The **Netherlands-US Tax Treaty (Belastingverdrag)** provides relief mechanisms:
  - Tax credits in the US for Dutch taxes paid
  - Exemption method for certain income categories
- The 30% ruling interacts with the treaty:
  - The non-taxable 30% portion reduces Dutch tax, but the US may still tax the full gross amount
  - The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE, IRC Sec. 911) may be claimed in the US but cannot be combined with the foreign tax credit for the same income
  - Professional tax advice essential for US citizens

### UK Citizens (Post-Brexit)
- Since Brexit (1 January 2021): UK nationals need work permits (kennismigrant or other)
- The **Netherlands-UK Tax Treaty** provides relief for double taxation
- UK has a remittance basis option for non-domiciled residents, but this does not affect Dutch tax obligations
- Potential interaction with UK State Pension and Dutch AOW

### General Treaty Considerations
- The 30% ruling reduces the Dutch tax base, potentially reducing available foreign tax credits in the home country
- Tax treaty benefits and the 30% ruling are separate systems - both can apply simultaneously
- The Totalization Agreement (see below) addresses social security, not income tax

## Social Security Implications

### Dutch Social Security for Incoming Employees
- Employees working in the Netherlands are generally insured under Dutch social security (volksverzekeringen: AOW, Anw, Wlz, and werknemersverzekeringen: WW, WIA, ZW)
- Social security contributions (premies volksverzekeringen) are included in the loonheffing (payroll tax + social security combined)
- The 30% ruling does NOT reduce the base for social security contributions (premies are calculated on the unreduced salary)

### Totalization Agreements (Verdragen inzake sociale zekerheid)
- The Netherlands has bilateral social security agreements with various countries to prevent double contributions:
  - **EU/EEA**: Regulation 883/2004 coordinates social security
  - **US**: Totalization Agreement (Verdrag inzake sociale zekerheid) since 1990
  - **UK**: Post-Brexit Trade and Cooperation Agreement preserves social security coordination
  - **Other countries**: bilateral agreements with Japan, South Korea, Canada, etc.
- Under these agreements: an employee is generally insured in only one country at a time
- A **Certificate of Coverage (detacheringsverklaring / A1 certificate in EU)** may exempt the employee from Dutch social security if they remain insured in their home country (typically for posted workers up to 2 years)

### Practical Impact
- Incoming employees covered under Dutch social security build up AOW pension rights (2% per year of insurance)
- They may also contribute to Dutch healthcare (Zorgverzekeringswet - mandatory health insurance)
- Private pension: the 30% ruling does not affect employer pension schemes, but interaction with cross-border pension taxation should be analyzed

## Practical Guidance

### For Employers
- Apply for the 30% ruling proactively (within 4 months of start date)
- Maintain documentation: proof of recruitment from abroad, salary records, residence history
- Monitor salary thresholds annually (indexed amounts change each year)
- If the employee's salary drops below the threshold: the ruling is revoked
- Consider the employer cost implications: the 30% allowance is included in the gross salary for calculating employer social security contributions

### For Employees
- Retain proof of prior foreign residence (at least 150 km from Dutch border)
- File the annual income tax return to elect partieel buitenlands belastingplichtige status (if still available)
- Plan for the expiration of the ruling: tax burden increases significantly after 5 years
- Consider voluntary pension contributions to manage post-ruling tax impact
- If changing employers: file a new application immediately to avoid gaps

### Common Pitfalls
- Missing the 4-month application deadline: ruling cannot be applied retroactively to the start date
- Assuming the 30% applies to ALL income: it only applies to employment income from the qualifying employer
- Failing to meet the 150 km rule: employees from Belgium or western Germany often do not qualify
- Not monitoring legislative changes: the 30% ruling framework changes frequently
- Double social security: failure to obtain a Certificate of Coverage when applicable
- Ignoring home-country tax obligations (especially US citizens)

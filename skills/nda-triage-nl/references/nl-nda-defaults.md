# Standard NDA Positions Under Dutch Law

## Overview

This reference covers the default legal positions, market standards, and common practices
for NDAs (geheimhoudingsovereenkomsten) governed by Dutch law. NDAs in the Netherlands are
governed primarily by general contract law (BW Boek 6) with supplementary protection from
the Wet bescherming bedrijfsgeheimen (Wbb, Dutch Trade Secrets Act).

## Mutual vs. Unilateral NDAs

### Mutual NDA (Wederzijdse geheimhoudingsovereenkomst)
- Both parties disclose and receive confidential information
- Obligations are reciprocal and symmetrical
- Most common in: business negotiations, potential partnerships, joint ventures, due diligence
- Default presumption: both parties have equal bargaining power
- Key drafting point: ensure the definition of confidential information works for both parties

### Unilateral NDA (Eenzijdige geheimhoudingsovereenkomst)
- One party (discloser/verstrekker) discloses; the other (recipient/ontvanger) receives
- Obligations run in one direction only
- Most common in: employer-employee relations, consultant engagements, supplier evaluations
- Key drafting point: the recipient typically has more onerous obligations
- Consider: whether the recipient might inadvertently disclose information to the discloser
  that should also be protected (argues for mutual NDA instead)

## Standard Term and Duration

### Agreement Term (Looptijd overeenkomst)
- **Market standard**: 1-3 years for the period during which information is exchanged
- Common structures:
  - Fixed term with automatic renewal
  - Linked to the duration of an underlying business relationship
  - Indefinite with termination notice period (typically 1-3 months)

### Confidentiality Obligation Duration (Duur geheimhoudingsverplichting)
- **Market standard**: 2-3 years after termination of the agreement or last disclosure
- **Trade secrets**: often indefinite or "for as long as the information qualifies as a
  trade secret" (aligned with the Wbb, which provides protection without a time limit)
- **Ordinary confidential information**: 2-5 years is typical; longer periods require
  justification based on the sensitivity of the information
- **Note**: Excessively long or perpetual obligations for non-trade-secret information may
  be moderated by the court under Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW (derogating effect of reasonableness
  and fairness) or Art. 6:258 BW (unforeseen circumstances)

## Penalty Clause Norms (Boetebeding)

### Legal Framework
- **Art. 6:91 BW** - A penalty clause is an ancillary provision whereby the debtor commits
  to a performance (usually payment of a sum) in case of non-performance or improper performance
- **Art. 6:92 BW** - Default rules (all may be contractually modified):
  - Lid 1: creditor cannot claim both performance AND the penalty
  - Lid 2: the penalty replaces supplementary damages (schadevergoeding)
  - Lid 3: no penalty is due if non-performance is not attributable to the debtor
- **Art. 6:93 BW** - Penalty is generally not due until an ingebrekestelling (notice of default)
  has been served, unless an exception to Art. 6:82 applies
- **Art. 6:94 BW** - Rechterlijke matiging: the court may reduce the penalty if fairness
  clearly so requires ("indien de billijkheid dit klaarblijkelijk eist")

### Market Standard Penalty Amounts

| Company Size / Risk | Per Breach | Per Day Continuing | Typical Cap |
|---------------------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| SME / Low risk | EUR 5,000-15,000 | EUR 500-1,500 | EUR 100,000-250,000 |
| Mid-market / Medium risk | EUR 10,000-25,000 | EUR 1,000-2,500 | EUR 250,000-500,000 |
| Large / High risk | EUR 25,000-50,000 | EUR 2,500-5,000 | EUR 500,000-1,000,000 |
| Strategic / Critical | EUR 50,000-100,000 | EUR 5,000-10,000 | Case-specific or uncapped |

### Penalty Clause Drafting Considerations

**Common structures:**
1. **Fixed penalty per breach** - "For each breach, the breaching party shall pay EUR X"
2. **Fixed penalty + daily penalty** - "EUR X per breach plus EUR Y for each day the breach
   continues" (most common structure in Dutch practice)
3. **Penalty as minimum damages** - Contractual deviation from Art. 6:92 lid 2 BW allowing
   the creditor to claim the penalty AND actual damages exceeding the penalty amount

**Deviation from Art. 6:92 BW (cumulation clause):**
- Very common in Dutch NDAs to include: "onverminderd het recht van de benadeelde partij om
  volledige schadevergoeding te vorderen" (without prejudice to the right to claim full damages)
- This overrides the default rule that the penalty replaces damages
- Courts accept this contractual deviation but may still moderate under Art. 6:94 BW

**Judicial moderation (Art. 6:94 BW):**
- High threshold: "fairness clearly so requires" (klaarblijkelijk)
- Hoge Raad (HR 27 april 2007, ECLI:NL:HR:2007:AZ6638, Intrahof/Bart Smit):
  moderation should be applied with restraint; the agreed penalty should remain the starting point
- Factors considered: relationship between actual damage and penalty amount, nature of the
  obligation, circumstances of the breach, relative positions of the parties
- In practice: courts are reluctant to moderate penalties in B2B contracts between
  sophisticated parties, but will moderate grossly disproportionate penalties

**Ingebrekestelling requirement (Art. 6:93 BW):**
- Default: a notice of default is required before the penalty becomes due
- Many NDAs contractually waive this requirement by stating that the penalty is "immediately
  due upon breach without any notice of default being required" (zonder dat een
  ingebrekestelling vereist is)
- This waiver is generally enforceable under Dutch law

## Standard Carve-Outs (Uitzonderingen)

### The Four Standard Exceptions

Every well-drafted NDA under Dutch law should include these four carve-outs from the
confidentiality obligation:

**1. Public Domain (Openbaar domein)**
- Information that is or becomes publicly available through no fault of the receiving party
- Drafting: "informatie die op het moment van verstrekking reeds algemeen bekend was of
  nadien algemeen bekend is geworden, zonder dat dit het gevolg is van een handelen of
  nalaten van de ontvangende partij"
- Note: partial public availability does not make a compilation public

**2. Prior Knowledge (Voorkennis)**
- Information already known to the receiving party at the time of disclosure
- Drafting: should require the receiving party to demonstrate prior knowledge through
  written records predating the disclosure
- Often combined with "independently developed" (see below)

**3. Independent Development (Onafhankelijke ontwikkeling)**
- Information independently developed by the receiving party without use of the confidential
  information
- Drafting: receiving party bears the burden of proof; should maintain contemporaneous records
- Important in technology and R&D contexts

**4. Third-Party Disclosure (Derdenverkrijging)**
- Information received from a third party who was not under an obligation of confidentiality
  with respect to the information
- Drafting: receiving party should verify the third party's right to disclose
- Due diligence obligation on the receiving party may apply

### Legally Compelled Disclosure (Wettelijke verplichting)

**Not technically a carve-out but an essential provision:**
- Obligation to disclose pursuant to a court order, regulatory requirement, or statutory
  duty (e.g., Wwft anti-money laundering, AFM/DNB regulatory reporting, tax authorities)
- Standard clause: receiving party must (1) notify the disclosing party promptly (if legally
  permitted), (2) limit disclosure to the minimum required, (3) cooperate to obtain
  protective measures (e.g., confidentiality order)
- Dutch law examples of legally compelled disclosure:
  - Art. 843a Rv (document production in litigation - exhibitieplicht)
  - Wwft (anti-money laundering reporting to FIU)
  - Tax authority information requests (AWR)
  - AFM/DNB supervisory powers (Wft)
  - Parliamentary inquiry (Wet op de parlementaire enquete)

### Residual Knowledge Clause (Restkennis-clausule)

- Permits the receiving party to use general knowledge, skills, and experience retained in
  the unaided memory of its personnel after the NDA period
- **Not standard** in most Dutch NDAs but increasingly seen in technology transactions
- Provides practical recognition that individuals cannot "unlearn" information
- Drafting requires careful limitation: does not authorize deliberate memorization or
  reproduction of specific confidential documents or data
- Dutch courts have not extensively addressed residual knowledge clauses specifically, but
  the principle aligns with the Wbb's limitation to "trade secrets" as specifically defined

## Non-Solicitation in NDAs (Non-wervingsbeding)

### Standard Position
- Non-solicitation clauses are **not standard** in standalone NDAs under Dutch law
- When included, they should be assessed for proportionality and reasonableness

### When Appropriate
- Pre-M&A or pre-investment due diligence (protection against cherry-picking key employees)
- Joint ventures where employees of both parties work together
- Consulting engagements where the consultant has access to client's team

### Market Norms
- **Scope**: Active solicitation only (not passive: responding to job applications)
- **Duration**: 6-12 months (in line with employment non-compete norms)
- **Covered persons**: Only employees directly involved in the project/disclosure
- **Separate penalty**: Often a separate (additional) penalty clause for non-solicitation breach
- **Proportionality**: Must be proportionate to the legitimate interest; overly broad clauses
  risk being set aside under Art. 6:248 lid 2 BW

### Distinction from Employment Non-Compete (Concurrentiebeding)
- Employment non-competes are governed by Art. 7:653 BW (strict formal requirements)
- NDA non-solicitation between businesses is governed by general contract law
- The employment law restrictions (writing requirement, adequate explanation for fixed-term
  contracts) do not apply to B2B non-solicitation clauses

## Choice of Law and Dispute Resolution

### Default: Dutch Law
- When both parties are Dutch or the main subject matter is in the Netherlands, Dutch law
  is the standard choice
- Explicitly specifying Dutch law is recommended to avoid uncertainty
- "Op deze overeenkomst is Nederlands recht van toepassing"

### Choice of Law Rules (Rome I Regulation)
- EU Regulation 593/2008 governs choice of law for contractual obligations
- Parties are free to choose the governing law (Art. 3 Rome I)
- Without a choice: the law of the country of habitual residence of the party performing
  the characteristic obligation (Art. 4 Rome I) - for NDAs this is often debated

### Dispute Resolution Options

**1. Dutch State Courts (Overheidsrechter)**
- Default if no arbitration clause
- Forum selection: typically the court in the district of the defendant's registered office,
  or a chosen competent court (forumkeuze)
- Advantages: lower cost, established precedent, right of appeal, interim measures readily
  available (kort geding)
- Standard clause: "De rechtbank [district] is bij uitsluiting bevoegd"

**2. Arbitration (Arbitrage)**
- NAI (Nederlands Arbitrage Instituut) - general commercial arbitration
- SGOA (Stichting Geschillenoplossing Automatisering) - IT and technology disputes
- ICC, LCIA for international transactions
- Advantages: confidentiality, specialist arbitrators, international enforceability (New York
  Convention)
- Disadvantage: higher costs, less accessible for smaller disputes

**3. Mediation followed by Arbitration/Litigation**
- Escalation clause: mandatory mediation before litigation/arbitration
- Growing practice in Dutch commercial contracts
- MfN (Mediatorsfederatie Nederland) mediation rules commonly referenced

### Interim Measures (Voorlopige voorzieningen)

**Kort geding (summary proceedings):**
- Available for urgent NDA disputes (e.g., imminent disclosure of confidential information)
- Can be obtained within days (sometimes hours in extreme cases)
- Court can order: prohibition of disclosure, seizure of documents, penalty for non-compliance
- Art. 254 Rv: voorzieningenrechter has broad powers in kort geding
- Important: even with an arbitration clause, Dutch courts retain jurisdiction for kort geding
  unless the arbitration clause explicitly and validly excludes it

## Haviltex Interpretation Standard

### Application to NDAs

The Haviltex standard (HR 13 maart 1981, NJ 1981/635) governs interpretation of NDA provisions:

> "De vraag hoe in een schriftelijk contract de verhouding van partijen is geregeld, kan niet
> worden beantwoord op grond van alleen maar een zuiver taalkundige uitleg van de bepalingen
> van dat contract. Voor de beantwoording van die vraag komt het immers aan op de zin die
> partijen in de gegeven omstandigheden over en weer redelijkerwijs aan deze bepalingen mochten
> toekennen en op hetgeen zij te dien aanzien redelijkerwijs van elkaar mochten verwachten."

Translation: The question of how a written contract governs the relationship between parties
cannot be answered solely on the basis of a purely linguistic interpretation. What matters is
the meaning that the parties could reasonably attribute to the provisions in the given
circumstances and what they could reasonably expect from each other.

### Practical Implications for NDA Interpretation
- Ambiguous confidentiality definitions will be interpreted considering the parties' intent
  and the context of the business relationship
- Pre-contractual negotiations, correspondence, and the purpose of the NDA are relevant
- Industry practice and customary usage may inform interpretation
- For penalty clauses: courts may look beyond literal text to assess whether the penalty was
  intended to cover the specific type of breach at issue
- Standard-form NDAs (used by one party for all counterparties) may be interpreted more
  objectively, approaching the CAO-norm (objective linguistic interpretation)

## Wet Bescherming Bedrijfsgeheimen (Wbb) - Dutch Trade Secrets Act

### Relationship to NDA
- The Wbb (implementing EU Directive 2016/943) provides statutory protection for trade secrets
  independent of any NDA
- An NDA provides **additional** contractual protection that may cover information not
  qualifying as a trade secret under the Wbb
- The Wbb sets a baseline; the NDA can extend protection beyond the statutory minimum

### Trade Secret Requirements (Art. 1 Wbb)
Information qualifies as a trade secret if:
1. It is **secret** - not generally known or readily accessible to the relevant circles
2. It has **commercial value** because it is secret
3. The holder has taken **reasonable steps** to keep it secret

### Practical Overlap
- Information protected by both NDA and Wbb: beneficiary can choose basis for enforcement
- Information protected by NDA only (not meeting Wbb criteria): purely contractual protection;
  enforcement via breach of contract (wanprestatie, Art. 6:74 BW)
- Information protected by Wbb only (no NDA): statutory protection; enforcement via Wbb
  procedures (Art. 5-8 Wbb, including interim measures and damages)

### Wbb Remedies (Art. 5-8 Wbb)
- Prohibition of use or disclosure (verbod op gebruik of openbaarmaking)
- Recall and destruction of infringing goods
- Damages (schadevergoeding)
- Publication of the judgment
- Preservation of confidentiality during proceedings (Art. 1019ib Rv - processuele
  geheimhouding, allowing restricted access to evidence containing trade secrets)

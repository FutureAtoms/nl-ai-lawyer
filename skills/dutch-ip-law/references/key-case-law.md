# Key Case Law - Dutch Intellectual Property Law

> This file contains landmark court decisions on Dutch and European IP law, covering
> copyright (auteursrecht), trademarks (merkenrecht), patents (octrooirecht), and
> related rights. Includes Dutch Supreme Court, CJEU, and Benelux Court decisions.
> All ECLI numbers should be verified against [Rechtspraak.nl](https://uitspraken.rechtspraak.nl).

---

## 1. Copyright - Originality (Auteursrecht / Werkbegrip)

### HR 30 May 2008, ECLI:NL:HR:2008:BC2153 (Endstra Tapes)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 30 May 2008
- **Parties:** Heirs of Willem Endstra v. Uitgeverij Nieuw Amsterdam and others
- **Key Legal Principle:** For a work to be protected by copyright under the
  Auteurswet, it must be the *eigen intellectuele schepping van de maker* (own
  intellectual creation of the author). The work must bear the *persoonlijk stempel
  van de maker* (personal stamp of the author). Mere labor, skill, or investment
  (*sweat of the brow*) is insufficient. There must be creative choices that give
  the work its original character.
- **Relevance:** This is the leading Dutch case on the originality threshold for
  copyright protection. It aligned Dutch law with the CJEU's later Infopaq standard
  and rejected the British *skill and labour* doctrine. Essential for assessing
  whether any work, including software, databases, and AI-generated content,
  qualifies for copyright protection.

### HR 22 February 2013, ECLI:NL:HR:2013:BY1529 (Stokke/H3 Products - Tripp Trapp)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 22 February 2013
- **Parties:** Stokke A.S. v. H3 Products B.V. (the Tripp Trapp high chair case)
- **Key Legal Principle:** The Hoge Raad confirmed that the Tripp Trapp children's
  chair is a protected work under the Auteurswet. Functional or technically
  determined elements (*technisch bepaalde elementen*) cannot contribute to the
  originality of the work. However, when functional design choices involve creative
  freedom, the resulting form can be protected. The *totaalindruk* (total impression)
  approach applies: infringement is assessed based on the overall impression of
  the original elements.
- **Relevance:** Key case for applied art / industrial design copyright. Establishes
  the boundary between functional and aesthetic elements in design protection.

## 2. Copyright - Infringement (Inbreuk op Auteursrecht)

### HR 12 April 2013, ECLI:NL:HR:2013:BY1532 (Stokke/Fikszo)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 12 April 2013
- **Parties:** Stokke A.S. v. Fikszo B.V. (related Tripp Trapp litigation)
- **Key Legal Principle:** Copyright infringement requires that the allegedly
  infringing work (*ontleend*) was derived from the original work and that the
  protected creative elements have been taken over (*overnemen van auteursrechtelijk
  beschermde trekken*). The test is whether the infringing work, assessed by its
  *totaalindruk* (total impression), shows sufficient similarity to the original
  work in its protected elements. The court must disregard unprotected elements
  (functional, banal, or common elements) in the comparison.
- **Relevance:** The standard test for copyright infringement in Dutch law. The
  *totaalindruk* approach requires a careful decomposition of works into protected
  and unprotected elements before comparing overall impressions.

### HR 29 November 2002, ECLI:NL:HR:2002:AE8456 (Una Voce Particolare)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 29 November 2002
- **Parties:** Related to musical work comparison
- **Key Legal Principle:** In assessing copyright infringement of musical works,
  the overall impression (*totaalindruk*) must be determined by considering all
  elements together (melody, harmony, rhythm, tempo, key). Individual common
  elements are not protected, but their specific combination may be.
- **Relevance:** Important for copyright infringement analysis in creative works
  where individual building blocks are common but combinations may be original.

## 3. CJEU - EU Copyright Harmonization

### CJEU 16 July 2009, C-5/08 (Infopaq International A/S v. Danske Dagblades Forening)

- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Date:** 16 July 2009
- **Parties:** Infopaq International A/S v. Danske Dagblades Forening
- **Key Legal Principle:** The concept of a "work" eligible for copyright protection
  throughout the EU requires *originality* in the sense that the work is the
  *author's own intellectual creation*. This harmonized originality standard applies
  across all EU member states. Even an extract of 11 consecutive words may be
  protected if it reflects the author's own intellectual creation.
- **Relevance:** This is the foundational CJEU decision on the EU-harmonized
  originality standard. It supersedes any divergent national standards and applies
  in the Netherlands via the Auteurswet. The Endstra Tapes decision was already
  aligned with this standard.

### CJEU 2 May 2012, C-406/10 (SAS Institute Inc. v. World Programming Ltd)

- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Date:** 2 May 2012
- **Parties:** SAS Institute Inc. v. World Programming Ltd
- **Key Legal Principle:** The functionality of a computer program, programming
  languages, and data file formats are not protectable by copyright under the
  Software Directive (2009/24/EC). Only the *expression* of a computer program
  (source code, object code) is protected, not the underlying ideas, principles,
  or logic. Interfaces are protectable only to the extent they constitute the
  author's own intellectual creation in their expression.
- **Relevance:** Critical for software copyright disputes. Programming concepts,
  algorithms, and functional specifications are not copyright-protected. Only
  the specific code expression is protected.

### CJEU 22 December 2010, C-393/09 (Bezpecnostni softwarova asociace - BSA)

- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Date:** 22 December 2010
- **Parties:** Bezpecnostni softwarova asociace v. Czech Ministry of Culture
- **Key Legal Principle:** A graphical user interface (GUI) of a software program is
  not protected as a computer program under the Software Directive but may be
  protected as a work under the Information Society Directive (2001/29/EC) if it
  is the author's own intellectual creation.
- **Relevance:** Distinguishes software code protection from GUI/design protection.
  Relevant for disputes involving software look-and-feel claims.

## 4. Trademark Law (Merkenrecht)

### CJEU 11 November 1997, C-251/95 (Sabel BV v. Puma AG)

- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Date:** 11 November 1997
- **Parties:** Sabel BV v. Puma AG, Rudolf Dassler Sport
- **Key Legal Principle:** The assessment of likelihood of confusion (*gevaar voor
  verwarring*) between trademarks must be based on the *overall impression* of the
  marks, taking into account their visual, phonetic, and conceptual similarities.
  A mere conceptual similarity alone is generally insufficient to establish
  likelihood of confusion. The assessment must consider the perspective of the
  *average consumer who is reasonably well-informed, observant, and circumspect*.
- **Relevance:** The standard framework for trademark infringement analysis in all
  Benelux and EU trademark disputes. The *gemiddelde consument* (average consumer)
  standard and the global assessment approach are applied in every trademark case.

### CJEU 22 June 1999, C-342/97 (Lloyd Schuhfabrik Meyer v. Klijsen Handel)

- **Court:** Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Date:** 22 June 1999
- **Parties:** Lloyd Schuhfabrik Meyer & Co. GmbH v. Klijsen Handel BV
- **Key Legal Principle:** In assessing likelihood of confusion, the degree of
  similarity between goods/services and the degree of similarity between marks
  are interdependent (*verwarringsgevaar globale beoordeling*). A lesser degree
  of similarity between goods may be offset by a greater degree of similarity
  between marks, and vice versa. The distinctive character of the earlier mark
  (inherent or acquired through use) is a relevant factor.
- **Relevance:** Establishes the *interdependence principle* for likelihood of
  confusion assessments. Applied consistently in Benelux trademark disputes.

### Hof Den Haag 26 March 2019, ECLI:NL:GHDHA:2019:594 (Trademark dilution)

- **Court:** Gerechtshof Den Haag
- **Date:** 26 March 2019
- **Key Legal Principle:** Protection of well-known marks (*bekend merk*) extends
  beyond identical or similar goods/services under Art. 2.20 lid 1 sub c BVIE
  (Benelux Convention on Intellectual Property). The mark holder must demonstrate:
  (1) the mark has a reputation, (2) the use creates a link in the mind of the
  public, and (3) the use takes unfair advantage of or is detrimental to the
  distinctive character or repute of the mark.
- **Relevance:** Relevant for well-known brand protection in the Benelux. The
  threshold for reputation and detriment is applied consistently with CJEU
  guidance (Intel, C-252/07; L'Oreal, C-487/07).

## 5. Software Copyright and Employer IP Ownership

### Art. 7 Auteurswet - Werkgeversauteursrecht

- **Statutory Rule:** If a work is created by an employee in the course of their
  employment (*dienstverband*), the employer is considered the author (maker) under
  Art. 7 Auteurswet, unless otherwise agreed.
- **Key Points:**
  1. The creation must be within the scope of the employment duties.
  2. This applies automatically; no assignment is needed.
  3. Freelancers and contractors are NOT covered by Art. 7; a separate assignment
     (*akte van overdracht*) is required under Art. 2 Auteurswet.

### Art. 8 Auteurswet - Corporate Authorship

- **Statutory Rule:** If a legal entity publicly discloses a work as its own without
  naming a natural person as author, the legal entity is considered the author.

### HR 1 June 1990, ECLI:NL:HR:1990:ZC8537 (Kluwer Academic Publishers)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 1 June 1990
- **Parties:** Kluwer Academic Publishers v. author
- **Key Legal Principle:** Art. 7 Auteurswet requires that the creation of the work
  falls within the employee's normal duties or specific instructions. Works created
  outside the scope of employment (e.g., personal projects, hobby work) remain
  with the employee even if created during working hours or using employer resources.
- **Relevance:** Defines the boundary of employer copyright ownership. Important for
  disputes over side projects, open-source contributions, and innovations by employees.

### Software-Specific Provisions

- **Art. 45h-45n Auteurswet** (implementing Software Directive 2009/24/EC):
  - Art. 45h: Rental rights (*verhuurrechten*) for software — author's consent required
    for renting out software. Employer copyright for software is governed by Art. 7 Aw.
  - Art. 45j: Lawful users of software may make copies and adaptations necessary for
    the intended use, including error correction. This right cannot be contractually
    overridden.
  - Art. 45k: The lawful user may make a backup copy if necessary for the intended use.
  - Art. 45m: Decompilation is permitted only for achieving interoperability with
    independently created software and only if the information is not otherwise available.

## 6. Patent Law (Octrooirecht) - Selected Cases

### HR 29 March 2002, ECLI:NL:HR:2002:AD8184 (Van Bentum/Kool)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 29 March 2002
- **Parties:** Van Bentum Recycling B.V. v. J. Kool
- **Key Legal Principle:** Patent claim interpretation under the Rijksoctrooiwet 1995
  (ROW) follows the Protocol on Interpretation of Art. 69 EPC. The claims define the
  scope of protection, but their interpretation is not limited to the strict literal
  meaning. The description and drawings serve as aids to interpretation. A fair
  balance must be struck between the patentee's legitimate protection and legal
  certainty for third parties.
- **Relevance:** The leading Dutch case on patent claim construction, aligning
  Dutch practice with the European Patent Convention framework.

## Notes for AI System

- The originality threshold in Dutch copyright law is the *eigen intellectuele
  schepping* standard (Endstra/Infopaq), not the British *skill and labour* test.
- For software disputes, distinguish between code expression (protected) and
  functionality/algorithms (not protected per SAS Institute).
- Employer ownership of IP is automatic under Art. 7 Auteurswet for employees
  but requires contractual assignment for freelancers and contractors.
- Trademark infringement analysis always requires the global assessment approach
  (visual, phonetic, conceptual) from the perspective of the average consumer.
- For Benelux trademarks, apply the BVIE (Benelux Convention on Intellectual Property).
  For EU trademarks, apply the EUTMR (Regulation 2017/1001). Both follow CJEU guidance.
- AI-generated works present open questions under Dutch copyright law: if no natural
  person made creative choices, the work may not meet the originality threshold.
  This is an evolving area with no definitive Dutch case law yet.

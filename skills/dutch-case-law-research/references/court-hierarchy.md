# Dutch Court Hierarchy (Rechterlijke Organisatie)

## Overview
The Dutch judicial system is organized in three tiers for civil and criminal matters, with a separate administrative law track.

---

## 1. Civil and Criminal Courts

### Tier 1: District Courts (Rechtbanken)
- **Number**: 11 districts
- **Jurisdiction**: First instance for virtually all civil and criminal cases
- **Internal divisions**:
  - **Kantonrechter** (Sub-district judge): Handles cases up to EUR 25,000; employment disputes; rental disputes; consumer matters. Simplified procedure, no mandatory lawyer (advocaat) required
  - **Civiele kamer** (Civil chamber): Cases above EUR 25,000; complex civil matters. Mandatory lawyer representation
  - **Strafkamer** (Criminal chamber): Criminal cases. Meervoudige kamer (3 judges) for serious offenses; politierechter (single judge) for minor offenses
  - **Bestuursrechter** (Administrative judge): Administrative law cases in first instance
- **Seats**: Amsterdam, Den Haag, Gelderland, Limburg, Midden-Nederland, Noord-Holland, Noord-Nederland, Oost-Brabant, Overijssel, Rotterdam, Zeeland-West-Brabant

### Tier 2: Courts of Appeal (Gerechtshoven)
- **Number**: 4 districts
- **Jurisdiction**: Appeals from Rechtbank decisions (both civil and criminal)
- **Full rehearing** (volledig hoger beroep): The Gerechtshof re-examines both facts and law
- **Internal structure**: Multiple chambers (kamers) by subject matter
- **Seats**:
  - Gerechtshof Amsterdam (also Arnhem-Leeuwarden for certain matters)
  - Gerechtshof Arnhem-Leeuwarden
  - Gerechtshof Den Haag (also handles patent appeals)
  - Gerechtshof 's-Hertogenbosch
- **Special jurisdiction**:
  - Gerechtshof Den Haag: Appeals in patent cases
  - Gerechtshof Amsterdam: Ondernemingskamer (Enterprise Chamber) for corporate disputes
  - Gerechtshof Arnhem-Leeuwarden: Land registry (kadaster) disputes

### Tier 3: Supreme Court (Hoge Raad der Nederlanden)
- **Location**: The Hague
- **Jurisdiction**: Cassation (cassatie) only - reviews legal questions, not facts
- **Grounds for cassation** (Art. 79 Wet RO):
  - Violation of law (schending van het recht)
  - Failure to comply with essential procedural requirements (verzuim van vormen)
- **No factual review**: The Hoge Raad accepts the facts as established by the Gerechtshof
- **Composition**: President, vice-presidents, and raadsheren; decisions by chambers of 3 or 5
- **Advocate General** (Procureur-Generaal): Provides independent advisory opinions (conclusies) before the Hoge Raad decides
- **Prejudiciele vragen**: Lower courts may refer preliminary questions of law to the Hoge Raad (since 2016)
- **Art. 81 RO**: The Hoge Raad may dismiss a cassation appeal without reasoning if the complaint does not lead to cassation and does not require a decision for the development of the law

## 2. Administrative Courts (Bestuursrechtspraak)

### First Instance
- **Rechtbank** (Bestuursrechter): First instance for most administrative disputes
- Subject to prior objection procedure (bezwaarprocedure) with the administrative body

### Appeal
Three separate highest administrative courts:

| Court | Abbreviation | Jurisdiction |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Afdeling Bestuursrechtspraak van de Raad van State (ABRvS) | RVS | General administrative law (spatial planning, environmental, immigration, etc.) |
| Centrale Raad van Beroep (CRvB) | CRVB | Social security law, civil servant law |
| College van Beroep voor het bedrijfsleven (CBb) | CBB | Economic administrative law (competition, telecom, financial regulation) |

**Note**: There is NO cassation to the Hoge Raad for administrative cases (except tax law).

### Tax Courts
- **Rechtbank**: First instance
- **Gerechtshof**: Appeal
- **Hoge Raad**: Cassation (the Hoge Raad IS the highest tax court)

## 3. Precedential Value (Precedentwerking)

### Binding precedent
- The Netherlands does not have a formal doctrine of binding precedent (stare decisis) as in common law systems
- However, in practice:
  - **Hoge Raad decisions** are effectively binding on all lower courts; deviation is extremely rare and will typically lead to cassation
  - **Published Hoge Raad decisions** form the backbone of Dutch legal interpretation

### Persuasive authority
- **Gerechtshof decisions**: Highly persuasive for Rechtbanken within the same appeal district
- **Rechtbank decisions**: Persuasive between Rechtbanken, especially from specialized chambers
- **Advocate General conclusions**: Not binding but highly influential; often cited by scholars and practitioners

### Special weight:
- **Ondernemingskamer** (Enterprise Chamber) decisions on corporate governance
- **Voorzieningenrechter** (preliminary relief judge) decisions establish interim standards
- **Prejudiciele vragen** answers by the Hoge Raad

---

## 4. Key Procedural Features

### Civil Procedure
- **Dagvaardingsprocedure**: Summons procedure (default for claims for performance/damages)
- **Verzoekschriftprocedure**: Petition procedure (used for specific statutory matters like bankruptcy, family law)
- **Kort geding**: Summary proceedings before the voorzieningenrechter (preliminary relief)
- **Bodemprocedure**: Proceedings on the merits

### Time Limits for Appeals
| Stage | Time Limit |
|-------|-----------|
| Hoger beroep (civil) | 3 months from judgment |
| Hoger beroep (criminal) | 14 days from judgment |
| Cassatie | 3 months from Gerechtshof judgment |
| Bestuursrecht: bezwaar | 6 weeks from decision |
| Bestuursrecht: beroep | 6 weeks from bezwaar decision |
| Bestuursrecht: hoger beroep | 6 weeks from Rechtbank judgment |

### Mandatory Legal Representation
- **Kantonrechter**: No mandatory lawyer (advocaat)
- **Rechtbank (civil chamber)**: Mandatory lawyer
- **Gerechtshof**: Mandatory lawyer
- **Hoge Raad**: Mandatory cassation lawyer (cassatieadvocaat)
- **Bestuursrechter**: Generally no mandatory lawyer (except certain tax and immigration cases)

# Landmark Cases - Dutch Case Law Research

> This file contains foundational Dutch court decisions that form the backbone of
> Dutch private law. These cases are frequently cited across multiple legal domains
> and serve as essential reference points for case law research.
> All ECLI numbers should be verified against the live [Rechtspraak.nl API](https://data.rechtspraak.nl).

---

## 1. Tort Law (Onrechtmatige Daad)

### HR 31 January 1919, ECLI:NL:HR:1919:AG1776 (Lindenbaum/Cohen)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 31 January 1919
- **Parties:** Lindenbaum v. Cohen
- **Key Legal Principle:** An unlawful act (*onrechtmatige daad*) is not limited to
  violations of statutory law, but also includes acts that violate unwritten norms
  of proper social conduct (*maatschappelijke betamelijkheid*). This decision
  expanded the definition of tort beyond the narrow statutory violation standard
  that prevailed under the old BW.
- **Relevance:** This is the most foundational case in Dutch tort law. It established
  the broad scope of Art. 6:162 BW (successor provision) and remains the starting
  point for any tort analysis. Every tort claim must be assessed against the three
  categories: (1) violation of a right, (2) act or omission violating a statutory
  duty, (3) act or omission violating unwritten social norms.

### HR 5 November 1965, ECLI:NL:HR:1965:AB7079 (Kelderluik)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 5 November 1965
- **Parties:** Sjouwerman v. Coca-Cola Export Corporation
- **Key Legal Principle:** Established the criteria for determining whether a party
  has violated its duty of care (*zorgvuldigheidsnorm*) by creating a dangerous
  situation. The four Kelderluik criteria are:
  1. How likely is it that others will fail to exercise proper care (*kans op onoplettendheid*)?
  2. How likely is it that this will lead to accidents (*kans op ongevallen*)?
  3. How serious can the consequences be (*ernst van de gevolgen*)?
  4. How burdensome are the precautionary measures (*bezwaarlijkheid van voorzorgsmaatregelen*)?
- **Relevance:** These criteria are the Dutch equivalent of the Learned Hand formula
  and are applied in virtually every negligence case. Essential for assessing duty
  of care in tort, product liability, and professional negligence matters.

## 2. Contract Law (Verbintenissenrecht)

### HR 13 March 1981, ECLI:NL:HR:1981:AG4158 (Haviltex)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 13 March 1981
- **Parties:** Ermes c.s. v. Haviltex
- **Key Legal Principle:** The meaning of a contractual clause cannot be determined
  by a purely linguistic interpretation but must be assessed based on the meaning
  the parties could reasonably attribute to it under the circumstances, and what
  they could reasonably expect from each other. This includes consideration of
  the social context, the parties' knowledge, and their expressed intentions.
- **Relevance:** The single most important case for Dutch contract interpretation.
  The Haviltex norm is the default standard for all contracts between private parties.
  See also DSM/Fox (ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AO1427) for the objective counterpart applicable
  to standardized contracts.

## 3. Corporate Law / Director Liability

### HR 8 December 2006, ECLI:NL:HR:2006:AZ0758 (Ontvanger/Roelofsen)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 8 December 2006
- **Parties:** De Ontvanger der Rijksbelastingen v. Roelofsen
- **Key Legal Principle:** A director of a legal entity can be personally liable
  in tort (Art. 6:162 BW) toward a creditor of the company if the director caused
  or allowed the company to default on its obligations, provided that a *sufficiently
  serious personal reproach* (*voldoende ernstig persoonlijk verwijt*) can be made.
  Two categories are distinguished:
  1. The director caused the company to enter into obligations while knowing it
     could not perform (*Beklamel norm*).
  2. The director caused or allowed the company to frustrate the satisfaction of
     its creditors' claims.
- **Relevance:** This is the leading case on external director liability (toward
  third-party creditors). Must be considered in any corporate litigation involving
  unpaid debts and potential director misconduct.

### HR 6 February 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AN8071 (Cancun)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 6 February 2004
- **Parties:** Cancun Holding I B.V. v. various parties
- **Key Legal Principle:** The corporate interest (*vennootschappelijk belang*) of
  a BV or NV is primarily determined by the continuity of the enterprise and the
  interests of all stakeholders involved (*bestendige succes van de onderneming*).
  Directors must act in the corporate interest, which is not identical to the
  shareholders' interest alone.
- **Relevance:** Foundational for understanding the duties of directors under Dutch
  corporate law. Directors who prioritize shareholder interests at the expense of
  creditors, employees, or the enterprise itself may breach their fiduciary duties.

### HR 21 December 2001, ECLI:NL:HR:2001:AD4499 (SOBI/Hurks)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 21 December 2001
- **Parties:** Stichting SOBI v. Hurks Groep B.V.
- **Key Legal Principle:** A parent company can be liable for the debts of its
  subsidiary if it has created the justified impression that it will ensure the
  subsidiary meets its obligations (*concern liability / concernaansprakelijkheid*).
  This can arise from: (1) intensive involvement in the subsidiary's management,
  (2) public representations of financial support, or (3) abuse of the corporate
  structure.
- **Relevance:** Critical for group liability assessments and intra-group financing
  arrangements. Parent companies must be cautious about the degree of control and
  the representations they make regarding subsidiaries.

### HR 9 July 2010, ECLI:NL:HR:2010:BM0976 (Beklamel)

- **Court:** Hoge Raad der Nederlanden
- **Date:** 9 July 2010
- **Parties:** Commonly referred to as the Beklamel norm (originally from
  HR 6 October 1989, NJ 1990/286)
- **Key Legal Principle:** A director is personally liable if he or she, on behalf
  of the company, entered into obligations with a third party while the director
  knew or should have known that the company would not be able to perform its
  obligations and would not offer sufficient recourse for damages. This is known
  as the *Beklamel norm*.
- **Relevance:** One of the two main grounds for external director liability
  (alongside the frustration of creditors ground from Ontvanger/Roelofsen).
  Frequently invoked in insolvency-related litigation.

## 5. Important Notes on ECLI Verification

> **WARNING:** ECLI numbers cited in legal databases and academic literature may
> contain errors or refer to superseded decisions. Always verify ECLI numbers
> against the live Rechtspraak.nl database before relying on them in legal advice.

### Verification Methods

1. **Rechtspraak.nl Search:** Use `https://uitspraken.rechtspraak.nl` and search
   by ECLI number directly.
2. **Open Data API:** Query `https://data.rechtspraak.nl/uitspraken/content?id=ECLI:NL:HR:YYYY:XXXXX`
   to retrieve the full text of the decision.
3. **LinkedData:** Use `https://linkeddata.overheid.nl/service/get-links?ext-id=ECLI:NL:HR:YYYY:XXXXX`
   to find related legislation and references.

### Known Issues

- Pre-2000 decisions may not have ECLI numbers in the Rechtspraak.nl database;
  use NJ (Nederlandse Jurisprudentie) references as fallback.
- Some ECLI numbers in older literature use reconstructed identifiers that may
  not match the official database entry.
- The Lindenbaum/Cohen ECLI (1919) is a reconstructed identifier and may not
  return results in the API; use NJ 1919, p. 161 as alternative citation.

## Notes for AI System

- When researching case law, always start with these landmark cases as anchor points.
- Cross-reference the ECLI against Rechtspraak.nl before citing in any output.
- For each domain-specific research task, consult the domain-specific key-case-law.md
  files in the relevant skill directories for additional precedents.
- Hoge Raad decisions are the highest authority; Gerechtshof decisions are persuasive
  but not binding on other courts; Rechtbank decisions are first instance only.
- The Ondernemingskamer (Enterprise Chamber) of the Amsterdam Gerechtshof has
  exclusive jurisdiction for enquiry proceedings and is a specialized authority
  in corporate law matters.

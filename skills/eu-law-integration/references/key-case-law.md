## 1. Foundational EU Law Principles Applied by Dutch Courts

### 1.1 CJEU 5 February 1963, Case 26/62 (Van Gend en Loos)
- **Court**: Court of Justice of the European Communities (now CJEU)
- **Date**: 5 February 1963
- **Key principle**: EU law creates direct rights for individuals that national courts must protect (direct effect). Treaty provisions that are clear, unconditional, and do not require further implementation are directly enforceable by individuals in national courts.
- **Relevance**: Originated from a Dutch case (Van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen). The foundation of direct effect doctrine in EU law.

### 1.2 CJEU 15 July 1964, Case 6/64 (Costa v ENEL)
- **Court**: Court of Justice of the European Communities (now CJEU)
- **Date**: 15 July 1964
- **Key principle**: EU law takes precedence (supremacy/voorrang) over conflicting national law, including later national legislation. National courts must set aside (buiten toepassing laten) any national provision that conflicts with directly effective EU law.
- **Relevance**: Dutch courts regularly apply this principle, setting aside conflicting provisions of Dutch law; integrated into Dutch legal practice through Art. 94 Grondwet (which already recognized supremacy of international treaties).

### 1.3 HR 2 november 2004, ECLI:NL:HR:2004:AR1797 (Rusttijden-arrest)
- **Court**: Hoge Raad
- **Date**: 2 November 2004
- **Key principle**: The Hoge Raad explicitly acknowledged the duty to set aside national law that conflicts with directly effective EU law, applying Van Gend en Loos and Costa v ENEL. The court disapplied a Dutch regulation on rest periods for transport workers to the extent it conflicted with an EU directive.
- **Relevance**: Practical demonstration of EU law supremacy by the Dutch Supreme Court.

## 2. Richtlijnconforme Interpretatie (Consistent Interpretation / Directive-Conform Interpretation)

### 2.1 CJEU 13 November 1990, C-106/89 (Marleasing)
- **Court**: Court of Justice of the European Communities (now CJEU)
- **Date**: 13 November 1990
- **Key principle**: National courts are obliged to interpret national law, as far as possible, in conformity with EU directives (richtlijnconforme interpretatie), regardless of whether the national law was adopted before or after the directive. This obligation extends to all national law, not just implementing legislation.
- **Relevance**: Dutch courts regularly apply this principle when interpreting BW provisions, Awb provisions, and sector-specific legislation.

### 2.2 HR 10 augustus 2007, ECLI:NL:HR:2007:AZ3758 (Wandelvierdaagse)
- **Court**: Hoge Raad
- **Date**: 10 August 2007
- **Key principle**: The Hoge Raad applied richtlijnconforme interpretatie of Dutch civil law in light of an EU directive on the liability of organizers of public events. The court interpreted the relevant BW provisions in a manner consistent with the directive's purpose, even though the Dutch legislator had not explicitly implemented the specific provision.
- **Relevance**: Shows the reach of Marleasing in Dutch private law.

### 2.3 HR 19 december 2014, ECLI:NL:HR:2014:3679 (Richtlijnconforme uitleg arbeidsrecht)
- **Court**: Hoge Raad
- **Date**: 19 December 2014
- **Key principle**: The HR interpreted the Dutch Working Conditions Act (Arbeidsomstandighedenwet) in conformity with the EU Framework Directive 89/391/EEC on occupational safety and health. The court held that the Dutch provisions must be read in light of the directive's purpose to ensure effective worker protection.
- **Relevance**: Demonstrates richtlijnconforme interpretatie in employment law.

## 3. Francovich State Liability (Staatsaansprakelijkheid)

### 3.1 CJEU 19 November 1991, C-6/90 and C-9/90 (Francovich)
- **Court**: Court of Justice of the European Communities (now CJEU)
- **Date**: 19 November 1991
- **Key principle**: Member States are liable for damage caused to individuals by failure to transpose an EU directive. Three conditions: (1) the directive grants rights to individuals, (2) the content of those rights is identifiable from the directive, and (3) there is a causal link between the failure to transpose and the damage suffered.
- **Relevance**: The basis for state liability claims against the Staat der Nederlanden under Art. 6:162 BW for failure to implement EU directives.

### 3.2 HR 18 september 2015, ECLI:NL:HR:2015:2722 (Staat/Stichting Mothers of Srebrenica -- partial Francovich analogy)
- **Court**: Hoge Raad
- **Date**: 18 September 2015
- **Key principle**: While primarily an international law case, the HR addressed the framework for state liability under Art. 6:162 BW for acts attributable to the State in an international context. The court's reasoning on the duty of care and foreseeability parallels the Francovich framework for EU law liability claims.
- **Relevance**: Illustrates how Art. 6:162 BW serves as the vehicle for state liability claims, including those grounded in EU law.

### 3.3 Rb. Den Haag 9 februari 2011, ECLI:NL:RBSGR:2011:BP3701
- **Court**: Rechtbank Den Haag (now Rechtbank Den Haag)
- **Date**: 9 February 2011
- **Key principle**: A Francovich-style claim against the Staat der Nederlanden for failure to correctly implement an EU directive on environmental impact assessment (MER-richtlijn). The court assessed whether the incorrect transposition caused the claimant to suffer identifiable damage.
- **Relevance**: Practical example of a Francovich claim proceeding through Dutch civil courts via Art. 6:162 BW.

## 4. Preliminary References by Dutch Courts (Art. 267 TFEU)

### 4.1 HR 16 oktober 2015, ECLI:NL:HR:2015:3099 (Prejudiciele verwijzing -- Uber/taxi)
- **Court**: Hoge Raad
- **Date**: 16 October 2015
- **Key principle**: The HR referred questions to the CJEU on the classification of Uber's services under the E-Commerce Directive and the Services Directive. The referral illustrates the duty of courts of last instance (Art. 267 lid 3 TFEU) to refer unless the answer is acte clair or acte eclaire.
- **Relevance**: Demonstrates the Dutch Supreme Court's engagement with the preliminary reference procedure.

### 4.2 ABRvS 22 februari 2017, ECLI:NL:RVS:2017:487 (Prejudiciele vragen stikstof/PAS)
- **Court**: ABRvS
- **Date**: 22 February 2017
- **Key principle**: The ABRvS referred questions to the CJEU regarding the compatibility of the Dutch Programma Aanpak Stikstof (PAS) with the Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC). The resulting CJEU judgment (C-293/17 and C-294/17, "PAS-uitspraak") invalidated the PAS framework, triggering the Dutch nitrogen crisis.
- **Relevance**: One of the most consequential preliminary references ever made by a Dutch court; led to fundamental changes in Dutch environmental and spatial planning law.

### 4.3 ABRvS 29 mei 2019, ECLI:NL:RVS:2019:1603 (Follow-up PAS -- after CJEU ruling)
- **Court**: ABRvS
- **Date**: 29 May 2019
- **Key principle**: After receiving the CJEU's answer, the ABRvS ruled that the PAS could no longer serve as the basis for granting permits for nitrogen-emitting activities. This follow-up judgment triggered the Dutch "stikstofcrisis" and led to emergency legislation (Spoedwet aanpak stikstof).
- **Relevance**: Illustrates the domestic impact of CJEU preliminary rulings on Dutch law and policy.

## 5. Horizontal Direct Effect / Incidental Horizontal Effect

### 5.1 CJEU 22 November 2005, C-144/04 (Mangold)
- **Court**: Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Date**: 22 November 2005
- **Key principle**: The principle of non-discrimination on grounds of age, as a general principle of EU law, can be invoked in horizontal disputes (between private parties). National courts must set aside conflicting national provisions even in private-law litigation, creating "incidental horizontal effect."
- **Relevance**: Applied by Dutch courts in employment discrimination cases involving age.

### 5.2 CJEU 19 January 2010, C-555/07 (Kucukdeveci)
- **Court**: Court of Justice of the European Union
- **Date**: 19 January 2010
- **Key principle**: Confirmed and reinforced Mangold: Directive 2000/78/EC on equal treatment in employment gives expression to the general principle of non-discrimination on grounds of age, which national courts must apply in horizontal disputes by setting aside conflicting national law.
- **Relevance**: Dutch courts (including Hoge Raad and kantonrechters) apply this when assessing age discrimination in employment relationships.

### 5.3 HR 18 december 2015, ECLI:NL:HR:2015:3628
- **Court**: Hoge Raad
- **Date**: 18 December 2015
- **Key principle**: The HR applied the Mangold/Kucukdeveci doctrine in a Dutch employment dispute, setting aside a provision of Dutch law that was incompatible with the EU prohibition of age discrimination. The court confirmed that the general principle of non-discrimination has incidental horizontal effect in private-law disputes.
- **Relevance**: Direct Dutch application of the EU incidental horizontal effect doctrine.

## 6. Trade Secrets (Wet bescherming bedrijfsgeheimen -- Wbb)

### 6.1 Rb. Den Haag 17 juli 2019, ECLI:NL:RBDHA:2019:7161
- **Court**: Rechtbank Den Haag
- **Date**: 17 July 2019
- **Key principle**: One of the first Dutch court decisions applying the Wet bescherming bedrijfsgeheimen (Wbb), implementing EU Directive 2016/943 on trade secrets. The court assessed whether the claimant's information qualified as a "bedrijfsgeheim" under Art. 1 Wbb (the information must be secret, have commercial value because of its secrecy, and be subject to reasonable protective measures).
- **Relevance**: Sets the standard for trade secret claims under the new Dutch Wbb framework.

### 6.2 Rb. Midden-Nederland 5 februari 2020, ECLI:NL:RBMNE:2020:428
- **Court**: Rechtbank Midden-Nederland
- **Date**: 5 February 2020
- **Key principle**: The requirement of "redelijke maatregelen" (reasonable steps to maintain secrecy) under the Wbb. The court held that a general confidentiality clause in an employment contract, without further technical or organizational measures, may be insufficient to demonstrate that reasonable protective steps were taken.
- **Relevance**: Guides companies on the level of protection required to maintain trade secret status.

### 6.3 Gerechtshof Den Haag 17 november 2020, ECLI:NL:GHDHA:2020:2127
- **Court**: Gerechtshof Den Haag
- **Date**: 17 November 2020
- **Key principle**: Scope of remedies under the Wbb: the court may order cessation of use, recall of infringing products, and damages. The Gerechtshof addressed the interaction between the Wbb remedies and the general tort law framework (Art. 6:162 BW).
- **Relevance**: Clarifies the enforcement toolkit available for trade secret holders.

## 7. Additional Foundational EU Principles in Dutch Practice

### 7.1 CJEU 9 March 1978, Case 106/77 (Simmenthal II)
- **Court**: Court of Justice of the European Communities (now CJEU)
- **Date**: 9 March 1978
- **Key principle**: Every national court must, within its jurisdiction, apply EU law in its entirety and set aside any conflicting national provision, without having to request or await the prior setting aside of that provision by any legislative or other constitutional procedure.
- **Relevance**: Regularly invoked by Dutch courts when disapplying national provisions that conflict with EU law; reinforces Art. 94 Grondwet.

### 7.2 HR 6 maart 2020, ECLI:NL:HR:2020:380 (Urgenda -- climate liability)
- **Court**: Hoge Raad
- **Date**: 6 March 2020
- **Key principle**: While primarily an ECHR case, the Urgenda ruling demonstrates Dutch courts' willingness to enforce international and European obligations (Art. 2 and 8 ECHR) against the State, ordering emission reduction targets. The HR's reasoning reflects the broader integration of European norms into domestic judicial practice.
- **Relevance**: Landmark ruling showing the interplay between European norms and Dutch tort law (Art. 6:162 BW) in holding the State accountable.

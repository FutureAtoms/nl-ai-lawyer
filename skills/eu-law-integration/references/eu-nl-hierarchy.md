# Hierarchy of EU and Dutch Law

## The Dutch Monist System

The Netherlands follows a **monist** approach to international and EU law. Unlike dualist
systems (e.g., the UK, Germany), the Netherlands does not require separate transformation
of international law into domestic law. Treaty provisions that are "binding on all persons"
(een ieder verbindend) have direct internal effect upon publication.

### Constitutional Foundation

**Art. 93 Grondwet (Constitution):**
> "Bepalingen van verdragen en van besluiten van volkenrechtelijke organisaties, die naar
> haar inhoud een ieder kunnen verbinden, hebben verbindende kracht nadat zij zijn
> bekendgemaakt."
>
> Provisions of treaties and of decisions of international organizations which may be
> binding on all persons by virtue of their contents shall have binding force after they
> have been published.

**Art. 94 Grondwet:**
> "Binnen het Koninkrijk geldende wettelijke voorschriften vinden geen toepassing, indien
> deze toepassing niet verenigbaar is met een ieder verbindende bepalingen van verdragen
> en van besluiten van volkenrechtelijke organisaties."
>
> Statutory regulations in force within the Kingdom shall not be applied if such
> application is in conflict with provisions of treaties or of decisions of international
> organizations that are binding on all persons.

**Key implications:**
- Art. 94 enables Dutch courts to set aside conflicting national legislation
- This includes acts of Parliament (wetten in formele zin)
- The Netherlands does not have a constitutional court; all courts exercise this power
- This constitutional framework predates EU membership and was originally designed for
  general international law, but it provides a natural foundation for EU law primacy

## The Doctrine of Direct Effect (Rechtstreekse Werking)

### Origin: Van Gend en Loos (C-26/62, 1963)

The CJEU established that EU law creates rights for individuals that national courts must
protect. A provision has direct effect when it is:
1. **Clear and precise** (duidelijk en nauwkeurig)
2. **Unconditional** (onvoorwaardelijk) - not subject to further measures
3. **Does not leave a margin of discretion** to Member States or EU institutions

### Types of Direct Effect

#### Vertical Direct Effect (Verticale rechtstreekse werking)
- Individuals can invoke EU law provisions against **the State** and emanations of the State
- Applies to: Treaty provisions, regulations, directives (after transposition deadline)
- "State" broadly interpreted: includes municipalities, provinces, public bodies, state-owned
  enterprises (Foster v British Gas, C-188/89)

#### Horizontal Direct Effect (Horizontale rechtstreekse werking)
- Individuals can invoke EU law provisions against **other individuals/private parties**
- Applies to: Treaty provisions (e.g., Art. 157 TFEU equal pay), regulations, general
  principles of EU law
- **Directives do NOT have horizontal direct effect** (Marshall, C-152/84; Faccini Dori, C-91/92)
- But see: incidental horizontal effect via general principles (Mangold, C-144/04; Kucukdeveci, C-555/07)

### Direct Effect by Instrument Type

| EU Instrument | Vertical DE | Horizontal DE | Conditions |
|--------------|-------------|---------------|------------|
| Treaty Articles | Yes | Yes (some) | Clear, precise, unconditional |
| Regulations | Yes | Yes | Directly applicable by definition |
| Directives | Yes (after deadline) | No | Clear, precise, unconditional; only against State |
| Decisions (addressed to MS) | Yes | No | Clear, precise, unconditional |
| General Principles | Yes | Yes (debated) | Mangold/Kucukdeveci line |
| Charter of Fundamental Rights | Yes | Developing | Art. 51: only when implementing EU law |

## The Doctrine of Primacy (Voorrang)

### Origin: Costa v ENEL (C-6/64, 1964)

EU law takes precedence over **all** conflicting national law. Key principles:

1. **Primacy over all national law** - Including constitutional provisions
   (Internationale Handelsgesellschaft, C-11/70)
2. **Obligation to set aside** - National courts must disapply conflicting national law
   of their own motion (Simmenthal, C-106/77)
3. **Applies to later national legislation** - Lex posterior does not apply vis-a-vis EU law
4. **No invalidity of national law** - Conflicting national law is not void; it is simply
   not applied (inapplicability, not nullity)

### Dutch Application of Primacy

Dutch courts apply primacy through Art. 94 Grondwet. Notable Dutch practice:

- **Hoge Raad**: Regularly sets aside Dutch legislation conflicting with EU law
- **Raad van State (ABRvS)**: Applies primacy in administrative law cases, particularly
  immigration and environmental law
- **No Kompetenz-Kompetenz debate**: Unlike Germany (BVerfG Solange, ultra vires review)
  or France (Conseil Constitutionnel), the Netherlands has not engaged in significant
  resistance to EU primacy. The monist constitutional framework accommodates it naturally.

### Practical Example
If a Dutch statute (wet) conflicts with an EU regulation:
1. The Dutch court identifies the conflict
2. The court sets aside the Dutch provision under Art. 94 Gw
3. The EU regulation applies directly
4. The Dutch statute remains on the books but is not applied in that case

## Consistent Interpretation (Richtlijnconforme Interpretatie)

### Origin: Von Colson (C-14/83) and Marleasing (C-106/89)

National courts must interpret national law, as far as possible, in the light of the
wording and purpose of the relevant EU directive.

### Scope and Limits

**Scope:**
- Applies to **all national law**, not just implementing measures (Marleasing)
- Applies even to national law adopted **before** the directive (Marleasing)
- Applies from the date the transposition deadline has passed (Adeneler, C-212/04)
- During the transposition period: duty to refrain from seriously compromising the directive's
  objectives (Inter-Environnement Wallonie, C-129/96)

**Limits:**
- Cannot lead to an interpretation **contra legem** (against the clear wording of national law)
  (Pupino, C-105/03; but the boundary is fact-specific)
- Cannot impose or aggravate criminal liability (Kolpinghuis Nijmegen, C-80/86)
- Cannot create obligations for individuals that the directive does not create

### Dutch Practice (Richtlijnconforme Uitleg)

Dutch courts regularly apply consistent interpretation:
- **Hoge Raad** uses EU directives as interpretive aids even when national law appears clear
- Known as "richtlijnconforme uitleg" or "verdragsconforme uitleg"
- The Haviltex criterion (contractual interpretation) may be supplemented by directive-conforming
  interpretation when the contract relates to an area governed by EU directives
- Administrative courts (ABRvS, CRvB, CBb) actively apply directive-conforming interpretation
  in areas like environmental law, social security, and migration

## State Liability (Staatsaansprakelijkheid / Francovich)

### Origin: Francovich (C-6/90 and C-9/90, 1991)

A Member State that fails to properly implement EU law may be liable to compensate
individuals who suffer loss as a result.

### Conditions (Brasserie du Pecheur/Factortame III, C-46/93 and C-48/93)

Three cumulative conditions:
1. **The rule of EU law infringed confers rights on individuals**
2. **The breach is sufficiently serious** (voldoende gekwalificeerde schending)
   - Factors: clarity of the rule, degree of discretion, excusability of the error,
     whether the infringement was intentional
   - Non-implementation of a directive after the deadline is almost always sufficiently serious
3. **Direct causal link** between the breach and the damage suffered

### Dutch Implementation

- Claims for Francovich liability are brought as tort claims (onrechtmatige daad, Art. 6:162 BW)
  against the State (Staat der Nederlanden)
- The Hoge Raad has confirmed Francovich liability applies in Dutch law
- Claims filed at the Rechtbank Den Haag (exclusive jurisdiction for claims against the State)
- Limitation period: 5 years from the date the injured party became aware of the damage (Art. 3:310 BW)
- The State can also be liable for incorrect application of EU law by courts (Kobler, C-224/01),
  though only in cases of manifest infringement

## Preliminary Reference Procedure (Prejudiciele Verwijzing, Art. 267 TFEU)

### Mechanism
National courts may (or in some cases must) refer questions of EU law interpretation or
validity to the CJEU for a preliminary ruling.

### Who May Refer?
- **Any court or tribunal** (rechterlijke instantie) of a Member State may refer (Art. 267 lid 2 TFEU)
- In the Netherlands: Rechtbank, Gerechtshof, Hoge Raad, ABRvS, CRvB, CBb, and certain
  specialized tribunals

### Who Must Refer?
- Courts of **last instance** (courts against whose decisions there is no judicial remedy
  under national law) are **obliged** to refer unless:
  - The question has already been answered by the CJEU (**acte eclaire**, CILFIT, C-283/81)
  - The correct application of EU law is so obvious as to leave no scope for reasonable doubt
    (**acte clair**, CILFIT)
- In NL: Hoge Raad, ABRvS, CRvB, CBb are courts of last instance in their respective jurisdictions

### Dutch Practice
- The Hoge Raad regularly refers questions to the CJEU (dozens of references per year)
- The ABRvS refers frequently in immigration and environmental law matters
- Lower courts may also refer, though less commonly
- Average duration of a preliminary ruling procedure: 15-17 months
- Urgent preliminary ruling procedure (PPU - procedure prejudicielle d'urgence) available
  for detention/custody cases: approximately 2-3 months

### Effect of Preliminary Rulings
- Binding on the referring court and all other national courts
- Retroactive effect (ex tunc), unless the CJEU limits temporal effects
- Contributes to uniform interpretation of EU law across all Member States

## Hierarchy Summary

The effective hierarchy of norms in the Netherlands, from highest to lowest:

1. **EU primary law** (Treaties, Charter of Fundamental Rights, General Principles)
2. **EU secondary law with direct effect** (Regulations, Directive provisions after deadline)
3. **International treaties** (ECHR, bilateral treaties) - via Art. 93/94 Gw
4. **Grondwet** (Dutch Constitution) - but courts cannot review Acts of Parliament against
   the Constitution (Art. 120 Gw toetsingsverbod)
5. **Wetten in formele zin** (Acts of Parliament)
6. **Algemene Maatregelen van Bestuur (AMvB)** (Governmental Decrees)
7. **Ministeriele regelingen** (Ministerial Regulations)
8. **Provinciale verordeningen** (Provincial regulations)
9. **Gemeentelijke verordeningen** (Municipal regulations / APV)

**Important nuance regarding Art. 120 Gw:**
Dutch courts may **not** review Acts of Parliament against the Constitution (toetsingsverbod).
However, they **may** review Acts of Parliament against directly effective treaty provisions
(including EU law) via Art. 94 Gw. This means EU law and ECHR law effectively have a higher
status than the Dutch Constitution in judicial practice.

## Practical Guidance for Legal Analysis

### Checklist for Assessing EU-NL Law Interaction
1. Identify the relevant EU instrument and provision
2. Determine if the provision has direct effect (apply Van Gend en Loos criteria)
3. Check if there is conflicting Dutch law
4. If conflict exists: apply primacy (set aside Dutch law)
5. If no direct effect (e.g., directive against private party): apply consistent interpretation
6. If consistent interpretation is not possible: consider state liability (Francovich)
7. If the question is unclear: consider whether a preliminary reference is warranted

### Common Pitfalls
- Assuming directives have horizontal direct effect (they do not)
- Forgetting the duty of consistent interpretation before resorting to state liability
- Overlooking the broad definition of "State" for vertical direct effect purposes
- Not checking whether a CJEU ruling already addresses the question (acte eclaire)
- Confusing direct applicability (regulations) with direct effect (a quality of specific provisions)
